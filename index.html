The caves of Ajanta represent the highest achievement of Indian Buddhist art — a beginning to the end of a great tradition and a glorious chapter in the world history of wall painting. Cut into the rock of a horseshoe-shaped gorge in Maharashtra, these twenty-nine caves contain paintings executed over eight centuries, from the 2nd century BC to the 6th century AD.

Foreword
by S. Swaminathan

An Introduction

Though not an art historian or art critic, I was fascinated by Ajanta from my school days. I entered that world through the then available literature. What was on offer, for the most part, were scholarly works; there was a paucity of information for the lay, interested reader. This is so even today. This and my continued passionate interest in it are the only two reasons for my essay into this enchanting world of sublime beauty.

During the entire period of artist activity in Ajanta, the paintings were executed by Buddhist monks without any royal patronage. The caves, the excavation, sculpturing and painting, were completed in two phases: the first during 2nd-1st centuries BC and the next covering a period of two and a half centuries starting from the 4th century AD. In these caves one can see the progress from the early phase of paintings of the pre-Christian era of caves 9 and 10, reaching classical perfection by the 4th-5th centuries, then the falling off into mannerism and the baroque period and, finally, lapsing into artistic decline before the caves were ultimately abandoned by the 6th century AD.

The paintings and sculptures of Ajanta are a storehouse of information about the period: the costumes, textile design, the jewellery, the musical instruments then in use, the social order, court etiquette, ideas of beauty and morality, categories of taste, customs and mores of the people of the time, and their sense of levity and wit.

The paintings themselves, or what survives of them, tell us about the technical aspects of their art: such as the preparation of the ground, the execution of the painting itself with the sense of perspective, line, space division, colour-overlay, the material used in preparation of the pigmentation and the harnessing of the visual and tactile senses to the pacing of the narrative to be depicted. Mysteries abound: the yoking of the sacred and the profane; the adjacency of the naked and the robed, the division of the art activity between the ceilings and wall murals into geometric design and figurative narration, and so on.

The Ajanta paintings are the earliest surviving paintings of India, religious or secular, barring the primitive rock paintings. This spirit of Ajanta influenced the religious art of the whole of Asia: Sri Lanka, China, Japan and the countries of the Far East and South East Asia.

The Buddha and His disciples were Indians - of the same race as the artist-monks who did not feel the need to make a translation from foreign to familiar terms, as did the medieval artists of Europe and the Buddhist Asian countries. They painted and sculpted a world they were at home in; they painted, in a word, the life around them.

My contact with the glory of Ajanta, though not erudite, original or scholarly, has been long and continuous; what I have managed to put together here is all the information that was not available to me from any single source. This essay merely attempts to share my pleasure with you who, in turn, may find it limited or endless.

S. Swaminathan

Section One

Ajanta – An Introduction

1. Discovery

The treasure of Ajanta came to light thanks to the manoeuvres of a company of British soldiers in the year 1817. Soon pioneer archaeologists were attracted to the caves that were lost to civilisation for more than twelve centuries. James Burgess and Major William Gill made copies of some of the paintings and exhibited the first copies of the Ajanta paintings at the Crystal Palace in London in 1866. Unfortunately almost all of these replicas of the pictures in their original state perished in a disastrous fire. Later far less adequate copies were made by Griffiths and Lady Herringham, to be published in 1896 and 1915. Before Indian independnce, Ajanta was a part of Hyderabad state. Under the patronage of the Nizam, the then ruler of Hyderabad, Yazdani, who was the director of archaeology, edited and published two volumes on the paintings of Ajanta in 1933.

2. Location of Ajanta Caves

The caves of Ajanta are situated on the northern fringe of the Maharashtra plateau, in the district of Aurangabad. Ajanta is about 100 km by road from Aurangabad and about 60 km from Jalgaon; both are connected by rail with Mumbai and Delhi. There are air services from Aurangabad to Mumbai and Delhi. An extended stay at Aurangabad would be rewarding, as the equally important Ellora caves are only about 30 km away. (Refer to Fig. 1 for a map of the region)

The possible explanation for the monastic establishment at Ajanta is its proximity to the ancient trade routes.

When one approaches the site the unexpected sighting of the caves around the horseshoe shaped gorge is breathtaking. The Waghora river springs from its source at one end, to flow in a series of waterfalls dropping as much as thirty meters and passing through seven peculiar bowls of rock called Sat Kund, before entering the gorge.

The caves extend along a concave line running from east to west for about six hundred meters. A narrow pathway will take you to the entrance of Cave 1, to start the pilgrimage to the highest achievement of Indian Buddhist art - a beginning to the end of a great tradition and a glorious chapter in the world history of wall painting.

One can have no doubt about the suitability of the location for uninterrupted meditation and contemplation.

3. Layout of the Caves And Period of Excavation

The caves are aligned in a horseshoe form. There are a total of twenty-nine caves. The caves are numbered not on the basis of chronology, but on the basis of their physical location. The first cave in the outermost extremity serves as an entry point and is numbered one, and then the other caves follow sequentially. Fig. 2 shows the layout of the caves. The general arrangement was not preplanned, as they sprang up sporadically in different periods. (General View of Ajanta Caves: Mjt 020/21). ( A view of the Caves : nou 210)

The caves are of two types: Buddhist temples called chaitya-s and Buddhist monasteries called vihaara-s. There are five chaitya-s and the remaining are vihaara-s. Some of the caves are unfinished.

The caves belong to two distinct phases of Buddhist rock-cut architecture, separated from each other by an interval of about four centuries.

The earlier group of caves is 8, 9, 10, 12, 13 & 15A belonging to the pre-Christian era and belongs to the Hinaayana sect of Buddhism. The earliest is Cave 10, which was excavated, in the 2nd century BC. After a quiescence of about four centuries, excavation was revived on a much more ambitious scale, resulting in the remaining twenty-two caves that now stand as testimony to the Mahaayana sect of Buddhism.

Caves 1, 2, 6, 9, 10, 16 and 17 are important in order to appreciate the Ajanta paintings. Caves 1, 2, 4, 16, 19, 20, 23 and 26 contain some exquisite sculptures.

4. Political Situation and Patronage

The beginning of the Ajanta chapter is a tribute to the religious tolerance of Indian rulers.

The earliest caves in Ajanta (Caves 9 & 10) were excavated during the rule of the Saatavaahana-s, who had their capital at Pratishthana (called, Paithan, to day). During their rule there was brisk trade and commerce within the land and with the Mediterranean world, which brought in enormous riches.

The second phase was of greater artistic activity at Ajanta during the rule of the Vaakaataka and Chalukya dynasties from the 4th to the 7th centuries AD.

The rulers of the Vaakaataka and Chalukya dynasties, like the Saata-vaahana-s, were themselves Hindus but all these kings actively allowed Buddhism to flourish in their territory.

However it must be conceded that there was no direct royal patronage during almost the entire period of Ajanta's artistic activity. But the rich mercantile community, organising itself into guilds, had provided the requisite patronage.

Prof. Walter Spink of the University of Michigan at Ann Arbour, has devoted his whole life to unravelling the secrets of Ajanta. According to him Ajanta represents the apogee of the classical age and gives valuable insights into the material life, aesthetics and spiritual values of the period. Using contemporary detection techniques, he claims that he can date each painting and each sculpture on a year-to-year basis. According to his research, the second - that is the maharani - phase of Ajanta building activities began in 462 AD and was completed in just 18 years!

5. Themes and Composition

The subjects of the pictures are chosen mostly from the jaataka-s, Buddhist mythological stories of the previous lives of the Master.

Episodes from the life of the Buddha form the next important theme in Ajanta. The famous episodes are Thousand Buddhas [Kbl 118], a scene from the Miracle of Shraavasti, of Cave 2, the famous scene painted in Cave 17 depicting the Buddha meeting his wife and son after His Enlightenment titled Mother and Child before the Buddha [Kbl 200] and the Nativity of the Buddha of Cave 2. Some of these, like the first two are mostly self-contained, whereas some like the last one is narrative.

A few of the solo-pictures do not seem to have any religious import like the famous Lady at her Toilet [Kbl 048] of Cave 17.

The paintings in the last category are decorative in character. Here the Ajanta artist has chosen themes from all facets of life; real and imaginary embellished them with pure geometrical patterns in myriad forms. Floral decorations, geometrical designs, birds, animals, mythical nymphs and monsters fill up every square inch of available space - on pillars, triforia, pilasters and the ceilings. It is worth mentioning that the ceilings contain paintings only of secular themes.

6. Composition - Earlier Phase (2nd-1st centuries BC)

In the early phase of Indian wall decoration, that is, during the 2nd-1st centuries BC, the narrative paintings are arranged in the form of a long canvas, with the action progressing from episode to episode. This can be seen in the surviving paintings of this period in Caves 9 and 10. The scenes unfold on a ribbon-like space on the walls, but are not subdivided as in the later period except for an occasional tree, or a building or a group of rocks, which vaguely defines the end of one scene and the beginning of another.

An interesting feature of the Ajanta narration, both during this period as well as the later period, is that a strict chronology of events was not followed.

The giant canvas-like mural depicting Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 10 (Line ?) is an example these two features.

7. Composition - Later Phase (4th century AD onwards)

In later centuries the paintings overspread the entire surface of the wall. In these paintings narratives proceed from scene to scene and from act to act harmoniously. The scenes are not separated into frames that might disturb the concentration of the viewing devotees.

The Ajanta artists used specific conventions for separating scenes and acts from each other, using suggestive punctuation marks. Thus in a palace scene pillars may separate the scenes and gateways, rocks, etc. may mark the end of one act and the beginning of the next one. A detailed study of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1 would explain this.

INDIAN RELIGIOUS ART, BUDDHIST, JAINA OR HINDU, ARE THE ONLY TRULY INDIGENOUS ART

Rothenstein.... has justly made the point that in China and Japan, Buddhism was a foreign religion, and Buddhist art came to these countries with a heretic tradition already fixed and formulated. (But) to these Ajanta artists the Buddha and his disciples were Indians, men of their own race; they did not need to make translation from foreign to familiar terms, as did the medieval European painters in depicting the Gospel stories, in order to make the scenes intelligible ..... If you come to think of it, nearly all the religious art .... deals with far-off events, circumstanced in surrounding neither known nor understood except through books.

-Lawrence Binyon in the Introduction to the Mukul Dey’s My Pilgrimage to Ajanta and Bagh, 1973.

8. Painting Technique

The technique of Indian wall painting, which was followed with little change in all later examples of mural decoration in Asia, is completely different from that of the Western fresco. Fresco, more correctly fresco buono, in its true sense implies the application of colours to a layer of moist lime plaster. Indian and all Asian murals were painted on an almost dry wall, called fresco secco.

It might have taken centuries for the Indian artist to develop the technique of preparing the wall for painting, and also to select suitable pigments with an appropriate binder, though no trace of such experimentation has been found. The importance of these may be seen from the fact that the Ajanta paintings have withstood the ravages of time with remarkable resilience.

However, from the outlines of some incomplete paintings in certain caves we get to know the painting sequence followed by the Ajanta artist.

VAJRALEPA - PREPARATION OF THE WALL

"A buffalo-skin was boiled in water until it became soft. Sticks were then made of the paste and dried in the sunshine. It is stated that if colour is mixed with this hard plaster, called vajralepa, it makes it fast, and if white mud is mixed with it, it serves as perfect medium for coating walls. Vajralepa coating was usually done in three layers over a plaster which consisted of powdered brick, burnt conches and sand, mixed with a liquid preparation of molasses and drops of a decoction of mudga (Phaseolus munga). To this a quantity of mashed ripe bananas or tree resins and the pulp of bilva fruit (Aegle marmelos) was also added. After the mixture had dried it was again ground down and mixed with molasses and water until it became soft. The wall surface was washed down with water until it was thoroughly clean and then the plaster was applied with a spoon. This was the two-fold process by which the wall was prepared for painting.”

9. Rock-cut Architecture of Ajanta

The caves of Ajanta offer an instructive field for the study of the evolution of rock-cut architecture or cave architecture. Their structural qualities, like their sculpture, seldom receive due attention, which is focused more on the world-famous paintings that they bear.

It was in northern India that the great Buddhist emperor Ashoka initiated the cave architecture. But, it was in the middle part of India, called the Deccan, that it reached a level of sophistication unattained in the north.

Cave architecture is unique in the sense that it can be viewed as an enterprise of a sculptor. The Ajanta architect-sculptor showed his capability not only in carving a human or animal figure but something far grander and more monumental.

The cave architecture, at Ajanta and elsewhere, betrays the strong influence of wooden construction so as to create an illusion of building in wood. This, and other aspects, lead us to believe that the architect-sculptors were probably drawn from the profession of carpenters, with goldsmiths and ivory-carvers joining hands with the sculptors.

The evolution of rock-architecture, both chaitya-s and vihaara-s, follows the same pattern in this region and can be made to fall into two periods: the Hinaayana period of the pre-Christian era and the later Mahaayana period.

During the first phase, that is, the Hinaayana period of 2nd century BC to 2nd Century AD the sculptural activity was limited. Sculptures of this period can also be found in Pithalkhora caves in Aurangabad district and in the more famous Sanchi stoopa-s situated near Bhopal, capital of Madhya Pradesh. A continuity of this tradition can be seen in the caves of Bhaja (Pune district) and those at Kanheri, very close to Borivli, a suburb of Mumbai, and later at Amaravathi, near Vijayawada and Nagarjunakonda, on the banks of the river Krishna in Andhra Pradesh.

The second phase began in the late 5th century AD. Once a beginning had been made, side by side with the excavation of new caves the existing Hinaayana ones were suitably modified. The ornamental facade of Cave 10 belongs to this period. A View of the Caves [Nou 210], The Facade of Cave 19 [Nou 211] and the Chaitya Interior of Cave 19 [Nou 025], Interior View of Cave 1 [Mjt 007] and A View of the Main Hall of Cave 17 [Kbl 157] are examples of just such cave architecture.

10. Sculpture of Ajanta

During the first phase, that is, the Hinaayana phase, the Buddha was not shown in the human form, but only in symbols, such as the Wheel, the Bodhi Tree and the Footprints of the Buddha. But during the Mahaayana period sculptures and paintings of the Buddha and the Bodhi-sattva-s were added.

The figure-sculpture of Ajanta belongs to the great art-tradition of contemporary India. Sculpture, mostly dating from the 5th and 6th centuries AD, is remarkable for its classicism marked by qualities like grace, elegance, restraint and serenity. The Naaga Couple [nou 212] in Cave 19 and the vidyadhara-s (flying couple) in Cave 16 illustrate this. The giant reclining figure depicting the Final Extinction of the Master called Mahaa-pari-nirvaana [nou 214] in Cave 26 is a masterpiece.

The spiritual luminosity of the contemporary images of the Buddha is, however, wanting as the general character of the sculpture tends towards a certain heaviness of form.

It may be mentioned here that every one of the sculpture was plastered and painted, but most of this protective layer is now lost.

Cave Temples

It was Ashoka who initiated cutting living rocks into architectural forms as places of Worship. These called cave temples, built by Ashoka and the later kings can be found in Bihar (at Sitamarhi near Gaya), in the Deccan (Bhaja, early caves of Ajanta, Karle etc.), in Andhra (Guntapalli), and in Orissa (Udayagiri and Khandagiri). Later Buddhists caves belonging to the Mahaayana sect can be found in Madhya Pradesh (Bagh),the later Ajanta caves, Ellora, etc.

It was by about 5th century AD, that Hindus and Jains adopted the rock-out mode of architecture for their temples. Chandragupta II built the earliest at Udayagiri (Vidisha Dt.). In the South, a very large number of rock-cut temples were constructed by the Chalukyas of Vatapi (6-7th century), the Rushtrakutas (7-9th century), the Pallavas of Kanchi (6-9th century) and the Pandyas of Madurai (8th - 9th century)

11. Ajanta Painting Art Tradition

The Ajanta caves represent only the culmination, not the renaissance, of a very ancient Indian tradition of painting. Even the very earliest paintings at Ajanta, from the second century BC, demonstrate a sophisticated technique in every way comparable to the achievement of sculpture of the early classical period (4th-5th centuries AD).

For example, the Kaama-sootra of Vaatsyayana, the well-known treatise on eroticism, enumerates ‘Six Limbs of Painting’, whereas Samaraangana-sootra-dhaara, a 11th century treatise on architecture prescribes ‘Eight Limbs of Painting’. Brihat Samhita (6th century AD), and Vishnu-dharmottara (7th century AD) also set forth the rules and canons of painting. It is in these that we find classifications of the types of painting suitable for temples, palaces, and private dwellings and a distinction made between ‘true, lyrical and secular’ paintings.

12. Producing Depth and Relief

From very early times, Indian artists have been using a variety of techniques to produce the illusion of the third dimension. Perspective was one of them. Relief and plasticity is also realised suggesting a third dimension in terms of the distances through a technique called kshaya-vriddhi (loss-and-gain) meaning fore-shortening of the limbs.

More elaborate was the use of shading techniques, of which there were two main variations, called animnonnata (flat) style and nimnonnata (high-relief) style. The later, also called vartana (shading), had at least three variations: patraja (shading-like-the-lines-of-leaf), binduja (dot-and-stipple) and airika (softening-hard-lines). A totally different approach was ujjotana (adding-highlights).

All these are represented in the Ajanta paintings, and conclusively prove the high level of sophistication reached by the Ajanta artist.

13.Producing Depth and Relief - Perspective

The paintings of Ajanta conclusively prove that the Ajanta painter was proficient in perspective drawing. Further, the surviving paintings in Ajanta make it amply clear that the Ajanta artists were not only proficient in conventional perspective, but also employed such advanced techniques like reverse perspective and even multiple vision that the present-day movie-makers use. The choice of the technique depended on the theme and the space available.

A number of paintings at Ajanta can be sited as examples in the use of these techniques. The Gate of the Palace of Brahma-datta [mjt 097] of Cave 2 and the Monastery [kbl 227R] of Cave 17 are examples of normal perspective. In the scene Dancing Girl with Musicians [kbl 091], a scene from Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1 and in the Game of Dice [kbl 132], a scene from Vidhura-pandita Jaataka in Cave 2 we come across the use of reverse perspective. And, finally, the scenes from Lustration and Renunciation from Cave 1 are done using the technique of multiple vision.

Relief and plasticity is also realised suggesting a third dimension in terms of the distances through a technique called kshaya-vriddhi (loss-and-gain) meaning fore-shortening of the limbs; charging Bull [asi 030] and Elephant [asi 028] painted on the ceiling of Cave 1 are examples of this technique.

14. Producing Depth and Relief - Shading Techniques

The use of shading to produce effects of relief was apparently part of the Indian practice from very early times.

Certain passages in the Lankaavatara-sootra imply that an effect of illusion of reality was to be achieved through the use of relief modelling. "As a picture shows highness and lowness, whereas really there is nothing of the sort in it ... it is like the painter's canvas on which there is no depression or elevation as imagined by the ignorant."

Ajanta artists have employed basically two different methods to bring out depth and relief. One is a conventional flattened perspective called animnonnata (flat style), that is using dark colours for the subjects in the foreground against a background of lighter shades, or vice versa. The episodes from Shibi Jaataka painted in Cave 1, like the three wailing women in the scene, King weighs Himself [Kbl 074] and Prince and his Consort [Kbl 075] are examples of this style.

The more elaborate method is called nimnonnata (high-relief-style). In this, relief and depth were expressed by a judicious choice of tones and colours, known as vartana (shading) and ujjotana (adding-highlights).

The three kinds of vartana (shading) techniques are mentioned in the Vishnu-dharmottara. They are patraja (shading-like-the-lines-of-a-leaf), binduja (dot-and-stipple-method), and airika (softening-hard-lines).

Some of the animals, like the Elephant [ASI 028] and the charging Bull, [ASI 030] painted on the ceiling of Cave 1, are examples for the patraja method of shading. The contours on the bare body of the cherubs flying effortlessly on the four corners of the Squares and Circles - 3 [ASI 048] in Cave 2 has been brought out by the binduja method. The airika technique can be seen in a host of paintings in Ajanta, like the scenes from Chempayya Jaataka of Cave 1 and the Descent of Indra [kbl 164] in Cave 17.

On the other hand, ujjotana refers to the method of specifically adding lighter and brighter pigments to produce an optical effect. The ujjotana effect can be seen in the various scenes of Vidhurapandita Jaataka of Cave 2.

Between the nimnonnata and animnonnata styles the artists apparently enjoyed wide latitude in employing the methods best suited to their individual talents and their subject matter.

The chaaya-tapa technique creates a kind of soft chiaroscuro effect, which makes the sacred countenances of the Buddha and Bodhi-sattva-s radiate with a mysterious glow. The famous painting of Bodhi-sattva Padmapani [Kbl 069] in cave 1 is an example of the use of this technique.

It was with the discovery of the use of lapis lazuli, a blue coloured mineral, during 4th-5th centuries that the Ajanta artist added another dimension to his paintings. Its cool transparent blue was an effective medium for creating visual depth, by contrasting it with the warmth of the red and brown tones. The neutral tints of the chocolate-brown background of the earlier Hinaayana paintings, like the Raja with his Retinue of Cave 10 [Mjt 106], were now frequently replaced by various shades of blue.

15. Painting and Dance - A Unique Relation

The relationship between painting and dance, and between dance and sculpture is a remarkable Indian tradition not found elsewhere in the history of world art.

Vishnu-dharmottara (11th century, AD) stresses the impossibility of attaining a proper expression of feeling in painting without a knowledge of the art of dance. In the old days mastery in the art of dancing was invariably considered essential for personal perfection.

There are paintings from the earliest as well as the latest periods of Ajanta art which depict dance scenes. The dancers form a part of the entourage in the pre-Christian painting, Raja with the Retinue of Cave 10 [Mjt 106]. The dancer in the Naaga Court [Mjt 046] of Cave 1 and the dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091] a scene from Mahaa-janaka Jaataka, painted on the walls of Cave 1 are exquisite dancescenes. The vibrant grace of pose and gesture of these dancers invests them with a swaying, flower-like rhythm and movement.

A very important feature in the depiction of the human form, particularly of women is the emphasis on his/her gait, and the whole figure structured round three main axes in order to conform to this tribhanga pose. It gives the body an S-shaped rhythm, a fluency of line, which, together with the appropriate gestures of the hands, conveys a wide range of expressions. It is practised by dancers even today, particularly in odissi, a classical dance form from the Eastern part of India. Painting of Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069] and the dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091], both from Cave 1 are two such examples.

This kinship between painting and dance as also between painting and sculpture that we find in India is something altogether unique in the history of worldart.

16. Rendering Parts of the Body - Symbolism based on Animals and Plants

It was mentioned before that the traditional rendering of the parts of the body should resemble, and be based on, similes drawn from plant or animal-life. For example, while rendering the eyes, depending on the emotions to be conveyed, the model became a stylised design of a flower, fish or bird; but an added refinement of lines and the finish of colours made them intrinsically human and intensely expressive and distinguished. Such symbolism greatly enriched all art forms, both religious and secular.

Sensuous lips are ripe and full like the bimba-fruit (Momorda monadelpha), with the lower lip protruding, her modulus hands expose fingers likened to lotus-petals. Fingers nimble and sensitive, as if indicative of her thoughts, match perfectly with the rest of the movements and blend in the direction and glance of her eyes.

Accordingly, the eyes of the Great Being, like Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069] of Cave 1, are shaped like lotus petals, his arching brows like the curve of the Indian bow, and his face the perfect ovoid (shape of the egg). The shoulders of the divinity are likened to the massive domed head of the elephant, and his arms to its tapered trunk.

A further metaphor describes the torso as being like the body of a lion (simha-kati). King Sanjaya depicted in the scene Restoration of Children (ASI 071), of Vishvaantara Jaataka, is 'lion-waisted'.

In agreement with an old belief, the thigh and leg-line should be analogous to the trunk of a banana-tree drawn in tapering lines, the feet to the leaves (pada-pallava), neither of them revealing the bone-structure. We witness the perfect feminine form supported by tapering legs, at times so thin as to raise a doubt whether they could sustain the body-weight in many scenes in Ajanta. Some such scenes are the Queen in a Palace Scene [Nou 073] of Cave 1 and Maaya Devi in the scene Queen Mahaa-maaya Musing [Kbl 122b] in the narration of the Nativity of the Buddha in Cave 2. The feet of the Lady at her Toilet [Kbl 048] of Cave 17 truly resemble leaves. Feet like lotus flowers (charana-kamala) and gomukha khanda (torso-resembling-cow's-head) are two other metaphors met with frequently in both art and literature.

The emphasis, perhaps, is mainly on the neck, the bust, the waist and the hips. Particularly the pitcher-like breasts, so full of warmth and shape, stand in an incredible contrast to her slim waist, as in Maid tries to Revive the Princess [ASI 007] in Cave 1. This way of representation ignores the anatomy of muscles and has been termed `artistic anatomy' - a phrase coined by Abanindranath Tagore.

17. Body Postures (sthaana-s)

During the Vaakaataka period, a whole range of sthaana-s (body-postures) was systematised from the rijvagata (frontal) to parshvagata (strict profile). Most of the poses are found in Ajanta, for example, dancer Girl in the Naaga Court [Mjt 046] painted in Cave 1 is the paravritta pose.

From the beginning of the Mahaayana phase one cannot find a single scene in which the poses of women are monotonous. This is particularly so when women are shown in congregation, like the women of the Mahaa-janaka court painted in the various scenes of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1. The natural, but rather unusual rear view of Woman listens to Sermon [ASI 009], a scene from Shankha-paala Jaataka of Cave 1 is an interesting study. Votaries with Offerings [Kbl 126] have been portrayed with their swaying movement approaching the sanctum in Cave 2. Most of the scenes from the magnificent panel depicting Simhala Avadaana in Cave 17 have their characters in a variety of poses. And, the melancholy scene from Vishvaantara Jaataka of Vishvaantara consoles his Wife [Kbl 161] painted in Cave 17 is another example of the consummate skill of the Ajanta artist in this aspect.

One can notice restricted body positions, tending to monotony, in the pre-Christian Hinaayana paintings, like Raja with his Retinue [Mjt 106] in Cave 10.

18. Religious Motifs

That the theme of the paintings on the wall of the caves was religious and that the theme on the ceiling was secular was mentioned before. We also saw that the Buddha was not depicted in human form in the earlier Hinaayana period.

The later phase of the Ajanta wall paintings resembles the art of the Gupta period; the Vakataka-s were matrimonially related to the Gupta-s. By this time the early philosophical and moral concepts of the Hinaayana (Small Vehicle) were transformed into the completely theistic religion of the Mahaayana (Great Vehicle).

In early Buddhism the doctrine, rather than the Buddha, was worshipped and the possibility of salvation from the retribution of karma (rebirth) was open only to those who could enter the religious order. The Buddha himself was remembered only in symbolical references - the Bodhi-tree and the Feet of the Master - to his earthly mission. Owing to many reasons, in the later period the image of the Buddha came to be worshipped.

In the early Mahaayana-sutras, such as the Saddharma-pundarika of the 2nd century AD, we encounter the conception of the Buddha as a transcendental and divine personage and the introduction of the cult of the Bodhi-sattva-s.

The Bodhi-sattva-s who figure so prominently in the wall-paintings of Ajanta are mythical beings or archangels who pass from the realm of the immortal Buddha to the world of men. The term Bodhi-sattva, by definition, means ‘one capable of supreme knowledge or Buddhahood’.

The Bodhi-sattva-s of the Mahaayana pantheon are personifications of the virtues and power of the Buddha and are beings who have renounced the possibility of Buddhahood in order to devote themselves eternally to the alleviation of the pain of human existence.

These saving divinities invited the prayers of men like the gods and goddesses of the now emerging Brahminism.

19. Portrayal of Women

Without exaggeration it can be said that Ajanta's creative genius lies in the portrayal of women. The lovely ladies of the courts with their handmaids, the dancers and the musicians, the devotees, the common women and even the beggar girls are all drawn with brilliant zest and extraordinary knowledge. In fact, one wonders at the propriety of painting such sensuous and voluptuous women in religious places inhabited mainly by celibate monks who have renounced all worldly pleasures.

Whether they are painted in repose, talking to their lovers, instructing their handmaids, admiring themselves in mirrors, carrying offerings or simply standing, sitting and gossiping, the Ajanta women are always painted with a sort of wonder akin to awe.

Undoubtedly the Ajanta artist had succeeded in reproducing the soft roundness of her breasts, the curves of her hips, the turn of her head, the contortions of her body, the gestures of her hands, or the slanting glance of her eyes. Every one of the Ajanta women is invariably refreshing. An interesting feature of Ajanta women is that they never lose their dignity and nowhere are they besmirched or belittled.

Removed in time, we are not able to understand the Ajanta artist fully. This is perhaps because we apply today's scale of morality to judge the past. We have wondered why very sensuous women were painted at all in these religious caves. To add to our bewilderment, we find that the normal practice in Ajanta depict the heroines in the scenes naked, or in near nudity, while all the maids in the same scenes are fully clothed. Queen captivats Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L], a scene from Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1 and dying Princess [ASI 003], a scene from the episode of the Conversion of Nanda of cave 16 can be cited as two such examples. Further, we may have no explanation for the fact that most of the heroines of Ajanta are dark complexioned. Apart from the black Apsaras [Kbl 169] of Cave 17 and the dying Princess [ASI 003] of Cave 16, there are other dark-complexioned beauties in Ajanta.

Just as the Buddhas and Bodhi-sattva-s are the central themes in painting the sacred images, in the orbit of secular subjects the women are undoubtedly the focal point.

20. Painting on the Ceilings

For reasons unknown, the Ajanta artist never painted religious themes on the ceilings, where the colour scheme, delineation and repetitious themes were rendered with equal craftsmanship. Spontaneity of movement is evident in all these.

From the artistic point of view, the paintings on the ceilings show power of imagination, perhaps even excelling the wall-murals. In these paintings, the Ajanta artist might have attempted to offset the seriousness of the themes on the walls.

These drawings have taken the texture of a carpet, brilliantly woven, immediately captivating the eyes and filling the senses. They do not throw a challenge to the intellect but cover the void above the walls with idyllic coloured designs.

The ceilings are filled with geometric designs, flower decorations, animals and birds and humorous scenes.

21. Painting on the Ceilings - Geometric Designs

One of the themes is the huge concentric circle enclosed in a square, with a number of flowery bands within it; they are usually to be found in the centre of the ceiling, main hall, antechamber and inner shrines. The ceiling of Cave 2 is the best such example, of which Squares and Circles - 3 [ASI 048] is a good example.

Another equally cherished theme consists of a number of rectangular panels filled with decorative motifs framed by smaller squares or rectangles with representations of fruit and floral forms. Some of these motifs are repeated a number of times so that the effect is that of an enormous printed textile spread over the sanctuary. This effect may be seen in Cave 17 and a part of this is shown in Squares and Rectangles - 2 [ASI 083].

These decorative panels present as typical a collection of Indian patterns of ornamental design as one could find. The floral designs that fill these squares are perfect examples of the Indian ability to reduce natural forms to a moving decorative pattern.

These pliant vine patterns, filled with rhythmic adaptations of flower and bird and human forms, are the painted equivalents of those wonderful fantasies in relief sculpture that are to be seen both in the ornamentation of the Ajanta caves and in such a Gupta monument as the Dhamekh stoopa at Sarnath. Some of the paintings on the ceiling of Caves 1, 2 and 17 illustrate this.

22. Painting on the Ceilings - Animal Motifs

The artist has been able to draw animals spaced between flowers and plants with extraordinary dexterity. One of the most forceful and vigorous drawings of Ajanta depicts the fighting Bulls [Mjt 013] painted on a pillar of Cave 1. The complex of decoration on the ceiling of Cave 1 includes a wonderful panel of an Elephant [ASI 028] enclosed in an entanglement of lotus flowers. Although the elephant's shape has been distorted to fit the confines of the frame, the artist of this wonderfully alive and cursive drawing still suggests the massive anatomy and ponderous strength of the elephant.

23. Painting on the Ceilings - Scenes in a lighter Vein

As in other instances, a scene depicted on the ceiling of Cave 1 in which a Clown [ASI 008] is cradled amongst flowers, as if seated on a swing, shows the expertness of the artist in mingling a portrayal of what is seemingly naturalistic together with totally imaginary elements.

Panels as on the ceiling of Caves 1 and 17, with their grotesque and weird creatures, exhibit a remarkable charm.

Most intriguing and least expected in a religious shrine are the scenes depicting a royal harem like the panel Royal Harem [Kbl 108] and people enjoying each other's company with wine as depicted in Foreigners Drinking [ASI 043]!

Many of the motifs and ornaments that we find on the ceilings of Ajanta are still alive. They have been absorbed into the different branches of Indian artistic expression and are esteemed even today: in the alpana-s, jewellery, brocades and borders of conventional sari-s, dresses, etc.

24. Phases of Ajanta Art

Ajanta paintings were done over a period of eight centuries, from the 2nd century BC to the 6th century AD.

During this period there have been many changes - changes in taste, manner, and style. The history of these changes in style and manner is, in fact, the history of art.

Indeed it is interesting asking what a particular painting represents. For instance, in sculpture there have been images of the Buddha or the goddess Parvati depicted century after century; and once you know that an image represents the Buddha or the goddess Parvati, you have learned or understood very little. They are made so vastly different in every century, that you will be missing all the beauty and fascination if you are only interested in the theme, in the subject matter of a work of art.

It is fascinating to follow the change in taste and, thus, the development of style. For each generation expresses in works of art its own concept of beauty, and rejects the ideals of the past.

Sometimes simplicity is emphasised; at other times profuse and lavish ornamentation is in fashion. During one period painters prefer studied dignity and what they call ‘good taste’; during others they like rich decoration so much that every inch of a given wall is filled with endless and delicate ornamentation. This is style - style that is different in every age and always changes.

By sheer chance, the development of style from the beginning to its final decline can be witnessed within the physical limits of Ajanta. In this respect Ajanta has no parallel.

25. Phases of Ajanta Art - Pre-Classical Period (2nd-1st Centuries BC)

In the development of any art form, the initial stage is generally called archaic or in simple terms, primitive. This phase is characterised by rigid and lifeless portrayals. The artists are yet to understand true representation of movement.

It seems that the earliest existing examples of Ajanta painting, of the 2nd-1st century BC in the Caves 9 and 10 cannot be classified as archaic in the strictest sense of the word. They have already entered the transitional period that was to carry them on to the classical phase. This is evident from the subtle, beautifully drawn sketches that effectively portray the poses and gestures of the human figure and the grandeur of trees and flowers.

An expression of this period can be seen in Caves 9 and 10. The narration of Shyaama Jaataka in Cave 9, Raja with his Retinue [Mjt 106] in Cave 10 and what remains of the narration of Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 10 (Line ?) are the few survivors of time, neglect and vandalism.

26. Phases of Ajanta Art - Classical Period (4th-5th Centuries AD)

By the 4th and 5th centuries, AD, art in India reached a stage that must be called classical. The classic style means not only perfect mastery of the subject, but turning all this skill and knowledge to the noble purpose of creating beauty: everything is idealised, realism is only known and used in order to create things of beauty and perfection. There is a dignity and nobility in classical art that allows no exaggeration, no excess, no overstatement, no dramatisation.

Simhala Avadana in Cave 17, dying Princess [ASI 003] of Cave 16, Descent of Indra [Kbl 164] of Cave 17 and Votaries with Offerings [Kbl 126], certainly represent the classical peak of Ajanta painting, breathing peace, harmony, and balance. Agitation, overcrowding, too much movement and passion are all aliens to the classical art of this period.

The word `classic’ is advisedly used with reference to the Ajanta murals because they occupy the same position in relation to all later developments in the Buddhist art of Asia, as do the marbles of the Parthenon to the history of Western art. The styles of Buddhist painting in Tibet and Nepal, Central Asia, China, and ultimately Japan all have their beginnings in the classical mode represented by this series of murals.

27. Phases of Ajanta Art - Period of Mannerism (End of 5th Century, AD)

By the end of the 5th century, there are signs that the Indian artist has made a change of style. These signs are at first minute departures from the purely classical sentiment, and a very hesitant deviation from the classical concepts of beauty and idealism. This is the period of mannerism.

During this initial period of mannerism, Ajanta produced some of the finest examples of the sacred image; Buddha preaches to the Congregation [Nou 197] of Cave 17, and the incomparable Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069] and Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara [Kbl 066] both painted in Cave 1. The narration of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka in Cave 2 belongs to the period of transition from classicism to mannerism, for in this composition we find elements of both.

During this period one can notice that the differences have been narrowed in the portrayal of the sacred and the secular as compared to the earlier classical phase.

28. Phases of Ajanta Art - Baroque Period (Mid 6th Century AD)

By the middle of the 6th century, the style turned baroque, a style of over-ornamentation and exaggeration.

The broad vision is progressively dissected into compartments so that the narrative is painted, not in an idyllic landscape, but in a maze of pillars in royal pavilions, palace windows, porches, balconies, city walls, shops and monasteries.

The eye-slits are stretched out of all proportion to the face, men look effeminate and women exaggeratedly feminine, with bulging curves and rounded breasts. Both men and women wear excessive ornament.

The affected, grossly exaggerated attitudes of the characters who partake in the Mahaa-janaka Jaataka [Cave 1], for example, one of its scene Queen captivates Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L], and in certain scenes of Vidhurapandita Jaataka [Cave 2] are examples of this style.

29. Phases of Ajanta Art - Period of Decline (End 6th Century, AD)

Artistic standards were in the decline from the end of the 6th century; mercifully this phase did not last long, for the Ajanta caves were abandoned forever, for reasons unknown.

Take the case of Queen in a Palace Scene [Nou 073] in Cave 1. The classical poses and decorative elements are exaggerated with heavy heads, elongated eyes, excessively large mouths, thin legs, and superfluous hand gestures. In addition the composition is too crowded. The entire execution becomes careless, perhaps because of mass production, and the finish of the paintings was summary.

The painting presenting Two Figures of the Buddha [ASI 085] in teaching attitude in Cave 17 with disciples kneeling with folded hands is a specimen of this period of decadence. These are only two of many similar Buddhas that we find enclosed in separate cubicles, horizontally covering the whole of the upper panels of Cave 10. Compared with the other Buddhas on the walls of Ajanta painted earlier, they lack refinement and finish. The colours are naive, flat and unnuanced and the lines wanting in feeling.

Thousand Buddhas [Kbl 118] a scene from the Miracle of Shraavasti is obviously a product of this period.

Probably belonging to the same period, the painting of Some Foreigners [Kbl 109] from Cave 1 is a glaring example of the change of style and execution. Both the drawing and finish are degenerate and when compared with the other paintings of the same cave it appears clumsy and bare. Viewed critically, we notice that the refinements that are present in the others are non-existent here; gone are the shapely palms and sensitive fingers. The entire portrayal is rather stiff and simplified, having nothing in common with the linear expression of soft curves typical of Ajanta.

30. Ajanta - Inspiration at Home and Abroad

Just as the sculpture of the Gupta period provided a standard for the entire Asian world, so the paintings of Ajanta, in style, in type and in technique, exerted their influence on Buddhist art for centuries to follow. The paintings in the Bagh caves, about 400 km from Ajanta in the State of Madhya Pradesh, the paintings at Ellora, the 8th century Jain paintings of the early Pandya-s at Sittanavasal near Pudukkottai in Tamilnadu, Hindu paintings of the Chalukya-s at Badami in Karnataka are perpetuations of the refinement of the great murals of the Ajanta caves. The famous paintings of Sigiriya in Sri Lanka, contemporary with the great period of activity at Ajanta, may be regarded as rather bold provincial reflections of the Ajanta manner. This is true of the 12th century decorations of the northern temple at Polonnaruwa, in Sri Lanka, represent a conventionalised derivation from the narrative panels in Caves 1 and 17.

The spread of Buddhism northward and eastward over Turkestan to China brought with it a diffusion of the great styles of Indian sculpture and painting. The vast painted complex that once decorated the niche of the 175-foot Buddha at Bamiyan in Afganisthan represents the Ajanta style modified by a somewhat more linear and decorative Iranian idiom. The 7th century sanctuary of Fondukistan, also in Afganisthan, was decorated with paintings of Bodhi-sattva-s and Taras, which in their sensuous elegance are directly derived from the Ajanta style.

In all of these Afghan examples, the Indian style of drawing has become somewhat hard and wiry, and the soft shading of Ajanta has been conventionalised into a hard reinforcement of the contours in bands of darker pigments.

The eastward diffusion of the Ajanta style at sites like Kyzil and Turfan is marked by a gradual reduction of the Indian mode to a hard and wiry linear manner. Properly speaking the Central Asian style of these sites is probably to be explained as a fusion of Indian and Iranian elements. A shadow of the classic Indian tradition persists in the 6th and 7th century caves as the famous site Tun Huang in westernmost China, where in many instances Indian types and iconography are combined with the indigenous Chinese technique of definition in essentially linear terms.

The establishment of close political and religious ties between the T'ang Empire and India in the 7th and 8th centuries led to a much closer imitation of Indian models in the Far East. The annals of Chinese painting are filled with mentions of Chinese artists who were reputed to have introduced a style of painting in the Indian manner, which gave an illusion of relief through shading. A few fragments of painting from this period survive in Japan to indicate the spread of the Indian style to the Far East. For example, the paintings at the base of the Tachibana shrine at Horyu-ji in Nara are done in an Indo-Central Asian manner, seemingly transported to Japan untouched by Chinese influence. The figures of new-born souls seated on lotus flowers, both in pose and the use of heavy shading, are so like some of the figures in the Ajanta wall-paintings that they might almost have been painted by the same hand.

Similar parallels between Ajanta and the Japanese art of the 8th century are to be found in the famous wallpaintings in the Horyu-ji Kondo which were destroyed in 1948. These scattered fragments are all that survive to illustrate what must have been a near universal influence of Indian ideals in the Far East in the 7th and 8th centuries. The persistence of these canons in even later periods, however modified by native traditions, serve to indicate how the classical style of the Ajanta wall paintings moulded the destiny of all later styles of religious art in Asia. This is similar to how the classical styles of Greece and Rome affected the entire later development of art in the Western world.

31. The End of the Ajanta Epoch

The creative period of Ajanta ended as mysteriously as it had begun. Some of the unfinished caves, which were quite obviously abandoned unexpectedly, show that the emigration took place over a comparatively short span of time. Even if some of the caves continued to be lived in for a few hundred years after the 6th century, the merchant wealth that financed the decoration and ornamentation of the shrines had apparently been exhausted. In any event, the administrators of the monastic establishment could not have foreseen the impending misfortunes and privations, or they would not have planned new vihaara-s in the hard rock, only to leave them incomplete.

A parallel to such an unceremonious end can be seen in the abrupt cessation of sculptural activities in Mahabalipuram almost at the same time as that of Ajanta, leaving behind almost all the sculptural masterpieces incomplete.

Cave One
Vihaara · End of 5th century AD

Cave One

One of the finest vihaara-s (monasteries) among the cave temples of India, this cave has been dated to the end of the 5th century AD on the evidence of its architecture and sculptures.

The cave has the usual plan, with a verandah, a great congregation hall and a shrine. The verandah, with a cell at each end, is about 20 meters long, about 3 meters wide and about 4 meters high. A central door with carved jambs and entablature leads into the main hall. The ceiling is supported by twenty exquisitely carved and beautifully painted pillars, which are surrounded by an aisle. Facing the entrance, in the rear of the hall, there is an antechamber, leading into the shrine, which is 6 meters square. The vihaara has fourteen cells for the monks to stay.

The front porch has crumbled, but it still contains several bands of fine carvings. Among these are scenes from the life of the Buddha, elephant fights and hunting expeditions. The columns of the verandah and hall are also profusely carved. The shafts have vertical and spiral fluting encircled by bands of lovely tracery. The bases of the capitals are generally ornamented with mythical animals, religious stories and a great variety of floral designs.

Sculptures: The following are some of the important sculptures in this cave:

The Buddha in the Sanctum: The colossal figure of the Buddha in the garbha-graha (sanctum) is a fine specimen of Ajanta sculpture. The Buddha is seated in the padmasana (lotus-posture) in dharma chakra pravartana mudra (setting-wheel-of-Dharma-in-motion-attitude) is scooped in relief.

On either side is a standing chamara (fly-whisk) bearer. Both the figures are profusely ornamented, while their luxuriantly coiled tresses fall gracefully on one side. The vidyaadhara-s (flying-celestials) with garlands in hand on either side of the halo amidst clouds represented by ringlets are also well conceived figures.

Below, on a double-lotus pedestal is carved the Wheel of Law in profile. This is flanked on either side by a deer to suggest the Mrigadava (present day Sarnath) sermon. In the figures of the five monks flanking the left deer can be recognised pancha vareeya bhikshu-s (first-five-disciples).

The halo and chhatra (umbrella) over the head are painted. The door jambs of the shrine are elaborately carved with scroll-work, floral motifs, naaga-s (people belonging to the serpent world), mithuna-s (couples), female figures standing on makara-s (crocodile motifs), etc. The pillars of the ante-chamber have beautiful bracket-figures of women with attendants.

The composition is extremely well integrated and the attention of the onlooker is focused on the main object of worship. The sculpture, however, tends to be rather heavy and lacks the grace of that on the facade of Cave 19, and the grace of the sculpture of the Gupta period.

The Great Encounters: Three out of the four encounters, usually called the Ominous Sights, in the life of Gautama which prompted him to forsake the life of pleasure are carved over the cell to the left of the entrance. The panels to the right depict the young prince Gautama on a horse-driven chariot going for a ride, where scenes of death (extreme left), old age (left centre) and disease (right) confront him. At the centre he is seen listening to the music of a lady holding a veena on her lap. Within the three chaitya-arches below, musicians, playing the vina are shown in different poses.

Four Deer with a single Head: The famous ‘four deer with a single head’ can be found on the central column of the right row of the hall. The rendering of the four deer with one head is so cleverly done that it looks consistent and realistic.

Paintings: Large areas of the walls and the ceiling, once covered with paintings, are much damaged. But, even so, this cave along with Cave 17 contain more surviving paintings than others. The paintings below are listed following the clock-wise circumbulation (pradakshina). Some of the important paintings, which are taken up for explanation, are given below.

As one enters the cave through the main doorway and turns left the first painting one sees on the inside wall of front verandah between the main doorway and the window to the left of it, is Shibi Jaataka of Cave 1, an exquisite composition. The scenes Royal Pavilion [Kbl 072] containing the details, Prince And His Consort [Kbl 075] and King weighs Himself [Kbl 074], containing among the grieving spectators Wailing Women [Mjt 051]. Another episode from the same jaataka is painted in Cave 17.

Bhikshu [Kbl 079], Janapada-Kalyaani [ASI 006], Maid with a Tray [Kbl 081L] and Maid tries to revive The Princess [ASI 007], are scenes from the narration of the Conversion of Nanda of Cave 1. This story is painted above the window on the inside wall of the front verandah to the left of the main doorway, beyond the narration of Shibi Jaataka.

An unidentified scene known as Palace Scene of the Naga Kingdom [Nou 076], of which a Dancer in the Naaga Court [Mjt 046] is painted on the left wall of the inside of the front verandah.

Shankha-paala Jaataka is painted on the left side of the mail wall. Most of the scenes are obliterated. Among the important surviving scenes are Shankha-paala listens to the Sermon [Kbl 082], of which Woman ListensTo The Sermon [ASI 009] is a detail, Hunters Drag the Serpent [Kbl 083T/L] and Shankha-paala in Conversation with Alaara [Kbl 083B/L].

A major portion of the left wall contains the narration of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka, following the story of Shankha-paala Jaataka. The following are the principal scenes of this jaataka. In the first act there are three scenes. These are Queen captivates Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L], Dancing Girl With Musicians [Kbl 091] and Mahaa-Janaka Going Out Through The Palace Gate. In the next act there is one scene in which Mahaa-Janaka Meets An Ascetic [Kbl 085] in the Himavali Hill. In the first scene of the third act Mahaa-Janaka Announces His Decision [Kbl 087R] to abdicate. Reconciled Queen [Kbl 089] and bewildered royal Household [Kbl 088] are its details. In the next scene of the third act Mahaa-Janaka Leaves The Palace [Kbl 096] on a horseback. The Three Maids [Kbl 097] are among the royal entourage. The last scene painted is the Shipwreck Scene, which is greatly damaged. Sufficient portions of this narration still remain to stand testimony to the glorious baroque period at Ajanta.

The panel Lustration and Renunciation comprises three frames, Preparation for the Ceremony [ASI 019], Ceremonial Bath [ASI 020] and Royal Household pays Homage [ASI 017] and some consider this depiction to be part of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka, painted on the northwestern wall, exhibits the Ajanta artist’s ‘multiple vision’ technique of perspective.

The two Bodhi-sattva-s, Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069] and Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshwara [Kbl 066], painted on either side of the wall leading to the shrine, particularly the former, are masterpieces. The Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani Panel is also a grand composition, the Padma-paani’s Consort [Kbl 068], the Mithuna Figures [ASI 022] and a Kinnara playing the Lute [Kbl 071T] are important from the stylistic and historical points of view also. On the other hand the Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshwara Panel contains one of the most beautiful women of Ajanta, Shakti Pandara [Mjt 078].

The Temptations Of The Buddha [Nou 048] is painted over the entire left wall of the ante-chamber. In this, the activities of Maara, the Buddhist devil, are interpreted with a vigour and liveliness which has few parallels, the only competitor being a sculpture on the same theme in Cave 23. The characters used by the devil to distract the Buddha Daughter of Maara [ASI 027] and Maara’s Attendent [Nou 050] are two of its details.

Champeyya Jaataka is painted on the wall of the rear verandah to the left of Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara above the doors of the two cells. In this compact composion five scenes are painted,. They are: Naaga King repents his Life of Pleasures [Kbl 105], King Uggasena watches Snake-charmer's Tricks [Kbl 106T] along with the Courtiers watch Snake Dance, [ASI 036], Appearance of Champeyya's Wife and Child, and, finally and, Champeyya Gives A Sermon [Kbl 106B]. Three Maids listening to the Sermon [ASI 038], an exquisit portrayal of women in beautiful poses, is a detail from the last mentioned scene.

An unidentified solo painting Queen in a Palace Scene [Nou 073], painted on the right hand wall of the main hall, is an example of the composition of the period of declining artistic standards. The woman holding a Lotus [Mjt 034] is a detail of the composition.

Another unidentified scene depicts Some Foreigners [Kbl 109], important from the point of view of Ajanta art history, for this painting is of little artistic merit intrinsically.

Ceiling Paintings: The ceiling, and some of the pillars, contains some exquisite animal and geometric motifs. Fighting Bulls [Mjt 013] painted on the capital of the second pillar in the left hand corner of the hall is one of the most forceful and vigorous drawings at Ajanta. The Charging Bull [ASI 030] and Elephant [ASI 028] are splendid examples of depiction of animals by the Ajanta artist and they show how conversant he was with the technique of kshaya-vriddhi or foreshortening, a technique of showing depth in drawing.. Squares and Rectangles - 1 [ASI 025] is a sample how the Ajanta artist composed the ceiling paintings with squares and rectangles filling them with all kinds of secular motifs, like Clown [ASI 008], Mythical Birds [ASI 005] and Cherubs in playful Mood [Kbl 114B]. One may consider themes like royal Harem [Kbl 108] out of place in a religious place.

Shibi rescues a Pigion

Shibi Jaataka

Cave 1, 6th century AD

The story of the generous King Shibi must have been very popular. Anecdotes of this king are found in Hindu and Jain lore. In Buddhist literature also several stories are attributed to the charitable King Shibi. Two incidents are painted in Ajanta, the story of the king rescuing a pigeon in this Shibi Jaataka of Cave 1, and, the story of Shibi donating his eyes to a blind person in Shibi Jaataka of Cave 17. The full ‘pigeon and hawk’ anecdote, with scales for weighing an equivalent weight of flesh, is not to be found in the earlier versions of the jaataka stories.

The Jaataka: Bodhi-sattva was born as King Shibi, the benevolent monarch. Gods Yama and Indra wanted to test his magnanimity. Indra took the form of a pigeon and Yama that of a pursuing hawk. The pigeon sought protection from the king. But the hawk claimed the pigeon as his legitimate prey and appealed for justice. The king, in order to protect the surrendered pigeon, offered a portion of his own flesh equal in weight to that of the pigeon. As he started cutting his flesh and putting it on the scale, the pigeon became heavier and heavier. Finally the king himself had to climb on to the scale. It is then that the gods revealed their true forms and showered great boons on him.

The story in this cave is depicted in three episodes on the inside wall of the front verandah, between the main doorway and the window to the left of it.

The first episode starts on the left. This shows King Shibi in a palace surrounded by ladies of the court when the pigeon alights on his lap to seek refuge. This part of the painting is greatly damaged. Towards the right there is Royal Pavilion [Kbl 072] containing the detail, Prince And His Consort [Kbl 075]. The second and main episode, King weighs Himself [Kbl 074], begins immediately to the right of this pavilion. In this scene the king is standing by the side of the scales and appears to be on the point of sacrificing his own flesh to save the life of the pigeon. The three lovely figures in Wailing Women [Mjt 051] are among the five figures on the right-hand side of the Prince. The Bodhi-sattva's ordeal is the cause of their lament.

The third episode, which is painted below the two just mentioned, is damaged almost beyond recognition; but a few fragments show that it is the climax in which the gods, human beings, animals and birds are rejoicing at the Raja's success.

In both the Hinaayana and the Mahaayana phases of development inflection of line and gradation of tone achieve the illusion of volume. The Ajanta artist could choose between two techniques to create an illusion of depth and relief. One was animnonnata (flat) style of painting, where representation is achieved by simple juxtaposition of flat areas of contrasted colours in well-defined planes and, the other was nimnonnata (high-relief) style, in which this effect was brought out by various methods of shading (vartana). But what is more noteworthy is that the two styles, as a number of painting show, seem to overlap at every stage of the development of the art. This is in contrast with the Western tradition where the development of art is generally linear.

Wailing Women [Mjt 051] and Prince And His Consort [Kbl 075], details in this narration, are examples of this animnonnata style. This has a remarkable parallel with the ‘cloisonnage’ of Gauguin. These figures, set against the green leaves of a peepal tree beyond which the vermilion horizon can be seen, produce a pleasing colour pattern.

In Shibi Jaataka of Cave 17 another episode from the life of the benevolent king where he gave his own eyes to a needy blind Brahmin is painted.

Conversion of Nanda

Cave 1; 5th Century AD

Conversion of Nanda of Cave 1 is an episode from the life of the Buddha and some scenes from it are painted above the window on the inside wall of the front verandah to the left of the main doorway, beyond the narration of Shibi Jaataka of Cave 1.

The Story: After attaining Enlightenment, the Buddha converted both his wife and his son to the monastic order, to lead a life of compassion and austerity. But his cousin, Nanda, was weak at heart and was initially reluctant to join the order, but later converted to monkhood. He had his head shaved and went about begging as a mendicant. On hearing of this his wife Janapada-Kalyaani swooned. However Nanda was not really happy being a monk and was brooding over his beautiful wife. The Buddha wanted to show him the right path. The Buddha took him to the heavenly abode of Indra, the lord of the gods. Nanda could see women more beautiful than his wife and wanted to stay there permanently. He was then ridiculed by the others for favouring a life of pleasure that is transitory. Bitten with remorse Nanda came to his senses. Coming back to the earth he lived the life of a monk following the Law.

There is also a view that the scene depicts the happenings at Kapila-vastu, when the Buddha paid a visit to his father and then went to the chamber of his wife Yashodhara to beg alms of her.

The mural is greatly damaged, but from what is surviving we have a fair idea of the composition from the line sketch of Bhikshu at the Palace Door [Line 19].

We have in this scene Nanda now a Bhikshu [Kbl 079] standing at the door of his own house begging for alms. He is calm, dignified, and radiating a lambent glow of compassion. He has come to his own house seeking food.

Inside the apartment on the left, an attendant is seen announcing the arrival of Nanda as a Bhikshu to Princess Janapada-Kalyaani, [ASI 006] Nanda's wife. The attendant seems very excited. He kneels down deferentially to announce the Bhikshu. His dress is fascinating, almost modern, and we come across this dress in other scenes. Perhaps it was the standard livery for palace attendants! To his right is a Maid with a Tray [Kbl 081L] in her hands. Despite the damage, her poise is exquisite. There is another female figure to her right sitting face-to-face with the princess. With her right hand she is seen explaining something to her mistress.

Janapada-Kalyaani [ASI 006], sitting on a throne with down cast eyes, has a melancholy expression. She is extremely graceful and dominates the entire scene. The graceful fingers of her left hand show the agitation in her mind. The dark-green backdrop presents a contrast to the reddish brown and yellow colours of the central figure. The colour used for backwash, it can be seen, is evenly laid without many nuances, so as not to distract from the main figure.

There are two more damsels in a dejected mood behind the princess.

The custom of women decorating their forehead with a tilak, also known as bindi, is a common sight today. But, in the Ajanta portraiture we find women sporting tilak only infrequently. Perhaps this became mandatory in the Hindu way of life later. In this mural we find that both the princess and the lady-attendant with a tray are both wearing the bindi.

The confusion and excitement created within the palace and witnessed in the expressions of the attendants on seeing the king in the garb of a beggar, the agitated and melancholy look of the princes, all combine to tell the same story.

There is one more detail of this story in the greatly damaged painting. In this we see a Maid tries to revive the Princeess [ASI 007]. A well-built maid with a glowing complexion tends her mistress, not included in the picture, a suffering princess reclining on a couch. She bends over the rails holding a pitcher in her hands; while water and flower-petals pour out, she literally pours herself. The scene is saturated with pathos. It is an example of one of the many damsels ‘dressed in nakedness’ in the Ajanta imagery. The painter has utilised all artistic means in order to emphasise the theme in its minute details, making all the accompaniments support the emotional content of the motif.

The circular lines of the pitcher balance the curves of the maid's body, though all the curves stroking the body-line, head or bust, are downward and heavy-laden. The anxious eyes of the attendant shower benediction. The upper limbs of the body, the neck, the bust and the waist, and the hips, are delineated in great detail. The pitcher-like breasts stand in incredible contrast to her slim waist. The colours are soft, and the artist has avoided sharp contrasts keeping in line with the melancholic mood of the scene.

Conversion of Nanda story must have been popular then, as we find this story is depicted in Cave 16 as well. We will be witnessing a few very poignant scenes of the narrative in this cave, like Dying Princess [ASI 003] wherein the same scene is depicted with even greater force and feeling. The composition is also different. The Ajanta artist was not monotonous.

Palace Scene of the Naaga Kingdom

Cave 1, Early 6th century, AD

Palace Scene of the Naga Kingdom [Nou 076] is from an unidentified scene, and is painted on the left wall of the inside of the front verandah. The line sketch Naga Court (Line ?) also helps us in appreciating the composition.

The king, in whose court a dance is being performed, is apparently naaga raja (the serpent-king), since he and his queen have halos of cobra-hoods over their heads. The crowded scene, composed of beautifully attired ladies of the court, female attendants, dwarfs and other courtiers, is typical of the late period.

The principal Dancer in the Naga-Court [Mjt 046] is sketched in extremely graceful curves, with long sweeping brush-strokes, creating a perfect rhythm and balance. The silky shine of her blue jacket is complemented by her pinkish complexion, and her bangles and coiffure are depicted with great care. The colour scheme of clear blue, deep purple-brown and rich malachite-green with touches of clear red, is very beautiful. She is drawn in the paraavrita ("head and one shoulder turned backwards") described in the post-Gupta treatise, Vishnu-dharmottara.

A new colouring technique in the use of lapis lazuli, a cool transparent blue, came into use in Ajanta only in the 4th-5th centuries AD. This is employed during the Mahaayana period to considerable effect in a number of paintings to produce a sense of distance and depth, and thereby to enhance the feeling of movement and animation.

The attire of the dancer, her entourage and the dancing pose she strikes may be compared with those of Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091], a scene from Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in the same cave.

Shankha-paala jaataka

Cave 1, 4th century, AD

Shankha-paala Jaataka is a long story painted on a small compact space on the left side of the main hall.

The Jaataka: Attracted by the wealth and splendour of the naaga-world that he happened to visit, Bodhi-sattva born King Duryodana of Magadha, wished to be born as a naaga king in his next birth. He was, then, born as Shankha-paala, king of the serpents in his next birth and inherited enormous wealth. But soon he got tired of all the luxuries of royal life. He became enlightened on hearing a sermon by an ascetic, who was the king of Magadha before he became a saint. Shankha-paala decided to end this aimless life and went and lay on an ant-hill. Some hunters spotted the huge snake, caught him and treated him cruelly. Alaara, a compassionate merchant took pity on the snake and released him paying some compensation in the form of cattle to his captors. The grateful Shankha-paala took Alaara to his kingdom and treated him as a royal guest. Later Alaara became a monk and proceeded to the Himalayas to preach the Law.

Worship of serpents and mythological stories connected with the kingdom of the serpents called naaga-loka must have been very ancient. The Bodhi-sattva taking birth as a serpent has been narrated often. In this cave is painted Champeyya Jaataka, in which Bodhi-sattva incarnated as a naaga king and deserted the kingdom in similar circumstances.

The major part of the narration of this jaataka is given as a sketch Ascetic gives Sermon [Line 2] and also in the colour picture Shankha-paala listens to the Sermon [Kbl 082]. The ascetic giving a sermon was the former king of Magadha. The head of the ascetic is completely obliterated. At the centre, the serpent-king Shankha-paala listens to the sermon. Shankhapaala’s figure is better preserved. He is sitting on a cushion with a bolster at the back with interesting patterns. His countenance speaks of deep devotion and his folded hands show his humility. A group of listeners, young women and men, aged people, birds and beasts sit around the ascetic.

The Ajanta artist was adept in portraying human figures in a variety of poses, sthaana-s. Here is an example. Among the persons present is a Woman listens to the Sermon [ASI 009]. She is squatting with her back to the artist in a graceful pose, leaning to her right. She is supporting her head with her right hand, resting her elbow on her knee. This pose is typical in India, even today. A composition of great observation and draughtsmanship!

To the left of the naaga king is depicted a very elegant lady in tribhanga (triflex) pose, listening to the sermon with great devotion and attention.

In the lower panel we see Hunters drag the Serpent [Kbl 083T/L] along the road. The size of the capture when compared to those of his persecutors suggests that Shankhapaala, the naaga king, is willingly enduring the pangs, being sworn to ahimsa. The effort of pulling such a huge snake has been very realistically shown.

Below the serpent we see the compassionate Alaara offering Money and cattle as compensation for freeing the tortured snake. Though damaged, ewe can still identify a few cattle, standing testimony to the artist's acute observation.

We find Shankha-paala in Conversation with Alaara [Kbl 083B/L], his rescuer at the bottom left of the panel. The royal features of Shankha-paala with his naaga hood and the rustic demeanour of Alaara, the merchant, are studies in contrast.

Mahaa-janaka jaataka

Cave 1, End of 6th Century, AD

Renunciation is a favourite theme of the jaataka-s. This painting is generally considered as referring to the renunciation of King Mahaa-janaka. Scenes from this jaataka are painted on the left wall of the main hall starting from the top of the door of the second cell and covering the rest of the wall. Some scholars also consider the renunciation scenes, titled Lustration and Renunciation [Line 18], painted on the rear wall as belonging to this jaataka.

The mural is of great importance from the point of view of art history. Due to the fortunate survival of the major portion, depiction of this jaataka offers scope for a detailed study of both the art style and method of narration of this period. In terms of the art style, these paintings show the beginning of deterioration from the classical elegance to baroque ostentation. A detailed study of these paintings would be rewarding as it could provide a wealth of information regarding jewellery, textile and dress, musical instruments, architecture, etc.

In terms of the art style, these paintings herald a new phase of Ajanta art, that is the beginning of baroque ostentation. The classical phase, of which the paintings of Cave 16 like Dying Princess [ASI 003], Votaries with Offerings [Kbl 126] of Cave 2 and the most magnificent composition, Simhala Avadana of Cave 17 are proud examples, gave way to mannerism witnessed in the superb narration of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka. The baroque sophistication that followed shows the beginning of deterioration as may be seen in the scenes of this jaataka. The eyes-slits are stretched out of proportion to the face, men look effeminate, and women exaggeratedly feminine, with bulging curves. Both men and women wear excessive ornaments, and strings of jewellery hang from the pillars and pavilions of royal chambers.

The Jaataka: Bodhi-sattva born Mahaajanaka was the son of Aritha-janaka, who was banished from his kingdom Mithila by his brother Pola-janaka. Mahaa-janaka went all over the world as a merchant and amassed enough wealth to help him regain his father's kingdom. In the meantime his uncle Pola-janaka died survived by his most beautiful and haughty daughter, Shivaali. Before his death he expressed the wish that Shivaali should marry only that person who could fulfil three conditions. Thus he set about three almost impossible conditions.

Mahaa-janaka fulfilled all three conditions and married Shivaali without knowing that he is marrying his cousin. However, the pomp and luxuries of the palace life were not to his liking. In spite of the pleadings from his queen, Mahaa-janaka finally renounced the world and went to the Himalayas to meditate upon the Truth.

Before we proceed to follow the mural let us keep in mind certain Ajanta conventions. Firstly, scenes in Ajanta murals are not separated from each other by frames, but tactfully placed rocks, gateways and pillars which serve as vague dividers that do not irritate the eye. Secondly, the narration may not be painted chronologically. Thirdly portraits of the same character resemble one another very closely.

The painted narration starts only after the marriage of Mahaa-janaka. Fortunately in this case the scenes are painted mostly in the chronological order, excepting that one of the earlier scenes of shipwreck is painted at the very end.

The story is narrated in three acts not taking into account the shipwreck scene. The following description is supported by two line sketches and a host of colour pictures.

Starting from the left, in the first act we have three scenes shown in the sketch Mahaa-janaka in Dilemma (Line ?). On the extreme left we have the first scene of Queen captivates Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L]. Beyond the pavilion and the pillars is a scene where a Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091] attempts to divert the attention of the king. Undeterred by all these, we see in the next scene, Mahajanaka leaves the Palace through the palace gate. The gate perhaps divides this act from the next.

Let us start with the scene of Queen captivates Mahaa-Janaka [Kbl 090L]. In this scene, the king is seen seated on a bed. He wears a crown adorned with gems. On his left is Shivaali, his queen, leaning against him. She has either nothing on, or else her dress is so diaphanous as to be invisible. She is one of Ajanta's women ‘dressed in nakedness’. She has her right elbow nuzzling the lap of the king. Every line of her figure expresses her attitude of passionate yielding. The king does not look at his wife. But, he has a wild and vacant look. His right hand suggests despair.

In the pillared hall there is a bevy of ten maids-in-attendance. All are looking at the couple, except three, who are watching the dancing in front of the pavilion. Their looks invite the attention of the viewer to the focal part of the painting, the pathetic king. In front of the king is an urn and at his back is a striped bolster. The pillared hall is approachable by a flight of steps. A dwarf, who is to be found in most royal scenes in Ajanta, is in attendance sitting on the ground to the right of the king. Though the sitting posture is the same in this scene and in the later scene where Mahaa-Janaka announces his Decision [Kbl 087R] to abdicate, the emotional atmosphere is different.

Beyond the pillars in the forecourt we see in the next scene in which a Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091] is entertaining the dejected king to draw him to the pleasures of the world and to divert his attention from spiritual pursuits. Dancing must have been a sophisticated art during this Mahaayana period, as can be seen in this scene. A professional dancer strikes a graceful pose of tribhanga while her hands eloquently express the mudra-s (hand-gestures). She is attired in a long jacket with full sleeves of a different colour from her close-fitting blouse. Her striped skirt is loose and long, allowing her full freedom of movement. The thumb-ring set with a miniature mirror, the ear-rings of elaborate design, and the head-dress consisting of strings of gold beads or pearls are all carefully depicted, and the dancer's plaits are ingeniously entwined with flowers.

She is surrounded by a group of five musicians: two are playing flutes, two playing cymbals, one a pair of standing drums, another drawn in full profile is playing a bell-shaped drum with a narrow ring for a grip, and the fifth, with her back to us, a stringed instrument. Like the dancer, all the accompanying musicians show great dynamism and movement.

The palatial surroundings and decorations, the accompaniment of a host of musical instruments, the fantastic garments and elaborate ornaments have all successfully contributed to staging a spectacular scene.

The queen was not successful in her attempt to hold back the king and Mahaa-janaka's mental turmoil has not ceased. He seeks the advice of a saint. In this next scene Mahaa-janaka goes out through the Palace Gate on an elephant.

In the second act we have the scene of Mahaa-janaka Meets An Ascetic [Kbl 085] in the Himavali Hill. The ascetic with matted hair and a rosary in his hand is delivering his sermon from a stone platform. Among the large number of listeners we find the king with his hands folded in deep reverence. This detail is given in Mahaa-janaka listens to a sermon [Kbl 087L]. True to the spirit of the Buddhist faith, we have at his feet a pair of buck and doe poised with faces tilted up, as if in rapt attention. By the way, the corkscrew curls falling on to the shoulders of the ascetic are a baroque mannerism.

In the third and the last act, shown in the sketch Mahaa-janaka Abdicates (Line ?), the drama shifts back to the palace. On the left Mahaa-Janaka announces his Decision [Kbl 087R] to the Reconciled Queen [Kbl 89] and to the Bewildered Royal Household [Kbl 088] and then Mahaa-Janaka Leaves The Palace [Kbl 096] along with his entourage including Three Maids [Kbl 097].

In the first scene of the second act the enlightened Mahaa-Janaka announces his Decision [Kbl 087R] to abdicate. This scene is full of pathos and feeling.

To the left of the first scene of this panel - refer to Mahaa-janaka Abdicates (Line ?) - on the extreme left we see the queen-mother is sitting on a stool with the queen kneeling before her and an animated discussion is on. Perhaps the queen-mother is admonishing the queen. Two maids are to be seen witnessing the proceedings with great anxiety; the one behind the queen-mother, a chaawri-bearer, wearing a colourful dress, strikes a sculpturesque pose of great poise.

At the centre of the scene is the king announcing his decision to renounce the world in spite of the pleadings of Queen Shivaali. Perhaps the queen has reconciled herself to the situation. There are a few attendants around the royal couple, two are waving the whisk. The girl behind the queen is stunned with her eyebrows drawn and eyes dilated, and the girl above the queen is musing with a finger on her cheek. The dwarf is still holding on to the same vantage position.

Now we come to the last scene of the drama. Mahaa-janaka leaves the Palace [Kbl 096] on horseback through the palace gate, which starts a new act in this drama. The king is seen leaving the palace in full regal splendour, led by a procession of his loyal subjects. The royal umbrella is held aloft. A large number of palace musicians are in attendance. The entire scene is full of animation. In the musicians leading the procession are, in addition to a flautist and a cymbal-player, the two instruments we have seen before in the last scene, a conch-blower, one playing a double-sided drum like the modern day pakhawaj of the North or mridangam of the South. The whole scene looks like an excellent snapshot.

The panel that follows is the scene of shipwreck and its sequel and is greatly damaged. Further, as mentioned before, this scene is a very early scene when Mahaa-janaka went on a ship voyage to amass wealth.

Let us study the portrayal of characters. The king is depicted three times. They are in the scenes Queen captivates Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L], Mahaa-janaka Meets an Ascetic [Kbl 085] and Mahaa-Janaka announces his Decision [Kbl 087L]. Though the sitting posture in all the three scenes is the same, the emotional atmosphere is different. Let us compare these scenes from this angle.

In the first scene of Act I, the newly-wed king was in regal splendour - two rows of necklaces sparkling with diamonds and pearls, one is fitting round the neck and the other an elaborate one is slightly longer. These are in addition to the pearl-studded sacred-thread. When he met the ascetic the elaborate necklace was replaced with a less ostentatious one. (But why is the sacred-thread now around the left elbow, which in Hindu practice, is worn only during ceremonial ablution to the dead forefathers? Was the Ajanta artist careless?) And finally, when he announces his decision to renounce, he has cast off every-thing including the sacred-thread, excepting the close fitting beads. Is the removal of the sacred thread suggestive of the impending sanyaasa?

In the first scene the king has a vacant look reflecting his mental conflict and his hands showing his inner pain. While sitting before the sage his whole deportment is one of humility and utter surrender, his hands folded in prayer. But in the last scene his visage is serene and full of compassion and love, but of firm determination. His hands compose dharma chakra mudra (preaching-attitude) to show that he has now found the path.

The change in the attitude of Shivaali is even more pronounced in the two scenes that she depicted in, when pleading with the king in the first scene and when being informed of the king’s renunciation. From a conceited queen, ‘dressed nakedness’ determined to win over her husband in the earlier scene, she is now a well-draped lady sitting before the king "erect like a candle undisturbed by air" ready to receive the heart-breaking news.

It is interesting also to note that the continuity in dress and physical features of the characters is maintained, while bringing out the contrast in the expressions of the characters.

Musicians playing musical instruments have been shown many scenes in Ajanta. A variety of musical instruments have been depicted, both in paintings and in sculpture. These cover the period from the 2nd century, BC to the about 6th century, AD. So Ajanta offers scope for study of development of the musical heritage of India. We can see both the continuity and change over the period as presented in Ajanta itself.

First let us compare the musicians present in the two scenes in this jaataka. The accompanying musicians of the dancer in the earlier scene were cymbal-players, flautists, drummers and a musician playing a string-instrument. We find from this painting and also elsewhere in Ajanta that side-flutes were in vogue even during this period. It may be mentioned that side-flutes came into use in the West only in the 14th century. In the same way cymbals continue to keep time measure in dance performances even today. The bell-shaped drum with a narrow ring for a grip looks more like some of the drums used in folk music. The standing drums are no more found in India. We are not able to make a judgement on the string-instrument as this part of the painting is somewhat mutilated.

A slightly different group of musicians accompany the king when he abdicates. The musician blowing a conch-shell is shown with puffed cheeks and popping eyes. Conch is still being used for heralding the arrival of important people like the king or during the ceremonial procession of deities and pooja-s in temples and houses. The mridangam (or pakhawaj) player is shown very realistically in the midst of executing a complex rhythmic pattern with both the hands. His instrument and playing technique are the same as those used today. The playing posture as well as fingering of the flute player look natural, and we now realise an unbroken musical tradition of not less than 15 centuries.

We can witness another set of musicians in the composition Descent Of Indra [Kbl 164] in Cave 17. In the Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani Panel [Line 3] in this cave itself we come across the kinnara-s playing musical instruments, one of the instruments resembling the modern mandolin.

The dancer in the first act striking a graceful pose of tribhanga was already mentioned; her dress and ornaments were described in detail. Dancers have been portrayed also in other scenes in Ajanta. For example, Dancer in the Naga-Court [Mjt 046] in this cave is one, where the principal dancer is drawn matching the expertness of the dancer in this story, though not as elaborately. Fortunately we have a composition depicting dancers accompanied by musicians belonging to the earlier period, namely of the 2nd-1st century, BC in the Dancers and Musicians [Mjt 107] painted in Cave 10, though badly damaged. One can feel the unaffected gay abandon in the Hinaayana masterpiece, as against the Mahaayana painting which is more stylised. The scanty clothing and the few ornaments, such as bangles of conch-shell modelled on traditional folk ornaments of the pre-Christian era painting is in contrast to the beautifully designed garments, the variety of glittering trinkets, and bewildering coiffure of the dancer in the Mahaa-janaka court. The costumes, the hair styles, the shape of the eyes and the general atmosphere all epitomise the final phase in the development of the Ajanta style of painting.

We shall consider the various techniques used in Ajanta to produce an illusion of the third dimension. Perspective is a technique of drawing objects on a surface giving an illusion of distance and solidity. In this the farther objects look small. This may be called true perspective or normal perspective. Another way is the reverse perspective. The building in front of which the dancer is performing with her orchestra is not shown in the true perspective, but is drawn in the reverse perspective. This cannot be ascribed to the Ajanta artist's carelessness or to his lack of knowledge of drawing in perspective, for he has executed some remarkable paintings in true perspective. The paintings showing Gateway to Brahma-datta’s Palace [Mjt 097] in Cave 2 and the Monastery [Kbl 227R], a scene from Shibi Jaataka of Cave 17 are excellent examples for true perspective. We should imagine, then, the artist composed the scene to suite the theme rather than exhibiting realism. We come across with reverse perspective in the depiction of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka of Cave 2. We will have some more to say on the technique of showing distances while explaining Lustration and Renunciation [Line 18] scene in the same cave.

Another method of creating an illusion of the third dimension is producing depth and relief while portraying characters. One of the methods used for this is called ujjotana (adding-highlights). In the execution of the figure of Mahaa-janaka and other characters, the artist has made use of the ujjotana technique, that is, by adding white patches appearing on the chin, breasts, arms, legs or wherever an elevation of form was desired. This method is different from vartana (shading). Adding to this was the use of blue colour, especially for the background to create an illusion of depth in contrast to the warm red and brown colours.

A noteworthy feature of Ajanta is the expert draughtsmanship. The perfection of painting technique is displayed in the extraordinary breadth and confidence of draughtsmanship - the varying thickness of line drawn with a free flowing sweep of the brush to depict the oval faces, arched eyebrows, aquiline noses, and fine sensitive lips. The lines have become darker to serve as a suitable accessory to the vartana (shading) and ujjotana (adding-highlights) techniques for creating an illusion of depth. The visual depth is enhanced by the judicious use of blue - of lapis lazuli - by contrasting it with the warmth of the red and brown tones. The illusion of spaciousness is also evident where the reddish ochre, yellow ochre and dark brown tints applied to the dancing girl and her imposing costume stand out against a plain bluish-green background.

This draughtsmanship is evident in the variety of body positions of the characters portrayed. An important feature of the Ajanta style is the consummate skill in the use of variety of sthaana-s (body positions) while portraying characters in Ajanta. In all the scenes depicted in this narration, every one of the women is shown in different positions - from the rijvagata (frontal) to parshvagata (strict profile) to anriju (back view).

The Ajanta artists followed certain conventions in separating scenes. A gate is often placed to herald the beginning of an act. In this panel, we find the king on an elephant starts a new act. One will not fail to notice that the king passes through the gate once more in this narration, that is, when finally Mahaa-janaka leaves the Palace [Kbl 096] after abdication. Within the first Act the pillars of the pavilion separates the first scene from the following one of dance performance. So is the case in the last act.

Ajanta, as said before, is a treasure house to study the contemporary fashion in textiles, jewellery, etc. As an example, the chamara-bearing girl sports an upper-garment with rows of geese printed on it - a fashion referred to in the contemporary literature. Note the architectural details of the palace in this as well as in a later scene. This is characteristic of the baroque period - too many pillars and rather excessive decoration on the pavilion.

Lustration and Renunciation

Mahaa-janaka Jaataka

Cave 1, End of 6th Century, AD

On the rear wall of the main hall there is a panel showing the facade of three colonnaded pavilions. Many experts feel that these scenes are from Mahaa-janaka Jaataka, depicting lustration and renunciation of Mahaa-janaka. The description below is supported by a sketch Lustration and Renunciation [Line 18] showing the three pavilions and, their details in three colour pictures, Preparation for the Ceremony [ASI 019], Ceremonial Bath [ASI 020] and Royal Household pays Homage [ASI 17].

In the central pavilion is shown the Preparation for the Ceremony [ASI 019]. A lady with a tray displays movement so free and poise so graceful that its equal is hard to find. This lady, as well as the other one in the scene, is dressed in garments that are almost transparent or just nude. Also found in this central panel are two more persons: an old servant leaning on his staff and another carrying a pitcher on his head. The dark complexion of the lady with a tray is beautifully balanced by the red column towards her left and with the fair complexion of the other woman.

The pavilion on the extreme right depicts the scene of the king being given a Ceremonial Bath [ASI 020]. The king is shown squatting on a throne while two servants are pouring water over him from fluted metal pitchers. The wet curly hair of the king is seen falling on his shoulders. An odd assortment of persons, including a dwarf, is in attendance in this ceremonial bath, including three women holding trays. The three women attendants are painted in different colours and are extremely graceful in their act of aiding Mahaa-janaka with his royal dress and ornaments.

In between the left and central panels, apparently just outside the pillared partition, four beggars are seen begging for alms. One of them has an umbrella over his head. A clump of banana trees, beyond the brick wall, forms the background.

In the left panel we find Mahaa-janaka, now a venerated ascetic, exuding compassion, seated on a low stool and the Royal Household pays Homage [ASI 017]. A lady of distinction, perhaps his queen, kneels down before him while a few other distinguished ladies stand nearby.

In terms of composition, this panel is one of the masterpieces of Ajanta - a proper synthesis of the details and the characters are subdued enough to highlight the central theme - the royal lustration. The various skin-colours and the red of the columns of the pavilion are harmoniously blended against the dull olive green of areca-nut palm leaves, producing a delightful colour-pattern. The lavish furnishing and royal splendour of the palace is striking.

It was mentioned that some do not agree with the view that these scenes are from Mahaa-janaka Jaatak. They point out that the master artists of Ajanta were very particular about the physiognomic features of the characters portrayed, and portraits of the same character resemble one another with photographic accuracy. But the woman painted in this panel widely differs from the queen Shivaali painted in the panels described earlier.

While studying Ajanta paintings two interesting aspects in the portrayal of women can be noticed. Almost all the beautiful women of Ajanta are depicted as black beauties. The examples are: Shakti Pandara [Mjt 078], painted in the panel of Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara in this cave, Princess Irandati [Kbl 130] of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka in Cave 2, the Dying Princess [ASI 003] of Cave 16 and the famous Black Apsaras [Kbl 169] painted as part of a large panel commonly called the Adoration Of The Buddha , are all painted ‘as dark as the night’! Weren't both Rama and Krishna dark complexioned?

The second aspect is that we find that most of the heroines are ‘dressed in nakedness’. Without descending to vulgarity and retaining their essential dignity, the Ajanta damsels exude a rare charm and innocence. We have just seen that the two beautiful women in this panel are either without any dress or wear transparent dresses. In an earlier panel, Queen captivates Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L], the queen ‘has either nothing on, or else her dress is so diaphanous as not to leave her charms concealed’, whereas all the maids were fully clothed. But in the next scene when the king informs her of his abdication, she is ‘decently’ dressed, as the occasion demanded it. The Dying Princess [ASI 003] in Cave 16 is a nude, though, again, the artist has carefully arranged dresses for all the palace-maids.

Perhaps the contemporary taste was far more liberal or the artist could take liberties with social and ethical conventions while portraying women. But the intriguing question that cannot easily be answered is how these found a place at all in a monastery for monks of a strict religious order, which shunned the pleasures of life. Perhaps we are wrong in our understanding of the Buddhist religion of those times.

Finally let us discuss a technical feature. That the Ajanta artist employed both true perspective and reverse perspective was mentioned before. A mention to this effect was made while explaining the pavilion in reverse perspective in the Mahaa-janaka panel wherein a dancer was attempting to charm the king. We see that in this panel is depicted a sequence of scenes in three pavilions and that they are not in true perspective. The following may be the reason.

Painting this scene in true perspective would have posed some problems. If one were to stand in front of the right-most pavilion, the scene would look what is shown on the left of the line sketch Multiple Vision (Line ?). Then it would not be possible to draw the different people to portray the theme. For example, no details of the persons in the left pavilion can be shown. To solve this problem, the Ajanta artist has adopted the method the present day movie makers use called ‘multiple-vision’. That is, the Ajanta artist has taken three different ‘shots’ and superimposed them on one another, as shown on the right of Multiple Vision (Line ?). Actually, "the artist has not copied any existing pavilion, but has imagined in his mind's eye, and has wished to represent it in all its details".

Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani Panel

Cave 1, Second half of 6th century, AD

Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani, [Kbl 069] the central figure of this panel, is the most popular painting of Ajanta and no collection of Ajanta painting can be complete without this masterpiece. This panel is painted on the back wall, to the left of the antechamber, symmetrical with the Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara panel on the other side of the antechamber.

The painting belongs to the post-classical period of mannerism, to be followed by baroque ornamentation deviating from the classical idealism, as may be seen in the narration of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in this cave. Known for its brilliant composition, this panel compels the beholder's eye towards it and one is struck by the general ensemble and unity of the theme. The remarkable fact is that the massive proportions of the figure of Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani - it measures 1.8 metres across the shoulders - in no way detracts from the harmonious composition. The panel may be studied making use of the line sketch Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani Panel [Line 03]. Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069], Padma-paani’s Consort [Kbl 068] shown on his left, the Mithuna Figures [ASI 022] depicted on the left above the main figure, and, finally, the background itself are the details in colour.

Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069] is a personification of apparently contradictory qualities of perfect compassion and perfect indifference, both existing simultaneously. Out of compassion for the countless creatures in the cosmos, he renounced the pursuit of Buddha-hood and nirvaana, so that he might continue preaching the Buddhist doctrine until the last being in the cosmos received enlightenment and thus was released from the chain of rebirths.

On the other hand, this dispassionate Bodhi-sattva has, paradoxically enough, all the qualities that youth, high birth, noble lineage and religious ardour can produce: strong masculine limbs, a broad chest, a well-set neck, a high philosopher's brow, large meditative eyes and firm lips. The supple body is full of vigour, but the plastic grace is almost feminine and at the same time detached from the distracting environment. The Bodhi-sattva, lambent with the glow of wisdom, and marked out for the spirituality of Buddhahood wears a rich diadem on his forehead and rows of necklaces adorning his neck. Through the extraordinary confidence of the line and the softness of the shading the countenance reflects a lyrical and pensive abstraction. The flawless, opalescent smoothness of the skin, the beautiful proportion of body to face, and the eyes half-closed in meditation radiate a celestial beauty and purity. The textures and muscular structure are scarcely indicated so that the figures symbolize the divine anatomy of supernatural beings.

Thus, the Padma-paani figure is the finest example of the realisation of the classical ideal through symbolism based on animals and plants, as laid down in the shastra-s. Usually the limbs of the cult image are related to the shapes and forms of the vegetal and animal worlds. For example, the eyes of the Bodhi-sattva are likened to lotus petals, the curving brow-line to that of the arching bow and the ovoidal face is supposed to resemble an egg. Further, his shoulders and torso are compared with the domed head of an elephant and the body of a lion respectively.

During this period a new technique of luminosity called chaaya-tapa (shade-and- shine) was introduced. A soft glow that can be seen on the outline of the body against the slightly darker background. This technique has succeeded in casting that unreal, mystical light which makes the sacred countenances of the Buddhas and Bodhi-sattvas radiate with a mysterious glow.

The illusion of ethereal light gently thrown on the Bodhi-sattva from his left produces a soft shadow on the right side of the face and neck. But unlike normal chiaroscuro, the left side of the face is also treated with a deeper brown tone than the softly highlighted right cheek and nose. In conformity with the Ajanta tradition, there are no cast shadows. These are scrupulously shunned by the artists who realised, as did Leonardo da Vinci in his time, that direct light and cast shadows destroy the representation of true relief or volume.

The luminosity is further enhanced by the masterly handling of blue, lapis lazuli, sparingly employed on the necklace beads and delicately applied on the fringes of the fresh lotus. This is reflected in the warmth of the golden brown and pearl grey of the Bodhi-sattva's complexion. At the same time, perspective is created in the background by the relative depth in space of the blue jacket of the woman behind the Padma-paani's left arm, and of the blue birds in the conventional bands of hills in the top left-hand corner of the picture.

To the left of the Bodhi-sattvaa is painted Padma-paani’s Consort [Kbl 068]. She is holding a lotus to indicate that she is his consort. She is one of the most graceful women of Ajanta, exuding serenity and love. Also another instance of ‘black is beautiful’!

On the left part of the panel are the Mithuna Figures [ASI 022] watching the Great Being from their hill resort as they sit in an amorous mood, exhibiting an incredible bhaava-madhura (tender feeling) of reciprocal love. The elegance of the contentment and modesty of the figures is stressed more by the almost symbolic tenderness of the soft caress, than from the facial expressions.

A number of mithuna figures have been painted below the eight Buddhas in Cave 17. This painting may be compared with prince and his Consort [Kbl 075] a scene from Shibi Jaataka which depicts the same subject in the same cave, but provides a complete contrast in the manner of drawing and colouring. Prince and his Consort [Kbl 075] is a good example of the animnonnata (flat) style of shading consisting of flat irregular planes of primary colours. But Mithuna Figures [ASI 022] is done using the nimnonnata (high relief) treatment of painting, retaining the softness and roundness of the limbs, demarcated only by a slight deepening of the colours along the edges.

The restraint of the sacred style of painting in the Bodhi-sattvas is beautifully balanced by the lively background and the warm atmosphere of the secular scene. The background is infused with the colours of the luxuriant foliage of the areca palms and the ashoka trees, the strong red squares like pillars and cross-pieces which are a convention for the ridges of hills, the clear blue of the bird's feathers, and a variety of other colours which enliven the drapery and jewellery and even the skin-tones of the men and women.

There is a female chaawri-bearer wearing a long blue coat, while on the right is a dark mace-bearer in a long white coat. In the background monkeys are seen frolicking about and a pair of peacocks shrilling in joy. In the left corner is a Kinnara playing the Lute [Kbl 071T] quite similar to the present day mandolin, and cymbals, giving us an idea of the kind of musical instruments used at that time. We come across musicians and contemporary musical instruments in the scenes of Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091] and Mahaa-janaka leaves the Palace [Kbl 096] the narration of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka of Cave 1 and in the painting Descent Of Indra [Kbl 164] in Cave 17.

Temptations of the Buddha

Cave 1, 5th century, AD

Painted on the left wall of the ante-chamber, this panel, called the Temptations Of The Buddha [Nou 048], shows the assault on Gautama by Maara's army and the amorous attempts by Maara's daughters to deflect him from the path of Enlightenment.

The beauty of this painting and its artistic quality compare with the classic composition Simhala Avadaana depicted in Cave 17. Here too the artist's vision is projected over an entire wall, and the activities of Maara, the Buddhist devil, are interpreted with a vigour and liveliness which has few parallels.

The Episode: Gautama sat under the Bodhi tree in deep meditation in order to attain enlightenment when Maara, the Evil Spirit, approached him. Maara made many attempts to dislodge the Master from his resolve lest he should achieve the supreme moment and open up the path of salvation for humankind. Having scared away hosts of gods who were waiting for the great moment, the spirit of sensual pleasure and worldly passion at first tried to persuade the Bodhi-sattva to abandon his quest by tempting him with the desire for power and glory. For days Gautama withstood temptations of all kinds. Having failed, Maara, disguised as a messenger falsely told the would-be Buddha that his wicked cousin Devadatta had rebelled, thrown his father into prison and seized his wife Yashodara. It was in vain. He then sent his three most beautiful daughters called Lust, Thirst and Delight to disport themselves before the sage. They danced and sang and contrived every means of seduction; but Gautama rose above their wiles. Thereupon Maara summoned his demons and made a furious and appalling assault with whirlwind, tempest, flood and earthquake to dislodge Gautama. But Siddhartha, calm and unmoved, sat under the Bodhi tree. The frustrated Maara then challenged His right to sit on the spot and even demanded evidence of his goodness and benevolence. The Master called upon the Earth to bear witness to his right by virtue of his acts in previous existences. This he did by touching the ground with his hand, and the Earth spoke with a voice of thunder: "I am his witness." Maara, defeated, fled in dismay; and it was in the course of the succeeding night that Siddhartha passed from the state of being a Bodhi-sattva to complete and perfect Buddhahood.

In the following discussion you may refer to the line sketch Maara’s Episode (Line ?), and the pictures in colours Temptations Of The Buddha [Nou 048], Daughter Of Maara [ASI 027] and Maara’s Attendant [Nou 050] which are details of the composition.

The Buddha seated in the vajraasana (adamantine-posture) in bhoomi sparsha mudra (earth-touching-attitude), serene and calm, is painted in the centre of the wall, and He is surrounded by a fantastic variety of monsters and witches.

Around Him there is a group of seven young damsels whose pretty features, graceful poses and soft facial expressions have been justly compared to the women in Raphael's paintings. Especially beautiful are the two women one on either side of the Buddha. They are apparently two of the three enchanting daughters Of Maara, Rati (lust), Trishna (thirst) and Arati (delight) who unsuccessfully tried every means of seduction. A Daughter of Maara [ASI 027] is shown as an example. They have been painted in the mould of the classical beauty of Ajanta, well qualified to do their appointed task of seduction - full breasts, pouting lips, round eye-balls and ear-rings.

If Maara's daughters are personifications of seductive beauty, his army is a caricature of the horrible and detestable. Maara’s Attendant [Nou 050], a part of Maara’s army is painted to the right of the Supreme One beyond Maara's daughter and is an interesting study. The artist has employed deep contrasts in tone in the face to bring out the grotesque and banal facet of his mission. The serio-comic effect is principally created by placing the clumsy face in the shadow, accentuated by a few clownish spots of highlight on the chin, lower and upper lip, the nose and the two ungainly earrings. At the same time, the lighter areas balance the paleness of the skull and the band of the attendant's headgear. The result is one of ridicule.

In the execution of the characters of this drama, the artist has exhibited his skill in the use of highlighting, called ujjotana (adding-highlights), appearing on the chin, breasts, arms, legs or wherever an elevation of form was desired.

The theme of this unsuccessful venture of Maara and the Master's conquest of temptations is a favourite subject-matter of Buddhist artists. A master-piece depicting the same scene but in relief in stone, Temptations of the Buddha - Sculpture (Line ?), can be found in Cave 26. One is at a loss to decide who is superior - the master-artist with the brush or the one with the chisel and hammer. May be the sculptor has a slight edge over his brother-artist!

Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara Panel

Cave 1, Second half of 6th Century, AD

As in the composition of the Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani Panel [Line 03] on the left side of the wall leading to the ante-chamber, is the painting Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara Panel [Line 04]. In it we find, overwhelming his rich surroundings by his personal splendour, stands the Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara [Kbl 066].

On the top left near a gate is a standing couple absorbed in religious conversation, as is apparent from the mudra of the right hand of the male figure. A dark lady of singular charm, commonly known as Shakti Pandara [Mjt 078] or Dark Princess in the left lower corner, accepts lilies offered by another lady. Above this is a royal figure making an offering of lilies to the Bodhi-sattva. Though richly attired, his glory fades into insignificance in the presence of the divine Bodhi-sattva.

Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara [Kbl 066], earlier identified as Bodhi-sattva Vajrapani, is regarded as having emanated from Amitabha and his Shakti Pandara. He is the most esteemed Bodhi-sattva of the Mahaayana pantheon, being styled sangha-ratna (Jewel of the Order).

He poses with a Divine Buddha on the crest of his immense bejewelled diadem. Bodhi-sattvas are identified by just such a Buddha-sign on the crest. His dark complexion points to his southern origin. The elaborate jewellery of the Bodhi-sattva reflects southern customs and fashions. On the other hand the northern influence is still predominant in Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069], which has Aryan features and a fair complexion. The dark skin, round face and fuller lips that were prominent in the Ajanta paintings of the pre-Christian era reappear in the Deccani-type ethnic structure of the Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara [Kbl 066].

In the drawing and colouring the artists seem to have used similar techniques as were used in the Padma-paani panel. There is, however, a greater relief effect in the drawing of the Avalokiteshvara, chiefly because of the larger area of greenish background to the figure.

Shakti Pandara [Mjt 078], the lady on the right of the pair shown in the bottom-left corner in the panel, accepting lilies from the other lady, is one of the most famous of Ajanta paintings at Ajanta. In the opinion of Yazdani it is one of the finest works of art in the world. Popularly known as the Dark Princess, it is probably a representation of Amitabha's Shakti Pandara.

Its fine modelling, exquisite ornamentation, tranquillity and thoughtfulness are beautifully brought out by the dark complexion against a lighter background. Certainly there is no other portrait in Ajanta with such perfect eyes; the hazel-brown of the pupils and the red spots in the corners of the eyes radiate an astonishing natural friendliness. It is truly an unusual painting, considering the late period to which it belongs, when the elongated and exaggerated eyes of a declining art were already in vogue.

It may be recalled that in an earlier discussion it was pointed out that most of the beautiful women of Ajanta are depicted as black beauties and this detail was taken as an example. Note that Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara [Kbl 066] depicted in this panel is also of dark complexion and this was attributed to the southern influence.

Champeyya Jaataka

Cave 1, 4th century, AD

The story of Champeyya is painted on the wall of the rear verandah, left of Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara above the doors of the two cells.

The Jaataka: The Bodhi-sattva was once born as Champeyya, the naaga king. He felt unhappy with his wasteful life, and attempted to take his life. His wife Sumana helped to revive his interest in life and was his constant companion. Once he was caught by a cruel Brahmin, and taught him dancing. The Brahmin made the serpent-king dance before King Uggasena. Meanwhile, Sumana, who was searching for her lost husband, finally found her husband in the court of King Uggasena entertaining the king with his dance. On her plea the king released the serpent from captivity. It is then that King Uggasena came to know that the snake was none other than Bodhi-sattva born as the naaga king. Bodhi-sattva took King Uggasena to the naaga-world, where he gave him a sermon. Later he helped the king to vanquish his enemies.

The artist has delineated the story in five scenes, arranged in two rows. The top row depicts three scenes and on the bottom row the remaining two scenes. The entire jaataka is given in the sketch Story of Champeyya [Line 17], which may be referred to while following the individual scenes.

In the first scene, Champeyya Repents his Life of Pleasures [Kbl 105] shown at the extreme left of the top row, we see the Bodhi-sattva, born as naaga-king, Champeyya sitting on a throne repenting of his life of pleasures. Sumana, his queen is sitting by his side and is looking up eagerly towards her husband. As usual, the court dwarf is in attendance, this time holding a tray of fruits, flowers and cosmetics, but the way he looks up eagerly at the king shows his concern. There are two more maids-in-attendance in this scene.

To watch the next scene, King Uggasena watches Snake-Charmer's Tricks [Kbl 106T] we must go to the extreme right. The snake-charmer, we find, is on the extreme right. His face is damaged. The serpent is in front of him, uncoiling out of the basket. King Uggasena, with his head completely missing, is seen seated on a throne. The folds of his upper garments are artistically drawn and as usual the bolster at his back has interesting motifs on it. With his right hand he has made a gesture, expressive of wonder. His minister is sitting on his left. The serpent-king, Champeyya, while showing his dances, suddenly sees something that makes him stop midway in his dance.

As a part of the second scene we find some courtiers also watching the snake show. Among the Courtiers watch the Snake-Dance [ASI 036] there are two very interesting figures, whose racial traits and dress are very characteristic. The features of one of them, the moustache, and the tuft of hair on the crown of his head, are so typical that even today he might be taken for a citizen of Varanasi. His upper-cloth is of silver brocade, with floral patterns of silk worked on it, is reminiscent of the dress and the famous brocade industry of the place.

The features of the other figure are like those of a Brahmin of Orissa. The style of his hair and the manner in which he has thrown a part of his dhoti across his shoulder are also characteristic of the Orissa people. The poses of the figures are extremely interesting, particularly the homely way in which the Oriya is sitting and looking at his Varanasi comrade.

Between the two panels described above, we have the Appearance of Sumana and her Child. A mother with her child is seen appealing to a doorkeeper for admission to the court. This, in fact, was the reason why the naaga-king had halted his dance, for he suddenly recognises them as his own wife and son.

The part of the story of the king's recognition of the serpent as the naaga-king and the reason why he came to such a pitiable state has not been painted in this panel. The remaining story is painted in the bottom row.

Moving right, we come to a royal hall full of ladies and attendants, where Champeyya gives a Sermon [Kbl 106B] initiating Uggasena into the mysteries of the faith. Of the female figures, the most charming is that of Queen Sumana, standing above the left shoulder of Champeyya having a serpent-hood over her head. Her features are exquisite and her face has a delicate lambent grace.

There are as many as seven maids-in-attendance in different poses and attitudes. The Three Maids listening to the Sermon [ASI 038] are typical Ajanta beauties in graceful poses. One of the three male figures is our inexorable dwarf. The dwarf has raised his eyebrows to show his indifference to the serious sermons of the Bodhi-sattva.

The second male figure is that of an old servant standing on the right of Uggasena near the pillar. Here again we find the artist in a lighter vein, for the servant is on the point of helping himself to some fruit from a tray held by a girl who is too rapt in listening to the sermon to notice the theft. Another girl appears to be apprehensive of the mischief and without turning her head is glaring at the thief out of the corner of her eyes.

The third male figure is that of the naaga-King's bodyguard. Dressed in a long silk-coat with flowers interwoven in it and with tight sleeves, he is armed with a sword having a jewelled handle suggesting that he is the king's bodyguard. He has a cap on his head with a round brim and peaked top. Most likely, he is a foreigner. The scene also illustrates the court-architecture.

The Ajanta artist has a typical sense of humour. He portrays incidences in a lighter vein in rather serious situations. One instance was referred to in this narration. There are a few more of examples. In Cave 2, a humorous scene is sculpted under the figure of Hariti. On a pedestal we find a group of five boys goading two rams to fight, a common mischief of the ubiquitous backbenchers and truants even today!

Queen in a Palace Scene

Cave 1, End of 6th century AD

The mural commonly known as Queen in a Palace Scene [Nou 073] is painted on the right hand wall of the main hall. The painting has not yet been identified. A line sketch Queen in a Palace [Line 16] in addition the colour reproduction Queen in a Palace Scene [Nou 073] may referred to while following the discussion.

A queen rests on a cushion surrounded by her retinue and converses with the king. These figures are somewhat disproportionate and are disposed in a mechanical way. They represent a decline in artistic standards, marked by anatomical distortions. Compared with the fifth century paintings, the classical poses and decorative elements are exaggerated. The peculiarities of the style are the heavy heads, elongated eyes, excessively large mouths, thin legs, and the superfluous hand gestures. In addition the composition becomes careless, perhaps because of mass production, and the finish of the paintings was summary. There is an unmistakable overcrowding in the scene, which is against the spirit of classicism.

The Ajanta artist has adopted simple as well as sophisticated techniques to bring out depth and relief. One among them is vartana. In this, relief is brought about by various methods of shading. A different technique was ujjotana. In this technique, the addition of highlights appropriately gives a three dimensional effect. The use of this technique may be seen in this scene and in a detail of this scene, woman holding a Lotus [Mjt 034]. Highlights in the form of white patches may be found on the cheeks, the chin, nose, etc of the characters.

Some Foreigners

Cave 1, End of 6th century AD

On the right-hand wall is an unidentified scene depicting Some Foreigners [Kbl 109], which is significant for perhaps this panel is one of the last to be painted by the Ajanta artist before he abandoned the caves. This conclusion is based on the style of the painting.

The detail shows three fair skinned persons and a dark complexioned person who has an expressive face. These fair skinned people are most likely foreigners judging from their features and from the ornaments of the person in the foreground. This is understandable, for we have seen foreigners painted elsewhere in Ajanta, for example, a scene from a royal Harem [Kbl 108] on the ceiling of this cave and also on the ceilings of Cave 2, a scene called, Foreigners drinking [ASI 043]. Further Ajanta lay close to the trade route and among the benefactors and pilgrims would have been many from foreign countries.

Considering the style of execution, this painting definitely belongs to the period of decline. The drawing, colouring and the finish look clumsy. The hands and palms are no not shapely. The magic wrought by the Ajanta artist by the competent use of thick and thin lines producing soft curves is totally missing here. The lines are stiff and lifeless.

But Ajanta artist is enigmatic. The artistic quality of the man in the foreground commented on above is really not quite true for the two persons painted above him, nor to the expressive dark complexioned man to his left.

Scholars consider the Two Figures of the Buddhas [ASI 085] in Cave 10 to belong to the same decadent period.

Paintings on the Ceiling

That the Ajanta artist has not painted religious themes on the ceilings was mentioned elsewhere.. This perhaps gave him ample opportunity for his creative abilities. He gave expressions to his fantasies and rich imagery. In this cave, the ceiling, and some of the pillars, contain some exquisite animal and geometric motifs. The ceiling of the ante-chamber is embellished with a large a circular lotus-motif. The mail hall and elsewhere the decorations consist mostly of a number of rectangular panels filled with decorative motifs framed by smaller squares or rectangles with various decorative patterns. These decorative elements are of fruit and floral forms, animals, mythical forms, cherubs and human beings. Some of these motifs are repeated a number of times so that the effect is that of an enormous printed textile spread over the sanctuary. A selection from these individual motifs is given below.

Fighting Bulls [Mjt 007], painted on the capital of the second pillar in the left-hand corner of the hall, is one of the most forceful and vigorous drawings in Ajanta. The two bulls convey a tense fury, and this effect is enhanced by the delineation of their bodies in identical postures; their heads are lowered, muscles contracted, tails raised, and the curves of their necks and humps clearly delineated. We know of the same subject depicted in the Bhaja Cave, and one thousand years later a representation of two bulls engaged in combat painted in Mahal-i-Khas in Fatehpur Sikri, but neither of these paintings reaches the power of pictorial expression attained at Ajanta. To appreciate the artist’s confident draftsmanship a line sketch of this painting, Fighting Bulls (Line ?) is included. The placid Resting Bulls [Kbl 174] painted on the ceiling of Cave 17 is a study in contrast to these furious bulls.

A similar expression can be seen in the Charging Bull [ASI 030]. Ajanta artists have employed a number of techniques to give a feeling of depth. Showing farther objects in diminished size called foreshortening, is one such technique. This painting is a splendid example of how conversant the artist was with this technique. A totally different approach is the use of vartana (shading) techniques. One of them is called patraja wherein the depth is brought out by drawing lines of suitable thickness to follow the contours of the object. We can see this technique used in this painting, and in the painting of an Elephant [ASI 028] on the ceiling of this cave. Both these paintings illustrate the skill of the artist in composing animals spaced in flowers and plants.

Another repetitive motif was painting mythological creatures like the Mythical Birds [ASI 005]. The colour scheme is most pleasing.

The artist had ample scope for mixing what is real with what is totally imaginary. He found uninhibited space to record his sense of humour on the ceilings. For example the Cherubs in Playful Mood [Kbl 114B] is one of the many such cherub pairs we find on the ceiling. Clown [ASI 008] is an interesting study. The dwarf, cradled amongst flowers, uses the creeper as a swing, but the artist did not consider it inappropriate for a slender vine to support a man.

But it is the bacchanalian scene Royal Harem [Kbl 108] which takes the cake for its incongruity in a religious shrine. The characters are obviously from a foreign country. From their attire it may be deduced that they are from the Northwest of India. Some identify the main characters with the Persian emperor Khausrau and his queen Sirin.

The effect of such an aesthetically pleasing way of filling up the space with idyllic colour designs can be seen in Squares and Rectangles - 1 [ASI 025]. On the top two corners we have lotuses. The central square of the top row and the left corner of the bottom row are fruits that are all still life portraitures. The charging bull at the lower right corner is well animated. The central rectangle is a masterly composition. The motifs filling the space are a goose-like mythical bird, a parrot with a happy cherub at the centre, all in great harmony with the surrounding flowers.

Cave Two
Vihaara · 5th–6th century AD

Cave Two

This vihaara, although slightly smaller, has a similar plan to that of Cave 1. The verandah is about 2.5 meters wide and has a length of about 14 meters. The facades of the chapels at each end of the verandah are carved with figures of the naaga kings (serpent-kings) and their attendants, the portly gana-s (demigods). A richly carved doorway flanked by a decorated window on either side, leads to the main congregation hall. The congregation hall is about 14.5 meters square and the roof is supported by twelve massive, elaborately carved pillars. In the View of the Interior of Cave 2 [Kbl 019], we can see the pillars having very minute carvings and painted all over. In many pillars the paintings on the bases are still discernable as may be seen in the View of the Main Hall [Nou 040]. Rich tapestry of the paintings on the ceiling may be seen both the views. There are ten cells off four corridors. At the rear of the hall there is an ante-chamber leading to the shrine.

Main Shrine [Nou 020]: The entrances to the ante-chamber and to the shrine are richly carved. On the frames are sculpted a number of mithuna-figures, and the lower-most figures on both the sides are carved majestic gate-keeping yaksha-s. Towards the ceiling are images of the river goddesses, Ganga and Yamuna. The shrine contains a large figure of the Buddha in the dharma chakra pravartana mudra (turning-the-Wheel-of-Religion-attitude). He is flanked by two figures of Bodhi-sattva acting as chaamara-bearers. The painting on the ceiling of this shrine, Squares and Circle - 3 [ASI 048], is in a better state of preservation. On the ceiling of the ante-chamber we have in the ceiling painting Squares and Circles - 6 [Kbl 139B] whose motif is very interesting.

On either side of the ante-chamber is a chapel, the entrances to which are adorned with delicately-carved pillars. The chapel on the right is the Hariti shrine and is important because of its sculpture.

Hariti Shrine [Nou 035]: The Hariti shrine is the one to the right of the ante-chamber. The story of the conversion of the cannibal Hariti into the guardian-deity of the born infants is represented in a synoptic manner. Hariti and her consort Panchika are seated side by side, with the chaamara-bearers on either side, and a woman with a bird perched on her left hand and a flower in her right hand, between them. Two incidents of the former life of the Hariti are carved in the background, one each on either side above the whisk-bearers. On the right she is aggressively arguing with the Buddha to restore her child purposefully concealed by the Master. In the other she listens attentively to the words of wisdom of the Master, who advises her to give up cannibalism and to take up the protection of the children of rajagriha-s.

On the pedestal is an interesting scene of a classroom revealing the Ajanta artist's sense of humour. Starting from the right we find three sensitive boys engrossed in writing before a teacher holding a cudgel. The next two boys are boxing. And lastly, a group of five boys is goading two rams to fight.

The cave has the best ceiling paintings that have survived. On the ceiling of this shrine we find a beautiful ceiling decoration.

Paintings: The cave contains some very important paintings. The paintings in the ‘Hariti’ shrine are a testimony to the highest classical taste of Ajanta. The narration of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka belong to the baroque period. Finally, the paintings on the ceiling are the best in the whole Ajanta complex.

Among the important surviving paintings are:

Gate in King Brahma-datta’s Palace [Mjt 097], a scene from the Mahaa-hamsa Jaataka of Cave 2, painted on the left wall of the front verandah, is an example of normal perspective.

Nativity of the Buddha, many scenes of which somehow survived, is painted on the left wall. The narration starts with the Future Buddha in Tushita Heaven [Kbl 120], wherein we find the gods request Him to be born as the Buddha. Among the important surviving scenes of this story are, the King listens to the Interpretation of the Dream [Kbl 122T], Queen Mahaa-maaya musing [Kbl 122B] about the astrologer’s interpretation, and, finally, the Birth of the Buddha [Kbl 123].

Ajanta classicism at best can be enjoyed in the painting called Votaries with Offerings [Kbl 126] found on the walls of the Hariti shrine. Belonging to the same period and painted on the same Hariti shrine are the compositions Children playing with Hen [Kbl 127] and Child trying to snatch a Toy [ASI 46].

On the left-hand wall of the ante-chamber of the shrine is painted, Thousand Buddhas [Kbl 118], a scene from the Miracle of Shraavasti of Cave 2. The painting belongs to the period of artistic decline at Ajanta.

On the other hand, the representation of the Images of the Buddhas [Nou 133] is painted on the rear wall of the cave and is artistically superior to the one earlier described.

On the wall of the right corridor are painted a number of scenes from the Vidhura-pandita Jaataka. The scenes are Irandati meets Punyaka [Kbl 130], King Varuna’s Palace Scene [Kbl 131], a compact composition depicting Some Episodes from Vidhura-pandita Jaataka [Kbl 129]. These episodes are Game of Dice [Kbl 132], Vidhura-pandita counsels Dhananjaya [Kbl 133], Vidhura-pandita gives Farewell Sermon [Kbl 134], Queen Vimala and Princess Irandati [Kbl 137] and Vidhura-pandita in Naga Kingdom [Kbl 136]. The last painting is Departure of Vidhura-pandita [Nou 137].

Ceiling Paintings: This cave abounds in decorative designs painted on pillars, pilasters, ceilings, and for that matter, on every available surface. The motifs adopted for decoration are fruits (mangoes, pineapples, pears, grapes, berries, lichies, etc), flowers (lotus, waterlilies, champaka, etc), birds (geese, ducks, cranes, peacocks, parrots, etc), fantastic and sportive figures (dwarfs, buffoons, jugglers, foreigners in exotic garments, etc) and pure geometric patterns. For the geometric patterns, the most popular composition being a square containing a number of concentric circular bands around a central lotus-design, with the four corners - called spandrels - embellished with mithuna-s (couples), birds or mythical beings. The circular bands, in turn, are filled with wreaths of flowers. The best preserved of the ceiling paintings are in the sanctum.

Squares and Circle - 3 [ASI 048] has flying cherubs in the spandrels, while mithuna-s are painted in the spandrels of Squares and Circles - 7 [Kbl 139T] and Detail of Squares and Circles - 7 [Kbl 140]. In the ceiling painting Squares and Circles - 6 [Kbl 139B] the row of geese in a ring is a superb rendering.

As in Cave 1, the ceiling decoration consists of filling up the space with squares and rectangle. Belonging to this mode of decoration is Squares and Rectangles - 2 [ASI 047].

The ceiling also contain some scenes in a lighter vein, like, the Foreigners drinking [ASI 043].

Mahaa-hamsa Jaataka

Cave 2, 5th Century AD

There are four scenes depicted in Mahaa-hamsa Jaataka of Cave 2. The story is painted on the left wall of the front verandah. The lotus scenes are on either side of the cell door. The palace scene is above the cell door.

That the Ajanta artist was conversant with both normal perspective and reverse perspective was mentioned elsewhere. It was also pointed out that the reverse perspective was employed in the scene Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091]. We shall come across the reverse perspective in the Game of Dice [Kbl 132], a scene from Vidhura-pandita Jaataka painted in this cave.

In this panel, Gate in King Brahma-datta’s Palace [Mjt 097], the artist has employed the normal perspective. The colouring and the shading bring out clearly the form, shape and geometry of the gate and its environs. Anther example of the use of normal perspective is the panel Monastery [Kbl 227R], a scene from Shibi Jaataka of Cave 17.

Nativity of the Buddha

Cave 2, 5th Century, AD

Several scenes depicting the Nativity of the Buddha have been painted along the left wall of the hall above the lintel and also along the sides of the first and second cell doors.

The Story: First we shall follow the story of the birth of the Buddha as depicted in this cave. The Future Buddha was living in Tushita heaven. All the gods from the 'thousand worlds' requested Him to be born as the Buddha. The Great Being consented and informed them of the time, place and the family for his final birth. Thus during the mid-summer festival Queen Mahaa-maaya, queen of King Suddhodana of Kapila-vastu, had a dream. In this dream she saw a white elephant entering her womb through her right side. She related this dream to her husband, who consulted the revered Brahmins to interpret the dream. They said that the queen had conceived a male child, who would either become a Universal Monarch or become the Buddha. For confinement Queen Mahaa-maaya proceeded to the residence of her parents. Midway, in the beautiful grove of shaala trees, called Lumbini Grove, she stood holding the branch of a shaala tree and gave birth to a son. Brahma, Indra and other gods descended on to the grove to pay their respects to the newborn. 'The Baby looked around in all directions, and strode forward seven paces, followed by Mahaa-Brahma, holding over him a white umbrella'.

Some of the painted scenes are damaged by fire. The dream of Queen Mahaa-maaya is faintly visible. The scenes of a Brahmin interpreting the dream, the Queen musing about the interpretation and finally, the birth itself are more clearly discernible.

The first scene, Future Buddha in Tushita Heaven [Kbl 120], is enacted in the Tushita heaven, and painted above the second cell-door. Seated in pralamba (sitting-with-legs-hanging) pose hands striking dharma chakra pravartana mudra (teaching-attitude), He is surrounded by other deities and is making the announcement that He will be descending to the earth to be born to Queen Mahaa-maaya. The portrayal of the Master is not one of the best in Ajanta. The direct frontal pose without any inclination of the head is a rare feature in Ajanta art. Further the treatment of the whole composition lacks imagination and creativity. On the other hand the other scenes in the narrative are nor marred by this deficiency.

The next scene in which Queen Mahaa-maaya is dreaming is badly damaged, and is barely discernable. Hence we are constrained to move on to the next scene.

This scene in which the King listens to the Interpretation of the Dream [Kbl 122T] by a Brahmin called Asita, is painted between the front pilaster and the third cell door. (Refer also to the sketch King listens to the Astrologer – Line 15)

The royal couple, Suddhodana and Mahaa-maaya are seated on two thrones and Asita, the astrologer, squats in front of them having made a loop with his fingers. His long twisted moustache is prominent. He is telling them that the queen has conceived a son destined to become either a universal monarch or a universal teacher. There are three female attendants at the back of the throne, the one on the extreme left being a chaawri-bearer. She looks at the queen. The other two are looking in different directions. There are three more female attendants further down. All these nine figures are enclosed in a framework formed by the vertical pillars, the foreground and the architrave on the top.

Moving further right, beyond a pillar used by the artist to start a new scene, we find the figure of Queen Mahaa-maaya musing [Kbl 122B] over the prediction. (This scene is contained in the line sketch.) Here she stands, leaning against a pillar with her left leg touching it. We now see her musing over the exciting augury. That she is baffled is evident from her expression and the loop made by her fingers. Two more attendants look at her with anxiety written on their faces, but the queen appears to be unaware of their presence. In drawing the figure of the queen the artist is chiefly concerned with giving expression to her serenity mingled with a bashful smile expected of a would-be mother, and her anxiety for the safety of the life taking shape within her. Nevertheless, he drew in details the rich jewellery, elaborate coiffure and floral decoration over the chignon. The lady is very scantily dressed, particularly in contrast to the two attendants in this scene. This feature of being ‘dressed in nakedness’ is a common, but intriguing feature at Ajanta. Her legs resemble the trunk of banana-tree, following the established canons of the time.

The Birth of the Buddha [Kbl 123], is the next scene, painted on the space above the third and fourth cell-doors. The holy birth takes place in the Lumbini Grove. We see Queen Mahaa-maaya standing holding on to a branch of a shaala tree. The pose of the queen is very similar to that in the previous scene. In front of the queen are Mahaa-Brahma who holds a white umbrella. To his right is Indra respectfully holding the baby. By the side stands a chaawri-bearer showing great excitement. There are a few more figures in the group. To heighten the effect, the artist has taken liberties to incorporate gateways, buildings, compounds, etc, though these things are hard to imagine in a grove situated along a road.

The next scene in which the Holy Baby strides forward seven paces is badly damaged.

Votaries with Offerings

Cave 2, 4th century, AD

The classical style of Ajanta painting at its finest is undoubtedly represented by two superb panel, Votaries with Offerings, on the left hand wall of the shrine of Hariti, the angel who protects children. The admirable composition of these two panels of identical size is constructed around the five main figures in such a way that they occupy a major portion of the wall; the other figures are about half the size and occupy the four corners.

The scene set is a landscape of rocks and gardens with their swaying creepers and banana trees. This creates an atmosphere of carefree tranquillity, as the lovely women proceed with gentle swaying movements towards the shrine. Unobtrusive modelling and calm linear harmony, combined with a restrained use of red tints for the women, is balanced against the greens of the banana leaves.

The figures are drawn halfway between strict profile and complete frontality. The faces are similarly sketched, and in one or two cases the woman's head is elegantly turned towards her companion. There is a remarkable ease in the drawing of arms and hands; the arms bend at the elbows, mostly at right angles, or hang softly caressing the slightly curved hips. The exquisite, gently bending attitudes of the votaries as they walk along in the open air with uncovered breasts wearing simple attire and very few ornaments bear some similarity with the Hinaayana painting Raja with his Retinue [Mjt 106] in Cave 10.

But this painting represents an entirely new concept of Ajanta colour and design. The elongation of the suave and elegant figures, which began at Amaravati, situated near Vijayawada in Andhra Pradesh, where there are excellent Buddhist sculptures belonging to the 2nd century, AD, is completed in Ajanta with unmatched perfection. The curvature of the figures is supple, flexible and simple, yet not exaggerated, and there are none of those excessive gesticulations, that are typical of the later baroque period of the 6th century.

Another point to be noticed in the evolution of style is the shading technique, called vartana, to bring out depth against the uniform tonal level maintained in the earlier Hinaayana painting.

Two other compositions that arrest our attention painted in the same shrine, and that belong to the same period, are Children playing with Hen [Kbl 127] and Child trying to snatch a Toy [ASI 46]. Both these scenes are examples of the way the Ajanta artist handled with great interest, incidents from day to day life of common people..

These characters painted on the two walls in the Hariti Shrine together with the Simhala Avadaana painting in Cave 17 and the beautiful painting Dying Princess [ASI 003] in Cave 16, certainly represent the classical peak of Ajanta painting.

Thousand Buddhas

Miracle of Shraavasti

Cave 2, Late 6th century, AD

There are eight places that are considered the most sacred places in the Buddhist faith. They are referred to as attha-mahaa-thaanaani. These places derive their holiness because of their intimate connection with major events in the life of the Master. Of these four are related to the important events in the life of the Buddha: Lumbini, where Siddhartha was born, Gaya, where He attained Enlightenment; Sarnath, where he preached the First Sermon, and Kushinagara, where he obtained nirvaana.

The remaining four are the locations where He is believed to have performed miracles. One of these is the theme of this mural. In this Lord Buddha displays His miraculous powers at Shraavasti, by multiplying himself a thousand-fold to the bewilderment of His critics. This miracle is painted elsewhere in Ajanta as well.

The other miracles are: His ascending to the Heaven of Thirty-three Gods and preaching the Dharma is the theme of a mural called Buddha preaching to the Congregation [nou 197] and the Miracle at Raja-griha of the subjugation of a mad elephant, Nalagiri, both painted in Cave 17. The miracle of a number of monkeys offering Him honey at Vaishaali is not to be found in Ajanta.

Shraavasti was the capital of the ancient kingdom of Koshala and is identified with the present day villages Saheth and Maheth the former in the district of Gonda and the later in the district of Bahraich, both in the state of Uttar Pradesh.

There are excellent portrayals of the compassionate Buddha in Ajanta. But, none of the painters reached the mark of high artistry to be seen in this painting. Conventional norms and formulae, in certain instances, have had preferences over artistic empathy. This painting demonstrates that conventionalism and mechanical representation rather than artistry inspired the scene.

The scene on the right-hand wall of the ante-chamber, consisting of monotonously-reproduced figures of the Buddha in various mudra-s (hand-gestures), represents the Miracle of Shraavasti. Rows of seated Buddhas are also painted on the right and left walls of the shrine. These two compositions belong to different styles.

This miracle performed at Shraavasti became popular and has been painted in a number of caves in Ajanta. In this rows of Buddha-figures, one above the other, came to be painted. The repetitive theme in this Miracle of Shraavasti of Cave 2 reflects the artistic tendency of the age. The paintings lack the vigour, imagination and delicacy of the earlier ones. A fragmentary painted record on the back wall of the antechamber to the right of the shrine-door in Cave 2 may record the donation as that of the Thousand Buddhas ([Kbl 118]. This record is attributed to the 6th century, AD.

Trying to impart some artistic interest to the panel they took care to vary the Buddha's postures and mudra-s. The painters also introduced an element of colourfulness by painting the robes of the Buddha in different colours ranging from white, grey, brown and green to red.

This painting may be considered a part the continuous decline in standards at Ajanta. Also belonging to this period is the painting of Two Figures of The Buddha [ASI 085] in Cave 17. These are painted one above the other, each enclosed in a cubicle.

On the other hand, the Images of the Buddha [Nou 133], painted on the right and left walls of the shrine, is of considerable sophistication. This composition avoids the monotony of repetitive images of the Buddha witnessed in the previous depiction. This has been done by varying slightly the postures, particularly by turning some of the faces slightly, alternating the mudra-s and finally by the expert use of ujjotana (adding-highlights) technique and succeeded in creating a mysterious glow on the face the Master. To add to the effect the artist has provided the bolsters with a variety of designs on them. The same theme, Buddha baffles Critics [Kbl 23], of the Miracle of Shraavasti of Cave 6 is a superior composition, particularly the variation in both the size and the poses of the Master and also the refined portraiture of the worshippers shown below.

Vidhura-pandita Jaataka

Cave 2, 5th Century, AD

This jaataka tale is the story of Bodhi-sattva born as Vidhura-pandita who demonstrated his supreme wisdom as a minister in the court of King Dhananjaya. Several episodes from Vidhura-pandita Jaataka occupy a large portion of the right wall of the hall, above and between the second and third cell doors.

By the end of the 5th century AD, the classical upsurge of the Ajanta genius represented by the masterpieces in Caves 16 and 17 and the Hariti Shrine of Cave 2, began to give way to what has been called mannerism which later deteriorated into the baroque style. The latter style may be seen in the Mahaa-janaka Jaataka of Cave 1.

Passing from the classical to the manneristic mode, there were alterations in composition and in figure-drawing and decoration. The broad vision is progressively dissected into compartments so that the narrative is painted, not in an idyllic landscape, but in a maze of pillars of royal pavilions, palace windows, porches and balconies, city walls, shops and monasteries. Scenes became crowded with people, who are sophisticated, though they are not yet affected or effeminate. The men are still manly and the women feminine and they wear simple clothes and few ornaments.

For postures, expressions and movements characteristic of this period, it is difficult to match the episodes of the Vidhura-pandita Jaataka. Mention was made about the Ajanta artist's knowledge of perspectives, and his employing both the normal perspective and the reverse perspective. Certain scenes in the narration are depicted in the reverse perspective.

Since this is one of the surviving paintings where sufficient portions are discernible, let us first narrate the story before attempting to explain the painting.

The Jaataka: Bodhi-sattva was born in Indra-prastha and became a minister under King Dhananjaya belonging to the Kuru dynasty. He was universally admired for his wisdom. Once he delivered a sermon in the court of King Varuna, king of the naaga-s. Impressed with his discourse, the king presented him with a costly piece of jewellery. This was not to the liking of his queen, Vimala. She feigned illness and said she would die unless his heart was brought to her as a proof of his demise. The distraught king proclaimed that he would give his beautiful daughter Irandati in marriage to anyone who would perform this task. The king entreated his daughter to seek a brave husband. Once a yaksha general called Punyaka, while riding past in heaven on his magic horse, heard the sweet song of Irandati, and fell in love with her. He promised to get the heart of Vidhura-pandita. Punyaka was aware of King Dhananjaya’s weakness for the game of dice and enticed him to a game. In the ensuing game Punyaka won from the king Vidhura-pandita as a wager. On the way to the naaga world Punyaka made a number of unsuccessful attempts to kill Vidhura-pandita to get his heart. Finally, Vidhura-pandita volunteered to tell him the way to get his heart. Punyaka, taken aback at his generosity, brought Vidhura-pandita alive to the court of King Varuna. Both Varuna and his queen were enchanted by Vidhura-pandita’s wisdom and made him to stay as their guest for a few days before returning to the Kuru kingdom.

Now let us try to follow the story. We must remember that the painting sequence in Ajanta does not follow a chronological order. This we have seen in other panels as well.

The first three scenes are painted in the extreme right. The first two scenes are shown in Irandati meets Punyaka ( Kbl 130) and the third scene is King Varuna's Palace Scene [Kbl 131]. The following explanation is supported by a line sketch Punyaka’s Arrival [line 13] and the above two colour pictures.

In the centre of the line drawing, and on to the right of Irandati meets Punyaka ( Kbl 130), we find Princess Irandati on a Swing. This is one of the most beautiful compositions of Ajanta. The background is embellished with floral designs. The movement of the swing is aptly conveyed by the curves of the ropes and the streaming flags attached to the swing. The outstretched leg of the girl with toes brought together is a realistic rendering of a pendulum-like movement. According to the jaataka, while swinging carefree, Irandati was also singing in a sweet voice. The swing plays an important role in Indian cultural and social life, and has been extolled as romantic in Indian literature.

Punyaka, a yaksha general, who was flying on the back of a winged horse, hears her song and is attracted by it. Coming down he introduces himself. This scene is painted to the left of the previous scene. The separation of this composition into two scenes follows the Ajanta convention of placing a post or a pillar between two scenes. In this second scene she is standing and leaning against a post, talking to Punyaka. Obviously Punyaka has proposed marriage to the princess. Punyaka’s horse is by his right side.

Now the action shifts to the palace, depicted on the other side of the swing. This part is also shown in the line drawing Punyaka’s Arrival [Line 13]. In this King Varuna's Palace Scene [Kbl 131], we find the king seated along with his son on a slightly raised platform talking to his wife and his daughter on the proposal of marriage. The king and his son have serpent-hoods over their heads indicative of their naaga royalty. The son is in a contemplative mood. Irandati is talking as can be seen from her hand gestures. The rich hair-dresses of the characters and the textiles in this scene, particularly the cloth on the bolster, would provide us information for the study of the textile designs of the day.

Next we witness Queen Vimala and Princess Irandati having an intimate conversation regarding the marriage. This is painted above the third cell door to the right of the scene of Vidhura-pandita giving a Sermon, which is the last scene. The conversation takes place on a balcony. The princess looks coy and abashed, and the mother, we find from her gestures, is giving her some advice and, perhaps, assurance.

After the marriage Punyaka sets out to kill Vidhura-pandita and to bring back his heart. Punyaka knows King Dhananjaya's weakness for gambling.

Left to the second cell door and above it we see four main scenes. each enclosed in a pavilion, depicting Some Episodes from Vidhura-pandita Jaataka [Kbl 129]. Details of each of the scenes are also given and we shall discuss these scenes in a chronological order.

On the left side of the top left panel we have the scene called Game of Dice [Kbl 132]. The scenes are shown as line drawings in Game of Gambling [Line 11]. In this we see Punyaka enticing Dhananjaya to a game of dice and the game being played. On the left we see Punyaka showing a magnificent jewel as a wager to tempt King Dhananjaya to a game. The king is occupying a low seat; the cloth covering the seat is striped. He has made some gesture with his right hand whereas his left hand is resting on the left knee. Vidhura-pandita, sitting to the left of the king, is looking at Punyaka with concern. Below the king on his right is a door-keeper with a moustache and a long coat with tight sleeves. This was possibly the usual livery of doorkeepers, for we find the doorkeepers of Mahaa-hamsa Jaataka in the same cave, dressed similarly as also the one in Conversion of Nanda in Cave 1. In front of the doorkeeper, below the throne of the king there are two more figures, one male and the other female, the latter with the back to us. They are possibly personal attendants to the king. Both of them are in great animation.

To the right, King Dhananjaya and Punyaka are seen engaged in a game of dice. The king is seen on the left and Punyaka on the right with the game-board between them. The king is at the point of throwing the dice. Punyaka is serious and anxiously waiting for the dice to show up. The queen, painted below the king, is appealing to Punyaka, perhaps to stop the dangerous game.

The artist has adopted the technique of reverse perspective to draw the board of dice in minute detail showing clearly all the twenty-four divisions in two rows. The three dice are lying at the end of the king's outstretched hand. Such detail would not have been possible if normal perspective was followed. We have seen another example of this technique of reverse perspective in the building behind the dancer in Mahaa-janaka Jaataka.

The king loses the game, and, thus, his minister. In the next scene, Vidhura-pandita counsels Dhananjaya [Kbl 133], painted below the Game of Dice [Kbl 132], we see King Dhananjaya and his minister in conversation. The king rather hopes that the rules of the game will not allow him to wager his minister. But the truthful minister Vidhura-pandita counsels Dhananjaya that a minister is a legal wager, and thus he had now become the property of Punyaka. The scene has been somewhat damaged and hence the rest of the figures in this scene are not clearly seen.

Before leaving for the naaga-kingdom with his new master, Vidhura-pandita gives a Farewell Sermon [Kbl 134] in the court of King Dhananjaya. This scene is depicted to the right of the scene of Vidhura-pandita counselling his king. Between the two scenes is shown a balcony in which the queen is talking to a maid, perhaps telling her to request Vidhura-pandita to give a sermon before leaving. While giving the farewell sermon the minister is holding a lotus flower on his right hand symbolising his divinity. In this masterly composition, we see Vidhura-pandita as a personification of love and compassion. We have the king before him listening to the sermon in rapt attention. Behind Vidhura-pandita there are three graceful maidens with sorrowful faces at the imminent loss of such a saintly person.

Below this is a long panel depicting the Departure of Vidhura-pandita [Nou 137]. Similar in composition to the marching of Simhala in the Simhala Avadaana panel, the majestic movement of the horses in various hues, the stately gait of the caparisoned elephants, the variety of sthaana-s (body-postures) of the solders, their weapons etc are drawn with great skill.

According to the story, Punyaka takes the minister on his flying horse and flies towards the naaga-loka (serpent-kingdom). On the way he attempts to kill Vidhura-pandita to take out his heart, but fails. It is then that the compassionate Vidhura-pandita explains to him how he may be killed and his heart taken out. Ashamed of his proposed evil act Punyaka brings Vidhura-pandita alive to the naaga court.

The last scene is enacted in the kingdom of the naaga-s. This episode, painted to the right of the scene Game of Dice [Kbl 132], shows Vidhura-pandita in the Naga Kingdom [Kbl 136] preaching before an assemblage of naaga-s, headed by King Varuna himself. The major portion of the scene is given in the sketch, Vidhura-pandita in Naaga-loka [Line 14]. The teacher has, as usual, taken the central position and is practically the pivot of the composition. He is seen seated on a low stool, interlaced with tape. He has a tilak (decorative mark) on the forehead.

In front of the revered guest is the naaga-king with folded hands bowing his head in supplication. The halo of five serpents makes his identification easier. Besides the king we see two ladies, evidently Queen Vimala and Princess Irandati, listening to the sermon. They do not have their hoods now. Punyaka's attention is not on the discourse; he seems to be eyeing his sweetheart, Irandati.

The balcony scene to the right of this has already been described as Queen Vimala and Princess Irandati [Kbl 137]. Below this balcony is a window through which we see a couple. Some identify them as Vidhura-pandita, holding a lotus in his hand, and Queen Vimala.

After such a long description we may go back to the discussion on the art style of this narration. The reason for this extended discussion is that the mural exhibits characteristics of both the classical and the later baroque periods. Interestingly certain aspects of the composition resemble the pre-classical style. Thus in terms of art style this is really unique.

The facial features are similar to those in the pre-Christian era painting of Cave 10, like the Raja with his Retinue [Mjt 106]. The golden-brown complexion, round faces, full red lips, short, but pointed noses are considered to be Southern and non-Aryan. So also the veil over the women's head and the elaborate turbans on the heads of the men-folk. These are found in some measure in this painting. There are no exaggerations like bulging body-curves and elongated eyes. Men and women wear simple dresses and are bare above the waist.

On the other hand the spatial arrangement is not classical, at least in some scenes like the scene in which Punyaka entices Dhananjaya. That the narration takes place 'in a maze of pillars of royal pavilions, palace windows, porches and balconies' betrays its deviation from the classical idyllic landscape. Though a variety of body-positions are shown there is certain amount of monotony. This is seen in the scene, Punyaka enticing Dhananjaya in the panel, Game of Dice [Kbl 132] and in the panel Vidhura-pandita gives a Farewell Sermon [Kbl 134], the two adjacent panels.

Ceiling Paintings

Every available space on the ceilings and on the pillars has been painted. This cave contains the best decorations on the ceilings. The compositions of these ceiling paintings are, like in the other caves, of two major types. One is a square containing a number of concentric circular bands around a central lotus-design, with the four corners - called spandrels - embellished with mithuna-s (couples), birds or mythical beings. The circular bands, in turn, are filled with wreath of flowers. The best preserved of the ceiling paintings are in the sanctum. This kind is referred to as ‘Squares and Circles’ in the present discussion.

The other is filling up the space with squares and rectangles, in which are painted various motifs. These are being referred to as ‘Squares and Rectangles’ in the discussion.

Fruits, birds, animals and pure geometric patterns are the motifs used in the above. The fruits, which have been depicted, are fruits, mangoes, pineapples, pears, grapes, berries, lichies, etc. We have birds like, geese, ducks, cranes, peacocks, parrots, etc. There are also mythical animals of a wide variety. In a lighter vein we have sportive figures, like, dwarfs, buffoons, jugglers, foreigners in exotic garments, etc. And, finally, there are pure geometric patterns.

Apart from the colour harmony and delicacy of the designs, even a casual observer can not miss the movement and plasticity of forms.

The painting on the ceiling of the main shrine, Squares and Circle - 3 [ASI 048], is in a better state of preservation. The central circle contains a large, white and serene lotus, symbolising purity. The ring around it is a wreath. The one next is of some geometrical pattern. Filling up the spandrel are four flying gandharva-s, which arrest our attention. Bare-bodied and clad only in loincloths, these gandharva-s are in a playful mood. Their flight is unmistakable. The cumulous cloud behind and the fluttering ends of their garments point to this. Two of them are wearing socks. Binduja is a technique of vartana, a family of shading techniques adopted by the Ajanta artist to effect an illusion of depth and relief. This method, also known as the ‘dot-and-stipple-method’, uses a variation in the concentration of dots to produce the desired effect. This can be seen on the belly, chin and on the arms of the flying gandharva-s, bringing out the contours of their body.

On the ceiling of the ante-chamber we have in Squares and Circles - 6 [Kbl 139B] a portraiture of a row of geese in a ring. It is a superb rendering. About this Yazdani comments: “Some (of these ducks) have their necks sharply curved, as if they were in an angry mood, some have stretched their necks forward to show their determination to attack, some have bent them low to preen their feathers in an attitude of languor, some have turned their heads gracefully to one side to look amorously at their mates, and some have turned them with a romantic curve to show their feeling of ecstasy. The fine brush work of the artist in the delineation of the features of the birds also deserve a close study. The subject, both with regard to the ingenuity of design and perfection of technique plainly shows the skill of the Ajanta artist in decorative art.” But Narayan Sanyal points out one feature not mentioned by the others. The number of geese in the composition is twenty-three, an inconvenient prime number, precluding the possibility of using a stencil!

Painted outside the antechamber is another beautiful Squares and Circles - 7 [Kbl 139T]. A line drawing of this is given as A Ceiling Painting of Cave 2 (Line ?). Exquisite geometrical and floral patters fill the circular bands. Mithuna-s are painted in the spandrels. In spite of the restricted space the couples are not cramped as can be seen in the Detail of Squares and Circles - 7 [Kbl 140].

As in Cave 1, the ceiling decoration consists of filling up the space with squares and rectangles. Belonging to this mode of decoration is Squares and Rectangles - 2 [ASI 047], a part of the ceiling painting in the rear aisle. The geese, the mythical birds and the floral patterns are remarkable. Skilful shading has given the pure geometrical patterns a three dimensional effect.

The ceiling also contain some scenes in a lighter vein. Two Foreigners drinking [ASI 043] is a detail from the painting on the ceiling of the verandah. From their dress, they look like foreigners. But their dress is very interesting – head-dress, scarves, tunics and socks. The intriguing aspect of this detail as well as similar paintings in Ajanta is the inappropriateness of such drinking scenes in a religious shrine.

Cave Six
Vihaara · Two-storeyed · 5th–6th century AD

Cave Six

This cave is the only two-storied cave in Ajanta, whereas in Ellora one may find quite a few. This cave belongs to the 5th-6th centuries AD. The doorway is lavishly carved and the lower floor main hall has sixteen simple pillars without capitals. The shrine approached through an ante-chamber houses an image of the Buddha sitting on a lion throne.

To the right of the entrance is the staircase leading to the upper floor. The main hall of the upper floor has twelve pillars and two chapels on either side. An impressive figure of the Buddha is carved in the shrine approachable through an ante-chamber. The deity is attended by flying apsara-s and attendants.

Like all the other caves the walls of the ground and upper floors must have been painted, of which very little remains.

The following are some of the surviving paintings.

Bhikshu with Lotus [Kbl 024], painted near the feet of the Buddha in the Niche [Kbl 022], on the left wall of the ante-chamber of the first floor, is an excellent portraiture.

The miracle performed by the Buddha at Shraavasti to baffle his critics is depicted in this cave also. It may be seen on the right-hand wall of the chapel in the front corridor of the upper storey and a part is shown in Buddha baffles Critics [Kbl 023].

Bhikshu with Lotus

Cave 6, Late 5th century AD

Bikshu with Lotus [Kbl 024], painted near the feet of the Buddha in the Niche [Kbl 022], on the left wall of the ante-chamber of the first floor, is an excellent portraiture.

Here is an example of proper balance between sculpture and painting, a special feature of Indian art. On each sidewall of the ante-chamber in the first floor are elaborately sculpted images of the Buddha. The painting on all of them had vanished as in the other sculptures in Ajanta. But traces of the same can be noticed here and there. The main image is the Buddha in the Niche [Kbl 022]. At His feet is painted the Bhikshu with Lotus [Kbl 024].

The bhikshu is kneeling before the image of the Buddha. On the left-hand he is holding aloft a bunch of three lotuses and a metal incense burner on his right-hand. The three lotuses perhaps signify the ‘three jewels’ (tri-ratna), the Buddha, the Order and the Law. The kneeling posture and the expression on his face amply portrays devotion, dedication and humility.

The choice of this painting is also to show the varying techniques the Ajanta artist employed. The lines, thick or thin based on the pressure on the brush, depends on the theme and placement. Even the colour of the lines has significance. This figure is drawn in a firm black outline and the artist has employed only two tones for the figure, apart from the background. Devotee with Lotus [Mjt 146] is a similar painting from Cave 2, belonging to the same period.

Buddha baffles Critics

Miracle of Shraavasti

Cave 6, Late 5th century AD

The miracle performed by the Buddha at Shraavasti to baffle his critics is depicted in this cave also. It may be seen on the right-hand wall of the chapel in the front corridor of the upper storey and a part is shown in Buddha baffles Critics [Kbl 023].

A detailed discussion on the miracle and its importance in the Mahaayana sect of Buddhism may be found with reference to Thousand Buddhas ([Kbl 118] of Cave 2.

In this panel is shown the Buddha in the teaching attitude. Around Him are painted a number of Buddha figures. Some are in the padmaasana posture of sitting, on a double lotus seat, like the central figure. Some of the Buddha figures are painted in the standing fashion. The poses of the Buddha figures as well as the devotees shown on the bottom to the right of the panel are drawn with a self-assured mastery of sweeping lines. The pose of the nun, one of the devotees shown, is unique, and has no parallel in Ajanta. The right-hand strikes a pose anticipating the pose of classical dancers of the later periods.

Cave Nine
Chaitya · 1st century BC

Cave Nine

This chaitya (temple) is one of the earliest caves to be excavated and is assigned to the 1st century, BC. The plan of this temple is rectangular, and it has an architectural dignity all its own. It has twenty-one octagonal pillars that are about 3 meters high. The interior dimensions of the cave are 13.5 meters long, 7 metres wide and 7 meters high. The stoopa carved at the end of the hall has a dome one metre high above a cylindrical base and is crowned by a square capital representing the reliquary, a receptacle for relics. The chaitya has a beautifully decorated facade with a berm-railing, windows and lattice frame. The large figures of the Buddha carved along the sides of the cave are later additions in 5th-6th centuries.

The chaitya contains paintings of different periods. Some of these belong to the 1st century BC, while others are assigned to a period as late as the 4th-5th centuries, AD.

A Group of Votaries approaching a Stoopa (Line ?) painted on the left wall is a representative of the era of pre-Christian art.

A painting of a Kneeling Ascetic [Kbl 041] is included as an example of the 5th century AD painting and the variety of hairstyles painted in Ajanta.

A Group of Votaries approaching a Stoopa

Cave 9, 1st century BC

Most of the paintings from the earliest periods have, unfortunately, perished. Also many of the paintings copied by Griffiths in the last century have been consumed by fire. An extant sketch, A Group of Votaries approaching a Stoopa (Line ?) is reproduced to give an idea of the lost treasure.

In the figure is a group of sixteen votaries who are approaching the door of a stoopa. The variety of their postures and gestures and their hairstyles intertwined with strips of cloth are remarkable. The men's headgear, consisting of a topknot with elaborately plaited terminations protruding over the forehead, and their decorative waistbands are also extremely interesting. This very closely resembles the sculptures of Sanchi and Bharhut, both belonging to the same period as this cave. The grouping of the figures is natural, and is typical of the painting of this period.

On the right, the scene depicts the stoopa, which clearly shows the typical architecture of the 1st century BC. The stoopa is enclosed by four walls, which have two gates. The gate on the left side has a barrel-shaped roof with timber rafters, like those of the contemporary caves at Karle and Bedsa, both in the state of Maharashtra. The gateway on the right side is in the style of the Sanchi gates, and this painting is therefore thought by some to be contemporaneous. In the middle stands the stoopa under a number of umbrellas, which are Buddhist emblems of dignity and sanctity. Outside the enclosure can be seen the devotees and a banyan tree.

Kneeling Ascetic

Cave 9, 5th century AD

From the paintings of Ajanta one can get a fairly good account of the life style of people of the period. Kneeling Ascetic [Kbl 041] is included as an example of the style of painting during the 5th century AD painting and the variety of hairstyles painted in Ajanta.

This is a painting done over the pre-Christian era painting. It depicts an ascetic at his prayers. The posture of kneeling, his hands of prayer and, finally, the matted hair, all these are very similar to present day practices.

Cave Ten
Chaitya · 2nd century BC — The Oldest Cave

Cave Ten

On the strength of an inscription this chaitya (Sanctuary) is considered to be the oldest of the caves and has been assigned to the first half of the 2nd century BC.

It has an impressive, lofty ceiling and a spacious nave. Altogether it is about 29 meters long, 12 meters wide and 11 meters high. Thirty-nine pillars, which divide the aisles from the nave, support an entablature that rests on the vault of the barrel-shaped roof. The pillars are 12 meters high and lean inward in imitation of the posts of the ancient bamboo structures, to support the weight of the curvilinear roofs made of wooden rafters and crossbeams.

The chaitya, like Cave 9, contains paintings of different periods. On the strength of two inscriptions the oldest are thought to be roughly contemporaneous with the excavation of the cave in the 2nd century BC.

The paintings in this cave are distinguished by the fact that even though the two groups of surviving pieces are separated by a period of nearly six hundred years, both groups were created at period of stylistic transition. In this cave two changes in styles can be noticed. The earlier paintings belonged to the pre-Christian era, and the change from the style of this period to the classical phase is the first one. The 5th century paintings such as Gautama, the Buddha and The Buddha and the One-eyed Monk, painted on the sixth pillar on the right-hand side of the cave, were created in the course of transition from the classical phase to the baroque phase. The Gandhara influence can also be seen in these compositions.

We are fortunate that there are traces of surviving paintings, Raja with his Retinue [Mjt 106] and the accompanying Dancers and Musicians [Mjt 107], to give us an idea of the pre-Christian era paintings at Ajanta. This painting is important in the history of Indian painting, for this is the earliest survivor. We have also some excellent line sketches of Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 10 (Line ?) and Shyama Jaataka. Both these examples are impressive for their dynamic modelling and story-telling. The composition, Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 10 (Line ?), is important for another reason. The same jaataka is narrated in the 5th century AD in Cave 17, and this offers a comparative study of styles separated by six centuries.

A painting, Two Figures of the Buddha [ASI 085], is included to as an example of the painting belonging to the period of artistic decline at Ajanta.

Raja with his Retinue

Cave 10, 2nd or 1st century BC

This painting Raja with his Retinue [Mjt 106] is one of the rare surviving masterpieces of the pre-Christian era. A true understanding of the evolution in style that took place in the subsequent period is not possible without making some reference to it. The painting enshrines the pictorial manner as well as the social mood of the time. It is painted on the rear side of the left aisle of this chaitya. A part of the panel is given as a sketch called Raja and his Entourage (Line ?).

This painting, like those belonging to the pre-Christian era paintings of Ajanta, is painted longitudinally in narrow horizontal panels, maintaining the same tonal level. The scenes unfolding on a ribbon-like space on the walls are not subdivided as in the later period except for an occasional tree, or a building or a group of rocks, which vaguely defines the end of one scene and the beginning of another.

In this painting the Raja, accompanied by ten ladies and a child, is seen approaching the Bodhi tree decorated with banners. The women, nearly naked from the waist upward, are wearing simple ornaments comprising earrings, necklaces, armlets and bangles. Especially notable are the ivory bangles and those made of conch-shell, which cover almost the entire forearm. These are still in fashion among the Lambadi tribes in the Deccan. The women's hairstyles vary from the highly elaborate to the quite simple. On their foreheads a few ladies sport the tilak, circular colour-marks, such as Indian women still wear today. Another notable feature of the dress of this period is the veils over the women's heads. In the later period these are conspicuously absent, perhaps because of the influence of the North on the Deccan in the 3rd century, AD after the disappearance of the Andhra rule and the establishment of the Vakataka-s.

Their round faces with small bright eyes, short pointed noses, narrow mouths and full lips are southern features. At Ajanta the darker skins and non-Aryan features completely disappeared for quite some time, and reappeared possibly in the second half of the 5th century AD.

The Raja has a band of jewelry round his topknot of hair, which is arranged like the hood of a serpent. This was emblematic of his mythical descent from a serpent-king.

On the other side of the Bodhi tree is painted a group of women Dancers and Musicians [Mjt 107].

Dancers and Musicians

Cave 10, 2nd or 1st century, BC

In the entourage are a group of Dancers and Musicians [Mjt 107], painted to the right of the panel Raja with his Retinue [Mjt 106]

Bare to the waist, these women appear to be a party of common folk who have improvised an orchestra of their own to please the Raja and also to pay homage to the Bodhi tree. Two in the party have long trumpets and the rest are clapping their hands or dancing. The elegance of these figures conveys an atmosphere of carefree, uninhibited joy.

With their outstretched arms, simple gestures, their obviously improvised movements and the way in which every girl leans in the direction in which the group is moving, produces a delightful rhythm.

To understand the landmarks in style, this Hinaayana masterpiece may be compared with Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091], a scene from Mahaa-janaka Jaataka of Cave 1 belonging to the Mahaayana group done five hundred years later. The later though technically sophisticated and advanced, as can be seen in the elaborate sketches drawn in varying thickness of line and filled in with a variety of bright colours, betrays an affected style.

The personages in this painting are painted in dark brown or burnt sienna, the soft hues of a few colours, red ochre, yellow ochre, terra verde, lampblack and lime white. The themes are forcibly expressed in a style closely related to the contemporary sculpture in the round and in relief at the great Buddhist shrine of Sanchi. The costumes, the veils over the women's heads and the architecture depicted are typical of the Sanchi sculptures of the 2nd-1st century BC. Drawn in long, bold and vigorous curves with great precision, in a line of unvarying thickness, these representations speak of the artist's dexterity of drawing and expression.

An attempt to provide depth and relief is seen in the slight variation in tones, though the number of colours used is limited. Experiences would be gained in the next five centuries to master the art of relief modelling, through various means, like, vartana (shading) and ujjotana (adding-highlights).

Shad-danta Jaataka

Cave 10, 2nd century, BC

Almost all the paintings belonging to the early phase of Ajanta, that is, 2nd-1st centuries BC, are lost. One surviving painting is Raja with his Retinue [Mjt 106] in this cave. The Ajanta pioneers have made copies of the then existing paintings, both as line drawings and in colour. A notable painting illustrated here by a line-drawing is the story of Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 10 depicted on the back wall of the right-hand aisle behind pillars two to twelve. (shat = six, danta = tusked) This picture has suffered much from vandalism.

This painting, like those belonging to the pre-Christian era, is painted longitudinally in narrow horizontal panels, maintaining the same tonal level. The scenes unfolding on a ribbon-like space on the walls are not subdivided as in the later period except for an occasional tree, or a building or a group of rocks, which vaguely defines the end of one scene and the beginning of another.

The story is not told in a strict chronological order. This feature we find in the paintings belonging to the early as well as the later periods. The sequence of narration has been shown in the figure Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 10.

The Jaataka: The jaataka is the story of Bodhi-sattva born as an elephant-king with six tusks, hence called Shad-danta. One of his two wives, Chulla-subhadda, became angry with him as she felt that he neglected her in preference to the other queen. She, in the next birth was born as the Queen of Varanasi. She commanded that his tusks be produced before her to quench her vengeance. But when the tusks were actually brought before her, she breaks down and repents.

It is an excellent picture, full of love for life and nature, in which the artist has made full use of his proficiency in the drawing of animals and birds. The elegance of the figures and facial expressions reflect the late Sanchi sculpture.

A comparison of this composition with the one depicting Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 17 during the 5th century AD, brings out both the continuity and the variation in the artistic styles over a period of six hundred years.

The narration flows along the following lines:

1. Shad-danta's Life in his favourite Resort under a Banyan Tree (Line ?): In this drawing is shown a herd of elephants disporting themselves in the jungle amidst lotus flowers, ashoka and banyan trees, among them being the white, six-tusked elephant. The body of this elephant is dotted all over with small brown spots.

2. Shad-danta is seen again surrounded by six elephants (Line ?). The Bodhi-sattva now playfully holds a huge lotus in his trunk that he perhaps offers to Mahaa-subhadda that in turn enrages Chulla-subhadda.

3. Subhadda, Queen of Varanasi, feigns Illness and directs Sonuttara about the whereabouts of Shad-danta (Line ?): The young queen of Varanasi (who was Chulla-subhadda in her previous birth) is seated on a bamboo stool. She wants the tusks she has seen in a dream the night before. The king stands before her, promising to send a retinue of hunters to find out this six-tusked white elephant. There are four attendants in this scene, two seated and the other two standing. They are all facing the queen. There is a metal ewer beside the bed. Such ewers are still in use in India for carrying water for religious purposes. The sandals of the king also show a design available even now in the markets throughout the subcontinent. The king one feels, had been listening a moment before and has just stood up to say something in reply to the surly queen.

4. The King orders his Men to get the Tusks (Line ?): The king, seated on a throne in the court, is ordering Sonuttara and his lieutenant to arrange for the hunting mission. The stooping hunters show their obsequious reverence. The queen, seated on a couch beside the king, has turned her head to look at him. She has also raised two fingers of her right hand, and rests her legs on a low cushion. By the side of the chaawri-bearer behind the raja, is an Amazon holding the royal umbrella. The woman wears a scarf over her head. None of the other girls in this panel has this sort of headgear. A stout bodyguard, holding a staff is standing on the other side of the queen. The mango tree is at the back. We see the same girl noticed in the earlier scene, at the feet of the queen.

5. Sonuttara spies the Elephant from the Rocks (Line ?): Bodhi-sattva is seen surrounded by ten elephants and a calf. At the extreme right Sonuttara, with the same striped vest, stares at the colossal elephant with wonder, holding a bow in his left hand. The front-view of the elephant next to the hunter shows how the artist has tried to change the angle of vision to avoid monotony. Although the banana-grove is customarily associated with the elephant in Indian painting and literature, the artist has drawn a massive banyan tree in tune with the bulk and majestic figure of the Bodhi-sattva.

6. Sonuttara brings the sawn Tusks and Subhadda swoons at the Sight of the Tusks (Line ?): Sonuttara has arrived at the court of the king of Varanasi carrying the treasure. The queen has fallen into a swoon as she remembers her beloved of the previous birth. The king, seated by her on a chair that seems quite modern has turned round to support her with his left hand. There are four female attendants standing in a row at the back of the queen. The girl at the extreme right with a laurel on her head has covered her mouth with her right hand to stifle a shriek. The maid next to her has brought some water in a pot to revive the swooning queen. The damsel next to her, holding a fan in her right hand, looks at the former. The fourth, an umbrella-bearer, with a typical chignon on her head stares at Sonuttara. Besides these four girl-attendants, there are two more, one sitting at the feet of the queen, rubbing her feet, to help the blood circulation. The other damsel leans forward to balance the Queen as well as the composition. The realistic depiction of the scene, its dramatic treatment, together with the rhythmic expressions, all speak of the magnificent skill of the artist. The figures drawn in this scene reflect great plasticity of form mingled with artistic elegance and grace. The anxiety of the attendants, the expression of awe and wonder of Sonuttara at the sudden turn of events, the extreme perplexity of the king are all feelingly conveyed. But the melancholy and repentant look of the swooning queen surpasses them all and is comparable only to the monumental, the Dying Princess [ASI 003], painted in Cave 16, some eight centuries later.

7. Royal Couple, followed by Maids, is approaching a Chaitya (Line ?).

Two Figures of the Buddha

Cave 10, End of 6th century, AD

With the decline of artistic standards, the narrative scenes were replaced by repetitive representations of the Buddha. They came to be painted as a result of the gifts of Buddhist monks for earning religious merit for the attainment of supreme knowledge by all sentient beings beginning with the donors' parents. A painted record below the figures of eight Buddhas in Cave 22 tells us that the painting was the gift of a monk for the attainment of the supreme knowledge by all beings. The purpose of such gifts is further made clear by the statement that they endow the donor with good looks, good luck and good qualities.

In this painting Two Figures of the Buddha [ASI 085] are shown seated on a full-blown lotus against a bolster. The devotee who caused the figure to be painted also sits near the Master with his hands folded in anjali mudra (praying-attitude).

This painting is a specimen of the period of decadence. These are only two of many such Buddhas that we find enclosed in separate cubicles. Compared with other the Buddhas on the walls of Ajanta painted earlier, they lack refinement and finish. The colours are naive, flat and unnuanced – the lines wanting in feeling.

This painting may be considered a part of the continuous decline in standard at Ajanta. Also belonging to this period is the painting Thousand Buddhas [Kbl 118], a scene from the Miracle of Shraavasti in Cave 2.

Cave Sixteen
Vihaara · 5th century AD

Cave Sixteen

This vihaara shows the vigour and elegance of Buddhist architecture in the 5th century AD. The spacious verandah, about 20 meters long and about 3 meters wide, is flanked in front by six octagonal pillars and two pilasters. The hall, which is about 22 meters square and 4.6 meters high, has three entrances. The pilasters on both sides of the main doorway have sculptures of beautiful women standing on makara-s (crocodile-motifs). The hall contains twenty columns. The shrine, with a colossal Buddha, is scooped out from the back gallery of the hall, which has two side-chambers. The vihaara has no antechamber.

An interesting feature of this cave is that its plan is incised in an inscription on the left-hand wall near the end of the facade.

Sculptures: The front aisle has its ceiling carved in imitation of wooden rafters and beams. Carved on the bracket-capitals are some of the fine gandharva-sculptures, perhaps intended to convey the effect of a celestial region. These figures - dwarfs, musicians and flying couples - are shown in varying moods, a few groaning under weight, some serious, others taking it lightly and still others enjoying it. Specially interesting are the two vidyadhara-s (flying couples) notable for their graceful poise and features.

Paintings: There are a number of paintings that have survived the ravages of time and vandalism. The following have been taken for detailed discussion.

Maho-sadha and Four envious Counsellors [Mjt 111], a scene from Mahaa-Ummaga Jaataka, the story of the supernatural child Maho-sadha, is painted on the left wall of the front corridor, and is continued on the adjoining pilaster.

The story of the Conversion of Nanda is narrated in this cave also, and more forcefully than in Cave 1. It is painted in the left corridor, above the doors of the first and second cells from the left. The scene in which Nanda shaves his Head [Kbl 150] is a realistic portrayal, and Nanda in dejected Mood [Kbl 152] is full of pathos. But the most important scene and one of the best compositions of Ajanta is Dying Princess [ASI 003]. Several scenes from this episode are found in this cave and are worth studying in detail.

Mahaa-Ummaga Jaataka

Cave 16, 5th century, AD

Maha-Ummaga Jaataka is painted on the left wall of the front corridor, and is continued on the adjoining pilaster.

The Jaataka: Maha-Ummaga Jaataka is the story of Bodhi-sattva; who as a mere seven-year-old the boy, Maho-sadha, successfully solved the riddles which were posed to him by the people to test his supernatural powers and preached the Law.

The painting was praised by Griffiths in 1874. He could not copy it because it was covered with mud and grit. It was partially cleaned by the two Italian restorers in 1920-21. However, the gelatine they used to brighten the images soon turned yellow and opaque. The Archaeological Department of the Government of India has recently removed this layer of gelatine.

The explanation of the jaataka is supported by a line sketch Supernatural Child Maho-sadha [Line 10] and a colour picture Mahosadha and Four envious Councillors [Mjt 111], which is a detail of the mural.

Let us look at the line sketch Supernatural Child Maho-sadha [Line 10]. The lake, garden and building shown in the painting are apparently the tank, grove and palace built by the supernatural child, which are mentioned in the jaataka. The top left-hand corner of the picture shows a rider, representing the councillor of King Videha, who was deputed by his master to test Maho-sadha's wisdom. He wears a coat with full sleeves and striped stockings, while a straight sword hangs by his side. One of the horse's hind legs is stretched backward and the other raised forward to convey the feeling of rapid movement. Foreign influence can be seen in the rider's uniform; similar costume and accoutrements are frequently depicted in the other cases.

At the bottom barely visible is a scene depicting ‘the riddle of the son’ where Maho-sadha was called upon to settle a dispute between a woman and a goblin over the motherhood of a child. Maho-sadha ordered the child to be cut into two so that each could have equal part. Realising this would lead to the death of the child the mother of the child gives up her claim and thus Maho-sadha establishes the rightful owner. In the surviving part of the painting we see a man and a woman holding out the child's body while a soldier raises his sword to cut the boy in two.

In a plantain-grove below the royal rider is the scene Maho-sadha and four envious Councillors [Mjt 111]. On the left there are four figures apparently representing the four envious councillors who attempted to destroy Maho-sadha. The two men nearest to the boy have long hair, while the third wears a Persian cap. The expression of the fourth is especially sinister; his hair is dishevelled and his hand is on his sword as he looks at the boy in a wild and threatening way. The boy, wearing a small cap on his head, is gesturing to emphasise a point.

The painting is especially interesting because an illusion of distances is conveyed by depicting the group of men against a comparatively spacious background that is fringed by the conventional bands of hills. The mountains are in perfect harmony with the equally stylised water, which is represented by semicircular arches. The barrel-shaped roofs and heavy curvilinear eves bear a striking resemblance to the rock-hewn caves of the early 5th century.

Conversion of Nanda

Cave 16, 4th century, AD

Three scenes from the Conversion of Nanda of Cave 16 are painted on the left wall above doors of the first two cells of the hall. They are, Nanda shaves his Head [Kbl 150], Nanda in dejected Mood [Kbl 152] and the Dying Princess [ASI 003]. Of these the last, showing the death-scene of Nanda's newly married wife, heart-broken at Nanda's renunciation is very famous.

Nanda shaves his Head [Kbl 150] is the first scene, in which Nanda is seen sitting on the floor and a barber is shaving his head. Only the lower part of the body of the barber is visible. But a noteworthy feature of this panel is that the posture of Nanda and the inclination of his head show a keen sense of observation by the artist. A young monk is watching the scene.

We see Nanda in dejected Mood [Kbl 152] in the next scene. The tonsured Nanda is seen sitting in a pillared pavilion brooding over his pathetic condition and is filled with remorse thinking about his beautiful wife. His sitting posture is realistic, with his knee supporting his right elbow and his hand on his right cheek. The pillars are beautifully ornamented.

Finally, we come to one of the most beautiful paintings of Ajanta, popularly known as Dying Princess [ASI 003]. The painting is greatly damaged, and to assist studying the composition a line sketch, Death of the Princess (Line ?), is also included.

The lady is seen in a faint on a couch at the sight of a crown presented by a messenger at the left-hand bottom corner. The entire panel is gloomy and melancholy. The expressions of the characters in the scene, together with their gestures, reflect extreme anxiety and the seriousness of the situation.

The messenger holding the crown is fully aware of the grave news he has brought. His eyes are full of sympathy for the unhappy lady. The other male attendant above him, wearing a light skullcap in the Persian style, has raised his hand in excitement. To emphasise the expression of pathos, the artist places highlights on the nose, lips and chin of these two figures.

The central figure is that of the dark complexioned princess who has fainted on a low throne. Her back is resting on an embroidered cushion and her legs are stretched out in front resting on the floor. She has her left elbow on the cushion, and a female attendant behind the throne holds the lady with both hands so that she may not fall down. The head of the lady hangs limp and is slightly inclined towards the artist to reveal a three-quarter frontal view instead of her profile. Her half-closed eyes indicate extreme mental agony and physical collapse.

A second maid on the right of the lady is eagerly looking at the mistress with her mouth half-open. She has a ribbon tied around her forehead. A third maid, wearing a tight upper cloth is fanning the mistress with a palm-leaf fan. Through utter panic she has twisted her figure in an ungainly and somewhat awkward manner. Her gawkiness and the half-open mouth of the other girl are certainly not things of beauty, but the artist appears to be aware that at moments of tense anxiety even a female beauty may well forget how to look her best.

At the back of the pavilion, a little higher up, there is a roof enclosed by a parapet under a banana tree. Two maids are seen there engaged in intimate conversation. One of them has a veil over her head. She has a pitcher in her left hand and with the right she makes the sign of the figure two, by raising two of her fingers. She is of a fair complexion, has a youthful face, but her left eye is damaged. The other maid of dark complexion has a muffler round her neck, the knot of which is prominent. Her extended left hand wearing a bangle is significant. It appears that the fair girl has raised her two fingers in reply to the other’s question.

On the roof of the pavilion perches a gorgeous peafowl with its head bent low. The peacock with its head raised high and its chest thrown out is an accepted symbol of pride in beauty. But as the theme of the picture is its utter defeat, the peacock is seen with its head drooping in sorrow. The entire panel is gloomy and melancholy.

One wonders why out of the six female figures the princess is practically nude, although the artist has carefully arranged dresses for the other female figures. We come across with the feature of heroines of Ajanta being 'clothed in nakedness' a number of times.

The princess, it may be pointed out, is of dark complexion, this 'black is beautiful', is frequently met in Ajanta. The other black beauties are Shakti Pandara [ASI 031] painted in the Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara Panel in cave 1, Princess Irandati of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka in Cave 2 and the famous Black Apsaras [Kbl 169] of Cave 17. While commenting on this aspect it was pointed out that both Rama and Krishna were dark complexioned. Hasn't Kalidasa extolled that 'black is beautiful' while describing classical beauty in his Megha-dootam?

John Griffths was Principal of J.J.School of Art, Mumbai, and he had supervised copying Ajanta paintings from 1872 to 1855. He has published a monumental two-volume book on Ajanta paintings in 1896. He wrote: “For pathos and sentiment and the unmistakable way of telling its story this picture, I consider, cannot be surpassed in the history of art. A Florentine could have put better drawing, and a Venetian better colour, but neither could have thrown better expression into it.”

Vishvaantara Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th Century, AD

A number of episodes from the Vishvaantara Jaataka are painted in this cave and cover a large portion of the wall on the left aisle, except for the first scene which is painted on the left side of the main verandah. Belonging to the classical period of Ajanta, the lovely composition provides a wealth of information about the prevailing taste of this period.

The Jaataka: It is the story of Bodhi-sattva born as Prince Vishvaantara who became famous for his generosity. It may be mentioned that as per Buddhist belief this was the last Bodhi-sattva incarnation before being born as the Buddha. Vishvaantara was the son of King Sanjaya and queen Phusati in the city of Jetuttara. He exhibited his generosity from the time of his birth. But what angered his subjects was his gifting of his magical elephant, which could bring rain to the neighbouring kingdom of Kalinga, which was going through severe famine. To assuage their feeling King Sanjaya was constrained to send his only son in exile. Vishvaantara, who left the kingdom along with his wife Maadri and two children Jaali and Kanhajina gifted away his chariot and horses to needy persons. A Brahmin, Jujuka, then, begged him to give away his sons to alleviate his sufferings. The benevolent prince complied. Jujuka ill-treated the boys and took them to Jetuttara, where people recognised the captive boys as the grandchildren of their king and felt deeply remorseful for banishing their prince. The king summoned Vishvaantara and Maadri from their hermitage. He paid a handsome ransom to the greedy Brahmin to release the two boys. Thus the family came to be happily reunited.

The drama starts with Vishvaantara distributes Alms [kbl 159] painted on the left side of the outer verandah. A large congregation of beggars, ascetics and attendants surround Prince Vishvaantara and Princess Maadri. Dark in complexion Vishvaantara is sitting on a low stool. The lower part of his figure is damaged. The expectation in the face of the assembly is well portrayed. Of some interest are the square shaped umbrellas some of the people are carrying. The scene faithfully depicts the usual commotion associated with any alms giving ceremony and the attempt by the royal attendants in trying to maintain order.

The second scene of Vishvaantara informing Maadri of his banishment and subsequent departure is painted above the entrance to the first cell. This is shown in the line sketch Banishment of Vishvaantara [Line 06] and two colour reproductions Vishvaantara consoles his Wife [Kbl 161] and Vishvaantara’s departure [Kbl 162].

In this scene, Vishvaantara consoles his Wife [Kbl 161], we find the prince, seated on a low throne in the pavilion is supporting Maadri, his wife, with his left hand and with the right offering her some stimulating drink. The expression of the couple shows anxiety and concern. The demeanour of the princess is extremely graceful. She leans on her beloved with complete submission. Her face is ravishing. With her dreamy half-closed eyes she looks obliquely at her consort. Her distress is indicated by her clinging to her husband for support and the prince's concern by his proffering her a cup of wine. The figure of the prince, badly damaged, registers a look of anxiety.

Beside the royal couple there are three figures inside the pavilion. The ubiquitous dwarf holding a vessel with raised eyes is standing in front. We have seen similar dwarfs in almost all the royal scenes. The third attendant in the composition is the one standing beside the pillar, wearing a greenish jacket and a Persian skullcap, and holding a liquor-jug, ready to pour out the contents into the cup of the prince. It will be noted that the glances of all the participants of this high drama converge on the hapless prince. There is one more female figure squatting Indian style with a water-pitcher on her lap.

The pavilion is supported by four round pillars. The wall and the pillars are reddish, while the parapet is yellow and the floor green with a sprinkling of white flowers here and there.

The whole scene exudes melancholy.

What follows is the scene of Vishvaantara’s departure [Kbl 162] to the left of the earlier picture. In this we witness the young royal couple leaving the palace watched by the attendants.

The central figures are identified by the umbrellas held over their heads, signifying their royalty. Also the physiognomy of Vishvaantara and Maadri remains unchanged: Vishvaantara depicted as dark complexioned with hair falling over the shoulders and with hair-band. The princess has two maids-in-attendance on either side, in addition to a male attendant with a drooping moustache. The prince is barefooted and clad only in a loincloth and some flowers in his hair.

Among the people present we see prominently a beggar with a crooked staff, possibly testing the generosity of the prince. Between the pavilion and the garden is painted a window, through which the king and the queen watch the departure of their beloved son.

The narration continues above the space between the third and fourth cell-doors, with Vishvaantara and Maadri taking leave of the king in one pavilion and then the queen in another pavilion.

To the left of the previous scene is depicted Vishvaantara informs his Wife [Kbl 186]. This is a repetition of an earlier scene. Here the artist has tactfully placed them at right angles to each other, as if they are seeing each other. Why this scene was necessary, when the same scene was enacted before, and more forcefully, we may never know! It may be that the story is told again starting from this scene. This guess is prompted by the fact that the narration is continuous from here to the end of the jaataka. The portrayal of both Vishvaantara and Maadri is superb bringing out the compassionate and gentle nature of the royal couple.

The Ajanta artist did not just paint scenes of religious themes on the wall, but embellished them with beautiful details of everyday happenings which may in no way be connected with the main story. For we have an exquisite painting of Maid braids the Hair [Kbl 187] of a woman.

In the middle of the wall between the second and third cell-doors, that is somewhat below the previous scene, the prince and his family are seen departing in a decorated carriage drawn by four horses. Among the crowd are disconsolate Citizens watch Vishvaanta’s Departure [Kbl 188R] portrays the departure of the prince and his family leaves the city. The composition is one of the masterpieces of Ajanta.

Let us now follow the procession. In front of the chariot are four Brahmins. The composition is interesting for the depiction of the market place through which the chariot passes. We see a shop with the milkman with two pails of milk and a basin of curd. There is shop where the oil-vendor is ladling oil into a cup. A weighing machine can be seen in the next grocer's shop. The buildings are two storied with shops in the ground floor and residences on the upper floor. Women are watching the departure of the prince from the open windows of these upper-floors. This reminds one of a similar scene in Mad Elephant Nalagiri [Kbl 172] in the episode called Miracle at Raja-griha, also in this cave, where a market scene is depicted.

In the next scene we find the royal family standing on the ground, and we should presume that the chariot has been donated to the four Brahmins we saw in the last scene.

Then follows the depiction of their life in Mount Vamka. In the damaged mural we can barely see the daily chore of Maadri, like collecting fruits in the wilderness, etc.

The next scene depicts the gifting away of his sons to a greedy Brahmin, Jujuka. Vishvaantara is seen in front of a hut made of leaves (parna-shaala). Vishvaantara informs Maadri of the development. Vishvaantara is sitting in front of the same hut and the figure of Maadri is drawn with the greatest care. She is a personification of sorrow.

In the next scene of happy reunion of the royal family, painted above the fourth cell door entrance, we see the events that take place in the court of King Sanjaya. The description of this scene is supported by a sketch King Sanjaya’s Court [Line 09] and a colour picture Restoration of Children [ASI 071].

At the left bottom corner we see the earlier sequence that of the guard enquiring of Jujuka, the greedy Brahmin, about the children. The guard is adorned with jewellery and the bejewelled sword hanging from his belt is very prominent. The posture of this guard recalls that of the guard of Avalokiteshwara in Cave 1. King Sanjaya is seated on a low throne with a rich diadem over his head and some precious jewellery on his person. He has a conventional lion-waist (simha-kati). In front of him Jujuka is seen receiving the ransom from the royal treasurer with his hands extended. To the left of the king is seen Queen Phusati seated on another stool. Between the king and the queen the artist has drawn Jaali, one of Vishvaantara’s children. The figure of Kanhajina, the other child, is on the left of the queen. At the foot of the king there are three noblemen apparently discussing among themselves the miraculous return of the children.

The finale of Coronation of Vishvaantara [Kbl 190] is mostly damaged, and what little remains, it is claimed, seems to have been retouched in a later period. The coronation scene, however, is well conceived. Vishvaantara is seen sitting in a throne while holy water is being poured over him. Queen Maadri is watching it from a pavilion on to his right. There are two women carrying offerings on a tray, both of them in striking graceful tribhanga (triflex) poses.

While describing Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069] in Cave 1 it was mentioned that, according to the canons of Indian painting, the limbs of human characters are related to the shapes and forms of the vegetal and animal worlds. Among the examples cited was the comparison of torso with the body of a lion. The torso of King Sanjaya in this panel is lion-waisted (simha-kati), and is an example of such sadrishya (similitude).

The figure of Jujuka in this series of paintings shows the mastery of the Ajanta artist in character-study. The artist has delineated all his ugly features - the broken front teeth, the goatee beard, the bald forehead and the spiky hair at the back of his skull, the parrot-like nose and small uncanny eyes, combined with the brightness which has come over his face at the jingle of the money which the royal treasurer is pouring into his outspread scarf.

Two more points arrest attention. Firstly, the continuity of costume and of make up. The artist has not forgotten that a corner of the Brahmin's umbrella is torn! This has been repeated carefully in all the scenes. Secondly the posture of one's ‘hair standing on end in expectation’ has been literally given expression to while drawing Jujuka at the moment of receiving the golden coins.

Descent of Indra

Adoration of the Buddha

Cave 17, 5th century, AD

The scene, Descent of Indra [Kbl 164] is painted on the back wall of the veranda to the left of the central door and by the side of the paintings depicting the alms-giving episode of Vishvaantara Jaataka. In the continuous panorama of murals on this wall, this group of six figures could be singled out showing the Descent of Indra [Kbl 164] with his troupe of celestial musicians from the heavens above. This may be taken as a solo-picture for this can neither be connected with any incident in the life of the Buddha nor associated with a jaataka.

A line sketch Indra with his Entourage [Line 05] and the colour reproduction Descent of Indra [Kbl 164] may be consulted while following the following description of the mural.

The central figure, that of Indra, is the biggest in size. He is of fair complexion, embellished with rich jewellery in the form of necklaces, armlets, bangles, earrings and a magnificent crown over his head. He has a dagger and a sword attached to his belt. The painting of Indra illustrates the extraordinary breadth and sureness of the draughtsmanship. The individual features, eyes and nose and mouth, are defined with free, flowing sweeps of the brush. The varying thickness of the lines provides a kind of shading, and the very freedom of the drawing imparts both freshness and boldness to the forms as well as the decorations.

The suggestion of movement is unmistakable. The strings of pearls round Indra's neck all swing in the opposite direction to the movement. The ends of ribbons tied round the hair are also flying. To emphasise movement towards the right-bottom corner, the artist has skilfully drawn a cluster of clouds, piled up in the opposite direction that is, receding towards the left top. The delineation of the cloud in the form of white patches with blue rims of varying thickness are so drawn as to appear as successive columns of cumulus cloud-heaps.

To the right of Indra, is shown a gandharva (celestial) with coarse features, a bulbous nose and sunken eyes, carrying a slender musical instrument. The up-turned loose ends of his upper garment with yellow stripes over green background, also suggest movement. The illusion of movement in space is skilfully created through these suggestions and the bent knees of the figures.

During this descent, Indra is accompanied by celestial musicians. To the right of Indra, there are two celestial nymphs. The one nearer the central figure, with a greenish or greyish hue, holds a pair of cymbals in her hands. Her eyes are downcast. She appears to be engrossed in music, oblivious of her surroundings. The dark girl next to her, also playing a pair of cymbals, is looking back at her companion while levitating forward. These two girls are loaded with sapphires, diamonds and pearls, although their garments are scanty, exposing the contours of their full breasts. The younger person near the left leg of Indra has a musical instrument, a sort of drum. With a twist of the neck he is looking back out of the corner of his eyes.

The most striking figure of the entire group is that of the flute-player who is a little ahead of the floating group. She is in her early teens, and since her head is turned away the artist has availed himself of this opportunity of displaying her coiffure by dividing the dark chignon into four quadrants by means of ribbons.

Musicians have been shown in the scenes of Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091] and Mahaa-janaka leaves the Palace [Kbl 096] in the narration of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1 and in the Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani panel [Line 03].

We have seen in the other compositions, like the scenes Queen captivates Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L] and Mahaa-janaka leaves the Palace [Kbl 096] of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1, that the Ajanta artist has used a variety of sthaana-s (body positions) while portraying characters

The suggestion of weightless grace and elegance in these figures reflects the postures of Indian dance and reminds us of the effortless flight in the Gupta sculpture or the Pallava sculpture at Mahabalipuram. The entire composition is rich in tonal harmony creating that gay mood, that idyllic charm mellowed with vibrant movement and music, which is the essence of this mural.

Yaksha Couple and Black Apsaras

Cave 17, 5th century, AD

To the right of the central door we see a few gandharva-s and kinnara-s being a part of Indra's train, floating through clouds towards the earth to worship the Buddha. Among these celestials proceeding to pay homage to the Buddha are a Yaksha-Couple [Kbl 168] and a Black Apsaras [Kbl 169] flying effortlessly through clouds, strong winds swaying their ornaments and tassels.

Flying celestials are common in Indian paintings and sculpture. Flying vidyaadhara-s are frequently seen behind the main deities, on the brackets, and in any place the artist feels would highlight the theme. We saw Indra descending to the earth to adore the Buddha. The treatise Vishnu-dharmottara prescribes the qualifications for the painter. It says: "He who paints waves, flames, smoke, streamers fluttering in the air, according to the movement of the wind, should be considered a great painter." Accordingly Ajanta painters took great pleasure in composing scenes involving movement with great zest.

The floating clouds, the swaying foliage and apsaras and gandharva-s flying swiftly through the air, produce a fantastic movement and excitement, and every connoisseur of art was intoxicated by its beauty. Often this depiction is considered to have been influenced by Kalidasa's poetry. Or is it the other way about?

We shall start with the Black Apsaras [Kbl 169]. A line sketch Black Beauty (line ?) may also be referred to. One of the most beautiful single heads in the entire Ajanta complex is that of this flying apsaras, commonly known as Black Apsaras (nymph). She drifts through the air while beating out a measure on tiny cymbals. Her beautiful jewelled ornaments would seem to place this picture in the class of painting described in the shaastra-s (canons) as most admired by women. Around her neck is a necklace of diamonds, sapphires, and pearls, its heavy pendants swaying with the nymph's movement. She wears matching strings of jewels in her hair. The pendulum movement of all the dangling ropes of precious stones to the right is echoed in the direction of the stiff white riband behind her head: the direction of all these ornaments subtly indicates the apsaras's flight towards the left of the composition.

The face of this apsaras is in the form of an elongated ovoid, almost the leaf shape often recommended in the shaastra-s. This, along with her narrow eyes and tiny mouth, suggests wistfulness and ethereality. The head has a haunting evocation of sensuous warmth, and, as so often at Ajanta, the hands pliant, elegant and infinitely graceful. Both in the lyrical beauty of the physical types and rich contrast between the dark flesh and the shining jewels, the paintings of Cave 17 are close in style to the murals at Bagh, in the state of Madhya Pradesh, some 250 km away.

Regarding the dark complexion of this apsaras, it is worth recalling that we come across with many black beauties in Ajanta. In fact, most of the beautiful women we see in Ajanta are dark complexioned. Thus, Shakti Pandara [ASI 031], the dark princess, painted in the panel of Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara in Cave 1, Princess Irandati of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka in Cave 2, The Dying Princess [ASI 003] of Cave 16 and this famous Black Apsaras, who are all painted ‘as dark as the night’ have already been referred to. This may be, it was felt, due to the southern influence. This line of thinking was due to the fact that Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshwara depicted in the same panel as that of the dark beauty, Shakti Pandara [ASI 031], is attributed to the southern influence.

Perhaps, at some time in the past Indian aesthetics accepted black as beautiful. Weren't both Rama and Krishna dark complexioned?

In the entourage we find a Yaksha-Couple [Kbl 168]. The composition is really sculpturesque!

This painting is another example how the Ajanta artist has handled the various techniques for plastic modelling, particularly in making a choice between vartana (shading) and ujjotana (adding-highlights) to suit a given theme. The sparkling diamonds, sapphires and pearls in her necklace and other jewellery proclaim this. The ujjotana method of highlighting applied to the chin and cheek is soft and subtle, keeping in view the mood of the subject matter, an example of classical restraint. This can be contrasted with a more liberal application of white patches to the characters of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka in Cave 2, heralding the beginning of mannerism in Ajanta.

Maanushi Buddhas and Mithuna Figures

Cave 17, 5th Century, AD

Above the main entrance leading to the main hall of this cave there is a row of eight figures, the seven Maanushi Buddhas [Nou 161/162] and Maitreya, the Future Buddha. Below this row, on the lintel, are eight panels of Mithuna Figures [Nou 161/162] in amorous attitudes. Sculptured figures of river-goddesses Ganga and Yamuna on a makara (crocodile) flank each side of the doorway.

Maanushi Buddhas: The eight Maanushi Buddhas (Mortal-Buddhas) are Vipashyin, Shikhi, Vishva-vasu, Kraku-chchhanda, Kanaka-muni, Kashyapa, Shaakya-simha (Gautama) and Maitreya. The first three show three different mudra-s, namely, dharma chakra mudra (turning-the-Wheel-of-Religion-attitude), dhyana mudra (meditation-attitude) and abhaya-mudra (assurance-attitude), respectively. The next two figures are deep in meditation. According to the cultic conventions, all the figures have some common features, such as curled locks of hair with an ushnisha (turban, actually, a characteristic mark of hair on the head of the Buddha), long ear-lobes and down-cast half-closed eyes. The sixth Buddha, Kashyapa, is painted with a pale reddish hue and the seventh Buddha, namely Gautama, has the conventional yellow robe on his person. The last in the list, Maitreya, the Future Buddha, is the only one wearing a magnificent crown and other jewellery. This is because, unlike others, he is yet to renounce all worldly possessions.

Mithuna Figures: Under each of the above Buddha figures is a small rectangular slab showing mithuna figures (couples). The varied tones of their skins, display a remarkable sense of colour and a controlled rhythm in the draughtsmanship.

Sportive and jolly, they are sipping wine, caressing or wooing the partner in love, although one cannot find any coarseness in their compositions. In one of the panels a girl offers a cup of wine to her beloved; in another, the male figure, recumbent on a bolster, is caressing his ladylove; in a third the two lovers are almost embracing each other.

One wonders the propriety of selecting eight amorous couples under the Buddha-figures in a vihaara, a chapel-cum-dormitory for celibate monks. Admittedly, the figures are realistic, and the composition, drawing and colouring are excellent; yet, with our contemporary sensitivity, we cannot but feel a little ill-at-ease at the awkward juxtaposition of the incompatible ideas conveyed by the two parallel rows. Perhaps, the possible explanation is that the artist-monks had no inhibition in portraying the pleasures of life.

Kinship between Sculpture and Painting: Two exquisite sculptured figures of riverine deities, Ganga and Yamuna, lend support and add welcome grace to the doorway. The kinship we find between sculpture and painting, as that between painting and dance, in India is something altogether unique in the history of world-art. Most impressive is the way the two art forms co-exist at Ajanta, complementing each other. Artistic ingenuity has reinforced the relationship.

This kinship is apparent in this complex. Each figure is in concord with the other, sculpture blends with the murals, colours with the colour scheme, flower-studded door-panels with the glowing wallscape and the decoratively painted ceiling. A yearning for mutual exchange and unity that we find here permeates the walls of Ajanta, resounding with the story of this unity bonded in faith.

It may be mentioned here that in Ajanta and elsewhere even the sculptures were fully painted, though most of the paint has disappeared. The blending achieved must have been astonishing when the stone carvings were bedecked in colour, falling in line with the hue and colour scheme of the murals. One can only imagine the riot of colours of the original scheme.

Miracle at Raja-griha

Cave 17, 5th Century, AD

On to the right of the main-doorway is painted this story of a miracle performed by the Buddha, called the Miracle at Raja-griha or the Subjugation of Nalagiri.

The Story: Deva-datta was a cousin of the Buddha. He was envious of the Great Being and attempted to murder him. He failed miserably every time. Subjugation of Nalagiri is one such episode. It may be recollected that King Bimbi-saara was a faithfully follower of the Buddha. But his son, Ajaata-shatru, who came to power after dethroning his father took steps to remove Buddhist influence in his country. Deva-datta conspired with King Ajaata-shatru of Raja-griha, and released an intoxicated elephant, called Nalagiri, on the streets of Raja-griha just when the Master was passing through. The people ran helter-skelter on seeing the charging elephant. But the Most Compassionate One was unperturbed. When Nalagiri came close he gently stroked him, to which the mad elephant responded by kneeling down in obeisance. Deva-datta's scheme was defeated by the love shown by the Buddha.

In the first act, we find the king and Deva-datta hatches a Conspiracy [Kbl 171]. The location is a two-storied palace building. On the right side of the top floor we have King Ajaata-shatru in royal dress and a crown. To his left is Deva-datta who is in the garb of a Brahmin priest. The left part of the king is damaged and so is the major portion of the right part of Deva-datta. The king is wearing jewellery studded with pearls, diamonds and other precious stones. From the gestures of these characters we may reconstruct the plot. Deva-datta has raised two fingers. He has argued his case.

Behind the king we find four women, three directly behind the king and one below the other maids. If we understand the syntax of Ajanta properly, the women are all palace-maids, and the queen is not among them. The maid in the middle is somewhat short and is wearing a long sleeved jacket. She is looking at the maid to her right, who, from her looks is shocked at the conspiracy taking shape in the balcony.

There are five maids seen on the lower floor. They are animatedly discussing the shocking rumour they have heard.

The noteworthy feature is the hairstyles of these maids. There is no repetition. Particularly interesting is the hair-do of the woman who is seen second from the right on the lower floor. This bears a close resemblance to the head-dress of present-day Mohini Aattam dancers of Kerala. The architectural details of this scene are worthy of study. Also interesting are the sthaana-s (body-postures) of the women and their mudra-s (hand-gestures).

Separated by a conventional arch, the next act begins. We see a bhikshu hurrying, probably taking the information to the Buddha. Unfortunately, this part of the mural is badly damaged. In the next Act, which is better preserved the charging of the rogue elephant and its subjugation, are depicted.

A line diagramme Subjugation of Nalagiri [Line 07] and a colour reproductions mad Elephant Nalagiri [Kbl 172] and Subjugation of the Elephant [Kbl 173] support the following description of the scene.

The incident takes place in the market place with rows of shops on the ground floor and residential apartments on the upper floor. The former has awnings of canvas or bamboo-mat in the form of lean-to verandahs to protect the shops against rain and sun. It may be pointed out that a similar market-scene can be found painted in Vishvaantara Jaataka in the same cave.

The awnings of the first shop from the left are found to have been dislodged by mad Elephant Nalagiri [Kbl 172], which dominates the composition. There are terror-stricken people here and there running away in fright. The shopkeepers pull down the door-shutters and the ladies in the balconies look horrified. Near the hind part of the elephant a mounted guard is seen with his right hand raised to warn the crowd. The gigantic monster has a sinister look and a man is seen dangling between the tusks.

In the next scene Subjugation of the Elephant [Kbl 173], to the right we find the elephant kneeling before the Buddha cringing in obeisance. The Master, standing on a lotus, with his disciple, Shari-putta, on his right, is seen caressing the subjugated giant, stroking him with affection. Shopkeepers and pedestrians are all impressed at the miracle. Some have folded their hands to show respect. The smartly dressed ladies on the upper storey, who were torn a few minutes ago in horror and anguish, are now relieved of the tension and are seen strewing flowers and garlands on the Master.

This particular scene has been a subject of criticism by Yazdani. He observed: "In drawing the figures of the ladies in the balconies, the artist has exhibited both imagination and artistic skill, but the composition of the entire subject would appeal more to a lover of the legend than to the art; for the two large representations of the elephant have covered almost the entire space, and the figures of the Buddha and his chief disciple show little spontaneity or freedom of expression qualities which are essential to art of a higher class."

A lover of art for art sake may not agree with the artist who is driven purely by religious fervour.

Shad-danta Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th century, AD

The Shad-danta Jaataka is painted in Cave 10 during the pre-Christian Hinaayana period. The same jaataka has been narrated in this cave, after a gap of about six hundred years. A comparative study of the two compositions would be rewarding, as this brings into focus both the continuity and the variation in the artistic styles over a period of these six hundred years. The painter has drawn Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 17 on the outer wall of the front corridor to the left of the main entrance.

The Jaataka: The jaataka is the story of Bodhi-sattva born as an elephant-king with six tusks, hence called, Shad-danta. While playing with his two wives, one of them, Chulla-subhadda, becomes angry as she feels that her husband neglects her in preference to the other queen. She, in the next birth was born as the Queen of Varanasi. She commands her men to get her the tusks of Shad-danta, in revenge. But when the tusks were actually brought, she breaks down and repents.

The story is depicted in four scenes: first, the queen's bedroom scene in which Queen plans her Revenge; second, the lotus lake in which we see Sporting Shad-danta [Kbl 179] with his herd; third, Sonuttara brings the Tusks [Kbl 180T] of Shad-danta to the royal court; and fourth, the Queen faints [Kbl 180B] on seeing them. The second to the fourth scenes are shown in the line sketch Shand-danta Story of Cave 17 [Line 01].

In the second scene, shown in the middle we find sporting Shad-danta [Kbl 179] with his herd in the beautiful lake. The lotus flowers on the surface of the water, barely visible at the bottom of the left side, vividly suggest the animals' carefree life in the jungle.

In the lower portion of the right hand panel is depicted the third scene. First, we find the six-tusked Shad-danta and his followers. The hunter, Sonuttara, has been drawn thrice. First, he stoops low before the Bodhi-sattva, in great reverence. In the second, he looks back at the elephant with gratitude while balancing the tusks on a pole. In the third, Sonuttara brings the Tusks [Kbl 180T] from Varanasi.

In the last episode, painted to the left, the Queen faints [Kbl 180B] at the sight of the six tusks. It appears as a framed picture in the mural.

In terms of style this composition is vastly different from the great mural narrating the same jaataka, Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 10. While the arrangement was horizontal in the earlier mural, it is executed in a zigzag fashion here. The composition of the scene in the two paintings is different. While she was seated in Cave 10, we find her fainting in the arms of the king here, like Maadri reclining on Vishvaantara in the scene Vishvaantara consoles his Wife [Kbl 161] in the narration of Vishvaantara Jaataka, painted on the outer verandah in the very same cave. Further, the drawing of the royal bedchamber within a frame is unusual even by the standards of the day. Continuity is also witnessed from the painting of elephants from the Hinaayana painting of the 2nd century BC of Cave 10 to this composition.

Though not connected with the narration directly Monkey beneath a Palas Tree [ASI 009] is an interesting painting. The relaxed, dignified calm of the monkey, consisting basically of one masterly sweep of a long stroke of the brush starting beneath the chin and forming a curve outlining the head and spine and terminating beneath the knee-cap, is a fine example of the translation of closely observed scenes and moments of action or repose into simple yet profoundly explicit drawings. An equally interesting feature of the scene (not shown in the colour reproduction) is row of ants on one of the branches painted by the artist. The Ajanta artist has paid minute attention to represent all kinds of living beings, including ants!

Mahaa-Kapi Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th century AD

The Buddhist faith preaches compassion towards all creatures. The Bodhi-sattva incarnated as animals many times to propagate this virtue. Once He was born as a monkey and the story is known as Mahaa-kapi Jaataka. There are two episodes narrated in this cave. In one of them He helped His herd escape from the attack of the king by forming Himself into a bridge across the river. In the second, He helps an ungrateful Brahmin who attempted to kill Him.

The first episode is painted on the inside wall of the front verandah, on the left when you enter through main entrance.

The Jaataka: Once the Bodhi-sattva was as born as the king of monkeys, and lived in a forest far away from the city. In the forest there was a very huge mango tree, which produced extremely delicious and extra-ordinarily large fruits. One day one of the fruits fell into the river and was picked up by a fisherman. Never having seen such a fruit he took it to his king. The king after enjoying the fruit, ordered his army to trace the tree which produced such delicious fruit. When the tree was located the king found to his dismay that a large herd of monkeys were destroying the mangoes. He, then ordered his army to shoot the monkeys down. The king-monkey apprehending danger to His subjects caught hold of a branch of the tree to convert his body into a bridge and ordered the monkeys to escape. The last monkeys, who were envious of the king-monkey, intentionally kicked the most benevolent monkey while jumping off his back. The tired monkey-king fell into the river below wounded. The astonished king , touched by what he had seen, rescued Him. But in spite of his nursing, the monkey-king died, but without any hatred towards the monkey who kicked Him.

In this badly damaged mural, the artist has taken some liberty in the narration. Instead of a mango tree he has drawn a banyan tree. This is evident from the shape of the leaves and from the presence of aerial roots. He has also added a touch of humour which will be mentioned in the discussion that follows. The mural is almost obliterated and hence the discussion is supported by a line diagramme Mahaa-kapi saves his Herd (Line ?).

The narration of this jaataka is another illustration that the scenes are not painted in the chronological sequence. In most of the paintings we find that scenes are grouped together on the basis of location. To follow the sequence would mean abrupt changes in the settings, which, the artist might have thought, would disturb the composition.

The story starts at the top left with a monkey sitting comfortably on a branch and enjoying a mango fruit. For the next scene is half way down the mural where the king riding a horse is marching with his army towards the forest. The king may be identified by the royal umbrella over him. The commotion of the marching of the army is evident, with the solders in great animation, carrying swords and shields. In the next scene, which is shown a little higher up, three archers are shooting at the monkeys on the tree. On the top of the mural is painted the monkey-king stretching his body to act as a bridge and monkeys passing over his back. Next we see the fallen monkey-king being rescued by two solders on a blanket. The scene in which the Bodhi-sattva is preaching the Law to the king is not included in the sketch.

Let us see the secular part of the composition, for which the Ajanta artist has always had a weakness. A low wall separates the river from the forest. The river is shown abounding in fishes and water birds. In the lower portion there are a number of bathers, males and females. The Ajanta artist who never misses an opportunity to add a touch of humour, has portrayed a short man, probably a court-jester, eves-dropping on the bathing beauties!

Another episode from Mahaa-kapi Jaataka is painted on the left-hand wall of the back corridor and the Monkey helps a Man [Kbl 192] and Man throws Stone on the Monkey [Kbl 193] are two of the important scenes.

Let me narrate this episode in brief. Once a man, while collecting some fruits in a forest, fell into a pit from which he could not come out. The Bodhi-sattva, born as a monkey, saw his plight and with great effort carried him on his back to safety. When the tired monkey was taking rest, the ungrateful man picked up a big rock and threw it at the monkey so that he could eat it. The monkey was badly injured and admonished the man for his ungrateful act. Then the compassionate monkey led him out of the forest.

In the first scene Monkey helps a Man [Kbl 192], we see on the top right the fallen man shouting for help. On the left is the compassionate monkey responding to his call. The forest scene is composed excellently, the trees and two deer are very natural.

In the scene Man throws Stone on the Monkey [Kbl 193], at the centre the exhausted monkey is asleep and the ungrateful man is about to throw a huge stone. The effort of lifting a huge stone is unmistakable. On the left we see the monkey chiding him.

Sutasoma Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th century, AD

Several episodes from the Sutasoma Jaataka are painted on the wall of the back corridor with a great deal of imagination and skill. Next to Simhala Avadaana and Vishvaantara Jaataka this narration is the longest in Ajanta. Unfortunately, a major potion of the painting has perished.

The Jaataka: King Sudaasa of Varanasi went on a hunting expedition. Exhausted, he took rest on a stone. While he was thus sleeping, a lioness attracted by the king came to him and licked his heel. This made her pregnant. Her son Saudaasa became the king after his father's death. One day his cook by mistake served him human flesh. Thereafter the lioness's instinct was quickened and he became a cannibal. He would satisfy his cannibalistic appetite by having his subjects murdered. In course of time the citizens drove him out of the country. While living in the forest, Saudaasa preyed on travellers. One day King Sutasoma of Indra-prastha, a Bodhi-sattva incarnation, had gone to the forest to bathe, where he was attacked by Saudaasa, and was taken as a prisoner. Sutasoma requested permission for a day's freedom so that he could hear a sermon from an ascetic. When Sutasoma kept his promise his captor was impressed. Then Bodhi-sattva preached the Law of Dharma to Saudaasa, who gave up cannibalism and joined the Holy Order.

One of the surviving early scenes of this story is the scene of the Deer Hunt [Mjt 167] by King Sudaasa. It shows the king of Varanasi setting out on a hunt with his retinue, accompanied by two other princes and a pack of dogs. The King is seen pursuing the deer and, having become separated from his retinue, entering a forest abounding in wild animals.

The nimble movement of the deer and the wild stare in its eyes are shown in this Deer Hunt [Mjt 167]. In this unsurpassed masterpiece, the horror of being hunted and killed is marvellously conveyed by the animal's abrupt and swift leap and the almost hypnotised look in its bulging eyes as it glances back at the approaching horse.

The forest scene continues above this panel, and the depicted scenes involve the lioness. We see the Court of Sudaasa [Kbl 195] where the lioness tells the king that she is carrying the king's child.

The Coronation of Saudaasa [Kbl 196] is the next important panel, where we find Saudaasa ascending the throne on the death of his father. The ceremonial bath is realistically portrayed and the excitement is unmistakable.

Another realistic presentation is the royal Kitchen of Saudaasa [Kbl 197T] where his cook inadvertently cooked human meat. The composition provides us information regarding contemporary social habits.

The next scene in which Saudaasa tastes Human Flesh [Kbl 197B] is another important panel. The king is seen sitting on a low stool and eating from a plate kept on another low stool. Two cooks are looking through a window, most likely, of the kitchen. The maids who are serving him are seen looking at the king with concern.

The detail of the battle scene, realistically depicting the confusion of war, in which cavalry and foot soldiers are participating, is shown in King fights against his Army [Kbl 198] when the army rose in revolt against the cannibalistic king.

Army driving Saudaasa to the Forest [Kbl 199] is depicted in the next scene. We see the king on the galloping horse. The movement of the horse and the commotion of the moving foot-solders are the result of close observation and draughtsmanship.

The Buddha preaching to the Congregation

The Miracle of Ascending the Heaven of Thirty-three Gods

Cave 17, 5th century

The theme of this painting is one of the four miracles performed by the Buddha. He ascended the Heaven of Thirty-three Gods and preached the Dharma to His Mother at Shaankashya, one of the eight holy places in the Buddhist faith. They are referred to as attha-mahaa-thaanaani.

Two more miracles have been depicted in Ajanta. His multiplying Himself a thousand-fold, called the Miracle of Shraavasti, is depicted in Cave 2 and is described in the scene Thousand Buddhas [Kbl 118]. The Miracle at Raaja-griha, the subjugation of a mad elephant at Raaja-griha is also painted in this cave. Shankaashya, where this miracle is believed to have taken place is known to day as Sankissa or Sankasia, in the district of Farukkhabad in the sate of Uttar Pradesh.

The Miracle: The Buddha went to the Heaven of Thirty-three Gods at the invitation of Indra (called Shakra in the Buddhist mythology). There he preached to the congregation of gods, which included His mother Mahaa-maaya. After this, He returned to the earth by the three-ladder made by Indra. On reaching the world He found a large assemblage of monks and noblemen waiting to hear His sermon. This theme is painted on the left wall of the ante-chamber.

The episode is composed in three rows. In the top row we see the Buddha preaching in heaven, in the middle we see the Buddha descending and in the bottom panel, the Master is giving a sermon before a congregation of people on earth. A major part is given in the sketch Preaching of the Buddha (Line ?).

The top panel is rather damaged, but some figures are identifiable. Close to the Buddha we see a group of women, including the mother of the Buddha Mahaa-maaya listens to the Sermon [Kbl 202]. Maaya-devi is ‘dressed in nakedness’, whereas the dresses of the other people look foreign. On the other side are the gods.

In the middle panel the Buddha can be seen descending to the earth. He is accompanied by His disciples and the gods. The Compassionate One is in His usual saffron robe.

The bottom row is better preserved. The characters comprising the audience are truly heterogeneous.

In this bottom panel of we see the Buddha preaches to the Congregation [Kbl 197]. He is sitting in dharma chakra mudra (teaching-attitude) exuding warmth and compassion. His throne is ornamented. His chair-bearers are Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani and Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara. There are also two flying cherubs behind him. On his left are a few people squatting on the ground. All of them look like monks. The elderly monk in the centre of the front row is identified as Shari-putta, one of His important disciples. The one to his right looking at the Master is Ananda, His cousin and disciple. The one behind Shari-putta with a checked upper-cloth is another disciple, Moggallana. We notice at least three men in royal uniform riding horses.

Then we have a Woman Elephant-Rider [Kbl 203] with her entourage. She must be a queen as there is an umbrella held over head. Some identify her as Maaya-devi, the mother of the Buddha. She is sitting with a bolster of interesting design at her back. Careful readers might have noticed the endless variety of textile designs in Ajanta.

On the left part of the panel, there is another group of Audience [Kbl 204]. Among the people sitting on the floor, in the first row, starting from the left, Ajaata-shatru, his father King Bimbi-saara of Magadha and King Prasenjita of Koshala. Behind Bimbi-saara is a person of foreign origin. There are also a number of people with curved swords held high. Behind them are two men on horses. They look like foreigners again. Further above them are two caparisoned elephants with raised trunks. The riders may be foreign statesmen.

This grand mural, where over fifty people participate, is a subject of great admiration by art critics all over the world.

Mother and Child before the Buddha

Buddha in Kapila-vastu

Cave 17; 5th century, AD

This mural, Mother and Child before the Buddha [Kbl 200], is considered the best piece of art at Ajanta. It is most unfortunate that this fresco has been damaged to a great extent during the last one and a half centuries. Splendid reproductions of this picture have been made by some of the pioneer visitors. The scene is painted on the rear wall of the ante-chamber to the left of the door leading to the shrine.

The scene shows Yashodhara and Rahula, wife and son of the Buddha before he renounced the world, meeting the Lord, when the latter came begging to the palace-door during his sojourn at Nigro-dharama Vihaara on the outskirts of Kapila-vastu.

The sketch The Buddha in Kapila-vastu [Line 12] and the Mother and Child before the Buddha [Kbl 200] and its detail the Mother and Child [Kbl 201] a reproduction in colour aid in following the description of the mural.

To the left is the massive standing figure of the Buddha measuring about 3 metres in height. The features of his face are obliterated but his extended hand holding the begging bowl and the angle of his head towards Yashodhara, in fact his entire gesture shows his compassion mixed with love. The orange colour of his long robe contrasts well with the dark tint of the background, which is again relieved here and there of its sombreness and monotony by the sprinkling of white flowers. The golden-brown head stands out almost in relief against the light green of the halo. The same effect of alto-relievo sculpture is noticeable between the jade-green begging bowl and the orange robe and the brown head.

In front of the Buddha stands Yashodhara with young Rahula. Rahula was only seven days old when his father renounced the world. Now he is a boy of seven years. He looks up at his father for the first time in his life. In his look we find awe and wonder mingled with love and respect. The figure of Yashodhara is superb. The artist has attributed to her all the charms of natural beauty. She has bedecked herself profusely with ornaments, perhaps with the faint hope of enticing Him to return to her.

It is true that we cannot say, from the faintness of the figure, whether the Blessed One notices in front of him his wife and child, or just a mother and a boy - just two entities worthy of receiving His universal love and compassion. But Yashodhara's look speaks more of love than of wonder. The poses of the heads of the mother and the child in the direction of the figure of the Buddha exhibits purity of form.

The artist has deified the Buddha by increasing the dimension of the figure, but he has added some mystic effect by not drawing the outlines with any firm and distinct brush-work. The Buddha thus appears more like an ethereal entity, more like a formless idea than a human being. In contrast, the figures of the mother and the child, though diminutive in size, are clearly drawn in detail. They belong to the earth, full of worldly emotions and feelings.

Laurence Binyon said: "I remember no picture anywhere more profoundly impressive in grandeur and in tenderness."

The scene is depicted in relief sculpture in Cave 19.

Maatri-poshaka Jaataka

Cave 17; 6th Century, AD

Maatri-poshaka Jaataka is an exquisite composition painted on the rear wall of the cave between the first and the second cell-doors right of the ante-chamber. The narration in this cave varies slightly from the jaataka story.

The Jaataka: The story as painted runs on the following lines. Bodhi-sattva was born as a magnificent white elephant in the Himalayas. His both the parents were blind and the white elephant took care of them with utmost devotion. Once he helped a man who lost his way in the forest. King Brahma-datta of Varanasi had lost his royal elephant and was prepared to pay a large amount of money to anyone who helped him to get a suitable elephant. The greedy person informed the king of the beautiful mighty elephant he saw in the Himalayas. A happy king sent his army to catch the animal. The elephant, in spite of his might, allowed himself to be bound and brought to Varanasi. But in the royal stable where he was housed he refused to eat the sumptuous food offered to him. The astonished kind wanted to know the reason. He let loose the elephant and followed him along with his solders. On reaching the forest the elephant met his old, blind parents and caressed them lovingly. The king was overjoyed with the devotion shown by the animal to his parents and freed the animal in the forest.

In the jaataka, the elephant was supporting only his blind mother, and hence the title. (maatri-poshaka means ‘caring-for-the-mother’). But in the Ajanta narration the elephant was nursing both his mother and father.

The story is narrated in two rows, as given in the line sketch Parents-Caring Elephant (Line ?). It starts in the left of the top row with the person who was rescued by the elephant giving out the whereabouts of the white elephant to the king. The scene of the captured elephant being led to the city is painted at the left bottom of the mural. The elephant refusing to eat is shown on the right of the top row. The released animal walking majestically towards the forest is pictured above the captured elephant and the happy reunion is found to the right of the bottom row. The entire bottom row is shown as Capture, Freedom and Reunion [Nou 192], of which the Happy Reunion with the blind Parents [Kbl 207B] is a detail.

In the first scene, the king is sitting on a low throne with his queen standing by his side. A person standing to his left is giving information regarding the white elephant that he saw in the forest. A person, perhaps one of the ministers, is listening to the conversation; so do the two women from a window.

The next scene is shown at the left bottom of the lower row of Capture, Freedom and Reunion [Nou 192], where the captured elephant is led to Varanasi. He is brought under control with strong ropes tied around his hind legs. The elephant is preceded by two horse-riders and followed by a prince with an umbrella over his head.

The refusal of the elephant to eat is shown in the right of the top row. In this scene the king, seen at the centre of the panel, is supervising the feeding the elephant. The king is identified by the customary royal umbrella. To his right half hidden by the wall, the queen is peeping, perhaps watching the strange spectacle. The person to the right of the king is obviously informing him that the elephant is refusing to eat. The maid further to the right is carrying some edibles on a tray. Before the brooding elephant is seen some food in a large vessel and also sugar-canes are seen lying around.

The king, after having failed in his attempt to feed the elephant, orders the release of the animal, fearing that it may die of hunger. He has also ordered his solders to find the reason for the strange behaviour of the magnificent animal. His being let loose and passing through a gateway can be seen in the middle of the bottom row, Capture, Freedom and Reunion [Nou 192]. The animal happy gait of the elephant is unmistakable. The animal is preceded by a few soldiers and followed by a horse rider and a few foot-soldiers.

Finally, the Happy Reunion with blind Parents [Kbl 207B] is painted on the right of the bottom row. We see the white elephant sprinkling water over his parents, and announcing his return. Being blind the parents happily caress their son with their trunks.

The importance of the composition lies in the artist displaying what one could call human feelings to animals. We have the white elephant painted four times, each in a different emotional situation. When he is caught and brought to Varanasi, he is moving against his wish. When he refuses food his determination is evident. Next on being let loose, he trots happily to meet his parents. When he meets his parents his joy knows no bounds.

Mahisha Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th century, AD

The story of a compassionate buffalo and a mischievous monkey is painted on the right hand wall of the cave of the rear corridor, to the left of the cell door. (mahisha = buffalo)

The Jaataka: In one of his previous births, the Bodhi-sattva was born as a buffalo and lived in the Himalayas. In the same forest lived a foolish monkey, whose normal pranks included teasing the venerable buffalo. The benevolent one tolerated all such mischief. One day a savage buffalo was standing in the place usually occupied by the benevolent buffalo. Without realising it the monkey was upto his usual tricks. The buffalo did not take kindly to the teasing of the monkey, threw him and trampled upon him, in spite of the pleadings by the unfortunate monkey.

The story Mahisha Jaataka [Nou 207] is painted in two scenes. The first scene may be found at the bottom. The mischievous monkey is troubling the kind-hearted buffalo by sitting oh his back and closing his eyes. In the second scene, the monkey is in trouble for he has teased a wrong buffalo, which has reacted violently to the monkey’s pranks. The painting shows the monkey, being thrown down by the angry buffalo, pleading with his tormentor to forgive him.

The depiction of the scenes is characterised by vigour, spontaneity and energy.

Simhala Avadaana

Cave 17; 5th Century, AD

The mural, Simhala Avadaana, one of the largest compositions, covers the entire wall of the right hand corridor and continues on the upper part of the pilaster between this corridor and the one behind it.

The end of the archaic and the blossoming of the classical period cannot be better represented than by this composition. It is undoubtedly the boldest and the most grandiose composition in the history of Indian painting, a single composition of unparalleled power. This along with the paintings in Cave 16 and those along the two walls of the Hariti Shrine of Cave 2 represent Ajanta's classical peak.

The Story: Simhala was the courageous son of a wealthy merchant in the kingdom of Simha-kalpa. Against the wishes of his father, Simhala undertook a sea-voyage and was shipwrecked. He and the five hundred merchants who were travelling with him, were cast upon the shores of an island called Taamra-dveepa. The island was inhabited by man-eating raakshasi-s. They lured the stranded merchants disguising themselves as beautiful women only to devour them after taking their original form in the night. Bodhi-sattva in the form of a horse, called Bahala, took most of them to safety, by letting them cling to his body when he flew away from the island.

In the meantime, one of the raakshasi-s managed to reach Simha-kalpa and went to King Simha-kesari in the form of a beautiful maiden. She had a child with her, and complained that Simhala had married her and that the child was his, and that he had now deserted her. This was denied by Simhala. But, enchanted by her beauty, the king took her into his harem, in spite of the advice against this. The raakshasi, having managed to enter the palace summoned the other raakshasi-s from Taamra-dveepa. They finally killed the king and other inhabitants of the palace and ate them up.

The terrified people of Simha-kalpa made Simhala their king. He raised a huge army and invaded Taamra-dveepa. In the ensuing battle Simhala defeated the raakshasi-s. For this reason the island was renamed as Simhala-dveepa (the present day Sri Lanka).

The story begins at the bottom right, between the third and fourth cell-doors, where the shipwrecked Simhala accompanied by five hundred other merchants is depicted.

Above the shipwreck scene are shown the merchants, cast ashore, on an island of raakshasi-s being entertained by the latter, who have assumed beautiful forms to entice them. Raakshasi-s as beautiful Maidens [Kbl 211B] and a Raakshasi feigns Love [ASI 080] shows some of these seductive beauties. The raakshasi-s dally with the beguiled merchants inside well decorated pavilions. Six or seven such decorated pavilions are shown, inside which we see couples indulging in revelry. The scenes are depicted with zest. One of them is Merry Couples under Tents [Kbl 212] being a sample of the bhaava madhura (tender-feeling). The raakshasi-s are shown inside pavilions as beautiful women and outside in their fearsome form. One of the tents is shown in detail in Dallying Couple under a Tent [Kbl 213].

When the merchants come under their spell, cannibalistic Raakshasi-s [Kbl 214] take their real form and kill them with their curved daggers and dreadful fangs, and devour their flesh. On the left a pale-coloured raakshasi is seen with a severed leg in her hand, about to eat it. The one on the right is about to attack a merchant.

In the meantime Bodhi-sattva arrives on the scene in the form of a horse called Bahala. Apprehending danger and accepting the offer of the Bodhi-sattva horse, Simhala with his merchants manage to cling to the to the horse. In this Escape of Simhala [Kbl 216B] the flight of the horse is shown above the scenes of revelry over the fourth cell-door.

On reaching the kingdom of Simha-kalpa, alighting near a gateway, Merchants show their Gratitude to Bodhi-sattva [Kbl 217]

Having lost their prey, one of the raakshasi-s manages to come to Simha-kalpa. Taking the form of a ravishing beauty, she approaches the court of King Simha-kesari.

This is one of the most beautiful compositions in the history of Indian painting. The panel is unfortunately badly damaged. An excellent line diagramme of the panel Court Scene (Line ?) by Syed Ahmad and a colour reproduction the Infatuated King [Kbl 228/229] will be referred to in the following discussion. On the left (in the line sketch) we see the raakshasi arriving as a beauty of unsurpassable elegance. She is seen standing on a lotus with a large retinue of attendants carrying her toilet. The procession includes an elephant and other paraphernalia. We now move to the right. Within the pavilion the king is seen seated with the usual bolster at the back.

The king becomes enamoured with her beauty and takes her as his wife. But obviously the old minister had advised him against this act. The minister can be seen to the left of the king with his chin on a walking stick, suggesting that he failed in his attempt to dissuade the king from his hasty action. In the painting we find two court women at the rear window and two peeping from behind a partition, curiosity writ large on their faces.

The next scene is enacted beyond the pillar that is directly behind the brooding minister. Since the king has admitted the raakshasi-in-disguise we see a procession of damsels carrying royal presents for the wedding. Lastly, beyond the last pillar on the right we see the king with her newly married queen, who stands by his left with a child. We see the old minister still sulking at the turn of events.

Having managed to enter the palace the raakshasi-in-disguise summons her friends from Taamra-dveepa. The result is a Massacre in the Palace, [Kbl 221] painted on the extreme left of the wall. The harem presents a gruesome sight. On the left a red complexioned raakshasi is seen holding the hair of a hapless woman and is about to cut it off. Another to the right has thrown down a woman and is pressing her down with her foot. The one on the right has plunged a dagger into the abdomen of a victim. Just above this are found two women in panic, one covering her eyes and the other holding her hands to her breast.

The king is also devoured by the cunning raakshasi (not shown in the figure).

Having found a place for sumptuous food we see Vultures on the Gate [Kbl 223] and hovering over the bloody scene.

The panic-stricken people of Simha-kalpa crown Simhala as their king and request him to destroy the raakshasi-s. Simhala, the king, prepares for war against the raakshasi-s and the next scene is of Army gets Ready [Kbl 224].

The last portion, painted between the second and third cell-doors, is important for its magnificent composition, and, fortunately, because it is in a reasonable state of preservation. On the top-left, we find Simhala rides a white Elephant [ASI 078] with two generals riding on elephants beside him. Over the head of Simhala is the royal umbrella, signifying that he is now the king. The slanting spears, the waving flags, the forward lean of the elephant riders and the curved heads and huddled trunks of the elephants, all express the emotion of movement.

Below this scene the army is seen landing and at the bottom we see the cavalry landing. To its right is the scene showing the landing of Simhala and his generals, all riding caparisoned elephants, reaching the shore, the riders shooting the raakshasi-s with bows and arrows. The royal umbrella, festoons and spearheads form a gorgeous pattern. Moving to the right we see the fierce fighting, leading to the surrender of the raakshasi-s. Some of these demons can be seen with folded arms and some others touching the ground, both denoting surrender.

The final scene, portrayed above the surrendering raakshasi-s, is the coronation of Simhala and the royal celebration.

In the midst of these scenes portraying battles and the turmoil of war, the panel at the same time marvellously recreates an atmosphere of pleasure in the beauties of nature by means of a very simple and natural style, characteristic of the classical mode.

The men are heroic and the women wear the simplest coiffures, with usually a plain ribbon and a few flowers tucked in the hair. Their ornaments generally consists of a single strand pearl necklace, plain round earrings and sometimes a ring. Wigs were not known at the time although corkscrew curls were occasionally beginning to drop over the temples. The costumes are not intricate, and for that reason freely reveal their slender bodies. Both the men and women are generally represented as definite ethnic types, with a fair or reddish-brown complexion, which obviously points to northern influences.

Lady at her Toilet

Cave 17, 6th century, AD

There are three categories of murals in Ajanta: the first, picture-narratives the subject-matter of which is a jaataka or episodes from the life of the Buddha; the second, decorative designs, mostly found on the ceilings; and, the last, solo-pictures, self-contained with mythological or secular themes. It is possible that we may call some of these pictures secular, simply because we are not able to connect them with any religious theme.

Lady at her Toilet [Kbl 048], a solo-picture has amazingly escaped the ravages of time. It is painted on the upper part of the pilaster between the front and right corridors. A line sketch Toilet Scene (Line ?) is included for better appreciation.

It presents an aristocratic lady at her toilet with maids-in-attendance on both sides and a female dwarf further down. The central lady has an apparel of some gossamer-like fabric, almost transparent. The existence of this almost invisible fabric is deduced from the long folds of the lower garment. The lady is adorned with elaborate jewellery and she holds a mirror in her left hand and a champaka flower in her right. The beads of the pearl necklace have been drawn with thick pigment, as in an oil-painting, so that the gems are raised in relief.

The figure of the chair-bearer to the right, though tarnished and dark now, shows spontaneity, elegance and grace.

The artist has painted the lady with the mirror with an unusually slender waist and with the right foot placed somewhat awkwardly. Indian art prefers to depict feminine beauty not as the artist sees her in real life, but as she reveals her charm in his mental vision, endowed with all the attributes showered on her by the classical poets.

The Shilpa Shaastra enjoins "The beauty of a female figure, be that human or divine, should be enhanced by elongating the eyes, enlarging the breasts and reducing the girth at the waist". Thus, a beautiful woman shall have legs coming down like the trunks of a banana tree, and the palms and feet shall resemble the leaves of trees. This simile pada-pallava (feet-like-leaves) was what the artist attempted, and not the feet made of bones and muscles. Sadrishya (resemblance), one of the six limbs of painting, is to be in the realm of bhaava (emotional appeal) and not in the superficiality of roopa (form). Examples of such similitude are charana-kamala (lotus-like feet) and simha-kati (lion-waisted). See also the line sketch pada-pallava (feet-like-leaves).

In drawing the chawri-bearer, who is to the right of the lady, the artist seems to be swayed by the canonical injunctions of the shaastra-s. "She should have her body weight on one leg only, the other being softly touching the ground; she should have graceful curves, alluring contours and should look askance." The other maid also stands in the abhanga stance to balance the composition. The dwarf is certainly not beautiful; but she harmoniously mingles with the schematic composition and accentuates the charm of the three damsels by contrast.

The most notable feature of the painting is, however, the lovely chiaroscuro, shown in the splashes of light on the rose-coloured ranges of the conventional hills and the dark green shade of the foliage of the mango-tree forming the background.

King Shibi donates his Eyes

Shibi Jaataka

Cave 17, Late 5th century AD

The story of the Bodhi-sattva born as King Shibi who was hailed for his generosity is painted in this cave. We have seen in the Shibi Jaataka of Cave 1, the king cut off a part of his own flesh to a hawk to save a pigeon. In this episode, from the Shibi Jaataka of Cave 17, the king gives away his eyes to a blind Brahmin. The episode is painted on the pilaster between the front and the right corridors and continued on the on the wall of the right corridor.

The Jaataka: King Shibi, ruler of Arittha-pura, was famous all over the world for his observing the Buddhist virtues steadfastly. He distributed alms generously. He built six monasteries, one at each of the city’s gates, from where alms were distributed every day. Lord Indra wanted to test his generosity. He came to the king in the guise of a blind Brahmin and sought the king’s eyes as alms. Much to the protest of his subjects, the king had his eyes gouged out and presented them to the Brahmin gladly. The story of the generous King Shibi is popular in Hindu and Jaina lore.

The painting is greatly damaged. The earlier visitors have written about four or five scenes, but only a part is now extant. In one of them we can see the king after he has given away his eyes. This is shown as a line sketch King Shibi in Pain (Line ?) and two details of this scene Wailing Woman -1 [Kbl 227B/L] and Wailing Woman - 2 [Kbl 227T/L]. Also reproduced is a view of a Monastery [Kbl 227R].

Monastery [Kbl 227R] is a view of one of the six halls of alms built by the king to distribute alms to the needy. On the right a person is bringing some stock of grain for distribution. At the centre a royal attendant is pouring grain onto a piece of cloth held by a woman, who is happy to receive it. The one behind her is waiting for his turn. On the left there is a long queue of persons waiting and watching. In the background is a lady in monastic dress holding a tray in her left hand. The whole scene is fully animated. The scene depicts contemporary wooden architecture, and is shown realistically in normal perspective. We have seen that the Ajanta artist has chosen reverse perspective in the Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1 and in the Vidhura-pandita Jaataka in cave 2.

The scene King Shibi in Pain (Line ?), is subsequent to his giving away his eyes to Indra who came in the guise of a blind Brahmin. King Shibi may be seen sitting on a low throne. From his visage it is unmistakable that he is in great pain. He is supporting his body with his right-hand and is covering his left eye with the other hand. Tears and blood can be seen dripping from the exposed right eye. His sitting posture and his diadem proclaim his royalty. Detail of two women in sorrow are Wailing Woman -1 [Kbl 227B/L] and Wailing Woman - 2 [Kbl 227T/L]. The former is seen beating her chest and pulling her hair, and the pain in the other woman is depicted in her holding her head in her hand. The sorrowful mood of the maidens as well of the of the people around pervades the whole atmosphere. The grief of the women portrayed in this scene is similar to what we observed in the women shown in Shibi Jaataka of Cave 1.

Mriga Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th century, AD

An episode from Mriga Jaataka covers the front wall to the left of the window. (Mriga = deer)

The Jaataka: Once the Bodhi-sattva was born as a Golden Deer with a sweet voice. One day the deer found a man drowning. It swam across and saved him. When the man thanked it, the deer requested him to keep its existence secret. On reaching his place, Varanasi, he heard that the King of Varanasi had offered a reward for anyone who could tell him about the Golden Deer. The greedy person guided the king to the abode of the deer. Before the king could shoot it, the deer spoke, and the king was enchanted by its sweet voice. On hearing that the man whom it had saved has treacherously betrayed the Golden Deer, the king decided to punish the man with death. The deer intervened and asked for mercy. The king, then, took the Golden Deer to Varanasi and requested it to give a sermon to his wife.

The story starts on this panel after the greedy man returns to Varanasi. The complete mural is reproduced as a sketch in Story of a golden Deer (Line ?) and some of its scenes are reproduced in colour.

On the top, in the sketch, we can see the man giving out information about the Golden Deer to the king. The king is sitting cross-legged on an erected platform. The drummer on the right suggests that the proclamation of the reward has just been made. There is a group of people excitedly discussing the announcement.

For the next scene we need to go to the bottom of the panel. We see the King arrives in the Forest [Kbl 229] with his retinue, the abode of the Golden Deer. The normal commotion associated with such an expedition is portrayed very effectively. The artist has succeeded in capturing the majestic trot of the royal horse.

To the right we see a Hunter attempts to capture the Deer [Kbl 230]. The hunter is shown of reddish complexion, obviously to highlight him from the others in the crowd. He, while attempting to capture the deer, has his hands chopped off. The chopped hands can be seen just in front of the deer.

Here the Ajanta artist has added this dramatic event not found in the jaataka story. To signify the forest location, the artist has painted a lion and a lot of vegetation in the lower portion.

The king, seeing the miracle, comes to know the truth regarding the deer that it is none other than the Bodhi-sattva Himself. In the middle scene we see The King returns on a Horseback [Kbl 232] along with the Golden Deer carried on a Chariot [Kbl 231] with great honour. The humility in the visage is unmistakable. Even the horse has lowered its head in reverence. Since it is a ceremonial procession the umbrella is in attendance. In the previous scene the umbrella was missing, perhaps because while hunting the umbrella-bearer cannot keep pace with the king riding a horse. The deer in the chariot is majestic and composed, as one would be who is about to who would preach the Law of Dharma to the queen on reaching the palace.

Mention may be made here of a common practice in Ajanta composition. Very often episodes of a story are grouped according to the place of their occurrence and not according to their sequence of occurrence as can be seen in this narration. For example, on the top row are grouped together those scenes which took place in the palace, and the forest scenes depicted in the bottom row. We can see similar groupings even in the pre-Christian era paintings like the Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 10.

Mahaa-hamsa Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th Century, AD

Mahaa-hamsa Jaataka, the story of the golden goose must have been very popular at Ajanta because it is also depicted in Cave 2. In this vihaara the story is painted in two episodes on the left wall and the adjoining pilaster of the front corridor, to the right of the cell door.

The Jaataka: On Mount Chitra-koota, there lived a flock of geese and their king was King Dhritarashtra, the Bodhi-sattva born as a Golden Goose. On day Queen Khema of Varanasi dreamt of listening to a beautiful sermon by a golden goose. She wished to hear the complete sermon in person. Having heard about the Golden Goose, the king constructed a beautiful lake, planted five kinds of lotus, scattered different varieties of grain to attract the geese, and laid a snare. He also kept a fowler as a watch. The geese having heard about the beautiful lake sought permission from their king through their minister, Sumukha. The Golden Goose warned them of the danger. But they insisted, and the entire flock went to the lake. While they were enjoying the bounty of grains, their king uttered a cry of warning on sighting the snare. All the geese flew away leaving Sumukha, the minister and King Dhritarashtra. The king asked his minister to escape, but he refused. Seeing the unusual sight the fowler caught both the birds and took them to the court. They were both received with great honour. Then Bodhi-sattva preached the Law to the royal couple.

In the first part the hunters are seen catching the sacred bird and his chief adviser, the sacred golden goose, in the lotus lake. This panel is greatly damaged.

A Fowler brings the Geese to the Court [Kbl 183] is painted on the pilaster to the right to the court scene which is to follow. Refer also to the line diagramme Golden Goose brought to the Court [Line 08]. In this we see the fowler carrying two birds, one in each hand, and also a few birds flying away. In terms of style, the birds are painted in a similar vein to the hen in the scene Children play with a Hen [Kbl 127]. Although the firm brush strokes have given the birds in this cave, an appearance of solidity and firmness, the composition and their movements in the air are most natural.

The final part depicts a court scene in which the Golden Goose gives a Sermon [Kbl 184] to the Raja of Varanasi and his consort. The king's court where the Bodhi-sattva gives a sermon is reminiscent of the court scenes we have scene elsewhere in Ajanta - of King Mahaa-janaka of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1 and of King Sanjaya of Vishvaantara Jaataka in Cave 17 for example.

Cave Nineteen
Chaitya · Mahaayana period · 5th century AD

Cave Nineteen

Though there are no surviving paintings in this cave, this magnificent chaitya belonging to the Mahaayana period is taken for discussion because it is a treasure-house of sculpture. The relief sculpture on the facade, the naaga couple on the flanking wall to the facade, and the chaitya interior, particularly, the stoopa are worth studying in detail.

Facade [Nou 211]: The highly ornate facade belongs to the classical period (5th century AD). The facade was artistically conceived resulting in an aesthetic unity of architecture and sculpture.

Projecting from the main entrance and supported by two beautifully moulded pillars is the mukha-mandapa (the passage leading to the shrine).

The first set of sculpture flanking the entrance depicts the Buddha. Rahula receiving begging Bowl from the Buddha (ASI C) (to your right); the Buddha appears in his mendicant garb at the palace door at Kapila-vastu where his son Rahula, led by his mother Yashodhara, receives the begging bowl from him. His superhuman stature and spiritual expression indicate his peerless wisdom and compassion. The crown held over His hallowed head by angels further enhances this effect. A comparison with the famous painting, Mother and Child before the Buddha [Kbl 200] in Cave 17 would be rewarding. To the right of this, beyond the pilaster, the Buddha is again portrayed. He is standing in various mudra-s, on a double lotus against the drum of the stoopa under a finely carved makara torana (portal-of-crocodile) motif. Above this rises the anda (signifying the universe, literally, egg, symbolising that the universe sprang from the 'egg' of Brahma, the creator), harmika and the triple chhatra-vali (umbrellas), flanked by celestials.

The corresponding figure on the left side depicts Buddha in the Varada-mudra (ASI B), his figure steeped in karuna (compassion). On his right is a standing woman bereft of all ornaments and near his left foot is a prostrate woman. It is believed that these two are portrayals of Aamra-paali, a famous courtesan of Vaishali. According to Ashva-ghosha, Amra-paali appeared in white garments and devoid of body-paint and ornaments before the Buddha like a woman of good family at the time of worshipping and ‘prostrated her slender body like a blossoming creeper and stood up full of piety’. Both the Buddhas stand gracefully in slight tribhanga postures. Their being half-inclined inwards, and therefore, facing the devotee entering the shrine has to be intentional, with their benign expressions creating a feeling of assurance in the heart of the worshipper. Even in the absence of the painted plaster today, one can marvel at the plastic wonder - the soft mellifluous contours of the bodies, coupled with the charming expression exuding spirituality.

The panel on (your) left has two compartments: the upper one having a seated figure of the Buddha in dharma chakra pravartana mudra (setting-wheel-of-dharma-in-motion gesture) and the lower, a standing figure, in varada mudra (benediction-gesture).

The upper part of the Facade [Nou 211] is dominated by an extremely elegant chaitya-window. On either side of the window is found figures of richly-bedecked and majestically standing yaksha-s. One is shown as Kubera (ASI F). These two guardians stand in tribhanga posture with a udara bandha (tight-belt) around their bellies. The general contours of the body are soft and fleshy. The facial expression is calm and charming with a smile concealed under a thick upper lip. A dwarf attendant is emptying a bag of wealth. A Buddha figure on his crest is a unique feature.

Naaga Couple [Nou 212]: On the flanking wall at a right angle to the facade, the naaga-raja (king-of-the-serpents) is carved in the niche. It is a marvellous piece of sculpture, and a lovely example of the classical art of the 5th century, AD. The king of the serpents sits on a rocky platform in mahaa-raaja-leela (royal-sitting-posture-of-ease) with a seven-hooded cobra behind his head, in royal splendour, a figure of majesty and dignity, with an impressive crown. His queen, a small and lovely creature, leans lovingly against his shoulder, both at ease, each leg in a different pose, comfortable and informal. From behind emerges a maid-in-waiting, undraped, waving a fly-whisk in the background, a symbol of royalty. The bodies are beautifully modelled; the king is powerful with a well-built masculine body; the two women are gentle, delicate and lovely.

There is no excess, no overstatement anywhere. The entire composition is excellently balanced with the king dominating the scene and the maid being the least important. Indeed, this is a typical example which shows that the Indian relief work is often much more pictorial than sculptural. This is very much like a painting, set in a frame. If you examine the two pilasters which frame this ‘picture’ so handsomely, you will observe that the ornamentation on these pilasters is also very moderate, most of the shaft of the pillar is undecorated, simple. This is most characteristic of the classical style. The same is true of personal jewellery: the king and queen have fine crowns, no doubt, but otherwise they have only a few ornaments on their bodies: a necklace, a few bracelets, armlets for the king, and an anklet for the queen. The beauty, the dignity, the elegance of this tranquil scene of ‘royal portraiture’ is undisturbed by extraneous adornment. Here is classic idealisation, the classic love of beauty, simplicity and dignity.

Buddha in the Niche (ASI E): Opposite the naaga raja image is the figure of the Buddha in pralamba-paada (leg-hanging) posture and in dharma chakra pravartana mudra. Absorbed in contemplation, he is seated on a raised platform with his feet resting on a double lotus pedestal.

Chaitya Interior [Nou 25]: Chaitya interior is an architectural and sculptural treasure, with the lavish sculptural decoration on pillars, capital and triforium and a standing image of the Buddha against the stoopa within a highly ornamented niche. On the capitals and the triforium are niches containing Buddha figures in dhyana mudra (meditating-attitude) separated from each other by a variety of foliage designs. The stoopa itself attracts the visitor and holds him spellbound by its artistic charm. In contrast to the stoopa-s in caves 9 and 10, this stoopa shows advanced characteristic with base-mouldings, a round anda and a tall harmika with tapering triple chhatravali-s supported by yaksha-s and surmounted by a poorna ghata (ceremonial-jar), reaching to the vaulted ceiling. The serenity and charm of the central Buddha dominate the warm spiritual atmosphere of the prayer-chamber.

Cave Twenty-six
Chaitya · Large sculptural ensemble

Cave Twenty-six

This chaitya follows cave 19 in general arrangement and decoration, but is considerably larger. This cave is also taken for discussion for its sculptural details, and the variations in approach to sculptural decoration.

In this cave the departure from the earlier tradition of decorating the interior walls with painting has been replaced by a large number of small and large sculptures panels. However, the ornamentation, executed in meticulous detail, gives the feeling of excess and a lack of the graceful proportions and rhythmic correlation of Cave 19.

Mention may also be made on changes in the structure and the decoration of the stoopa from the earlier ones. The stoopa proper has been relegated to the background, the emphasis being more on the elongated and decorated plinth. In the front of it is carved with a figure of the Buddha seated in the pralamba paada (hanging-legs) attitude under a pavilion.

There are two sculptural compositions, which arrest our attention. Both of them are found on the left wall. One is a colossal reclining Buddha depicting His Mahaa-parinirvaana [Nou 215]. The other is Temptations of the Buddha - Sculpture (Line ?), a composition in painting of the same theme, Temptations of the Buddha [Nou 048] has been painted in Cave 1, a comparison between that and this sculpture was made while discussing the painting.

Mahaa-parinirvaana [Nou 215]: Near the side door, is a colossal figure representing Mahaa-parinirvaana (Final-Extinction) of the Buddha at Kushi-nagara. The Buddha is reclining on the right side on a couch between two shaala trees. The right hand under his head is placed over a pillow. The folds of the robe and the fingernails are realistic. Apart from the softness of the pillow and other realistic details of the limbs and dress what appeals is the bhaava of the composition. Buddha’s Nirvana [Nou 008] exudes calm and total peace.

Below are the figures of His disciples mourning His pari-nirvaana and above are celestial beings rejoicing at the prospect of being with the Lord. At a niche beyond the feet of the Master, Ananda [Kbl 047], the most favoured disciple of the Buddha, is resting his on his right hand and weeping bitterly.

The longest figure of the Buddha in the entire series dominates the composition. The usual problem of frontality has been eliminated by intelligently placing the lower figures with their backs and side profiles towards the viewers. The panel is also important for it makes the beginning of a new method of stuccowork on the sculptures, which are further painted.

Temptations of the Buddha: The second, on the same wall, depicts, Temptations of the Buddha - Sculpture (Line ?) by Mara. The Buddha is seated, with his right palm in the bhumi-sparsha-mudra (earth-touching-gesture) under the Bodhi tree at the centre. On the left is Mara on an elephant accompanied by his host of demon-forces attacking the Buddha. On the right is the retreat of Mara. The lower panel shows the three Daughters of Mara (ASI L), Rati (lust), Trishna (thirst) and Arati (delight) tempting the Buddha with their bewitching charm. Their coquettish gestures, attractive dance and melodious music - all failed to tempt Gautama and swerve him from the path of Enlightenment. Here the Ajanta-sculptor excels in the modelling of the danseuse in her tribhanga (triflex) pose with intricately carved headgear, earrings, necklaces and her orchestra comprising the percussionists and a lady playing on cymbals. They are then shown seated on the right lower portion with their daughters, dejected at the failure of their mission.

The story-telling quality of the sculpture, the unity of the composition and the ingenious disposition of the figures attest to the mastery of the sculptor in his art equalling that of his brother artist in painting. The Ajanta painter has narrated the same scene, Temptations of the Buddha [Nou 048] in Cave 1 and a comparison between the two would be rewarding. This panel sets the trend towards the bas-reliefs in the round as seen in Ellora and later at Elephanta.
