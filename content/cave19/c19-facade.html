Facade [Nou 211]: The highly ornate facade belongs to the classical period (5th century AD). The facade was artistically conceived resulting in an aesthetic unity of architecture and sculpture.

Projecting from the main entrance and supported by two beautifully moulded pillars is the mukha-mandapa (the passage leading to the shrine).

The first set of sculpture flanking the entrance depicts the Buddha. Rahula receiving begging Bowl from the Buddha (ASI C) (to your right); the Buddha appears in his mendicant garb at the palace door at Kapila-vastu where his son Rahula, led by his mother Yashodhara, receives the begging bowl from him. His superhuman stature and spiritual expression indicate his peerless wisdom and compassion. The crown held over His hallowed head by angels further enhances this effect. A comparison with the famous painting, Mother and Child before the Buddha [Kbl 200] in Cave 17 would be rewarding. To the right of this, beyond the pilaster, the Buddha is again portrayed. He is standing in various mudra-s, on a double lotus against the drum of the stoopa under a finely carved makara torana (portal-of-crocodile) motif. Above this rises the anda (signifying the universe, literally, egg, symbolising that the universe sprang from the 'egg' of Brahma, the creator), harmika and the triple chhatra-vali (umbrellas), flanked by celestials.

The corresponding figure on the left side depicts Buddha in the Varada-mudra (ASI B), his figure steeped in karuna (compassion). On his right is a standing woman bereft of all ornaments and near his left foot is a prostrate woman. It is believed that these two are portrayals of Aamra-paali, a famous courtesan of Vaishali. According to Ashva-ghosha, Amra-paali appeared in white garments and devoid of body-paint and ornaments before the Buddha like a woman of good family at the time of worshipping and ‘prostrated her slender body like a blossoming creeper and stood up full of piety’. Both the Buddhas stand gracefully in slight tribhanga postures. Their being half-inclined inwards, and therefore, facing the devotee entering the shrine has to be intentional, with their benign expressions creating a feeling of assurance in the heart of the worshipper. Even in the absence of the painted plaster today, one can marvel at the plastic wonder - the soft mellifluous contours of the bodies, coupled with the charming expression exuding spirituality.

The panel on (your) left has two compartments: the upper one having a seated figure of the Buddha in dharma chakra pravartana mudra (setting-wheel-of-dharma-in-motion gesture) and the lower, a standing figure, in varada mudra (benediction-gesture).

The upper part of the Facade [Nou 211] is dominated by an extremely elegant chaitya-window. On either side of the window is found figures of richly-bedecked and majestically standing yaksha-s. One is shown as Kubera (ASI F). These two guardians stand in tribhanga posture with a udara bandha (tight-belt) around their bellies. The general contours of the body are soft and fleshy. The facial expression is calm and charming with a smile concealed under a thick upper lip. A dwarf attendant is emptying a bag of wealth. A Buddha figure on his crest is a unique feature.

Naaga Couple [Nou 212]: On the flanking wall at a right angle to the facade, the naaga-raja (king-of-the-serpents) is carved in the niche. It is a marvellous piece of sculpture, and a lovely example of the classical art of the 5th century, AD. The king of the serpents sits on a rocky platform in mahaa-raaja-leela (royal-sitting-posture-of-ease) with a seven-hooded cobra behind his head, in royal splendour, a figure of majesty and dignity, with an impressive crown. His queen, a small and lovely creature, leans lovingly against his shoulder, both at ease, each leg in a different pose, comfortable and informal. From behind emerges a maid-in-waiting, undraped, waving a fly-whisk in the background, a symbol of royalty. The bodies are beautifully modelled; the king is powerful with a well-built masculine body; the two women are gentle, delicate and lovely.

There is no excess, no overstatement anywhere. The entire composition is excellently balanced with the king dominating the scene and the maid being the least important. Indeed, this is a typical example which shows that the Indian relief work is often much more pictorial than sculptural. This is very much like a painting, set in a frame. If you examine the two pilasters which frame this ‘picture’ so handsomely, you will observe that the ornamentation on these pilasters is also very moderate, most of the shaft of the pillar is undecorated, simple. This is most characteristic of the classical style. The same is true of personal jewellery: the king and queen have fine crowns, no doubt, but otherwise they have only a few ornaments on their bodies: a necklace, a few bracelets, armlets for the king, and an anklet for the queen. The beauty, the dignity, the elegance of this tranquil scene of ‘royal portraiture’ is undisturbed by extraneous adornment. Here is classic idealisation, the classic love of beauty, simplicity and dignity.

Buddha in the Niche (ASI E): Opposite the naaga raja image is the figure of the Buddha in pralamba-paada (leg-hanging) posture and in dharma chakra pravartana mudra. Absorbed in contemplation, he is seated on a raised platform with his feet resting on a double lotus pedestal.
