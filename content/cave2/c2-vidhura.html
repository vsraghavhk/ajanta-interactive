Vidhura-pandita Jaataka

Cave 2, 5th Century, AD

This jaataka tale is the story of Bodhi-sattva born as Vidhura-pandita who demonstrated his supreme wisdom as a minister in the court of King Dhananjaya. Several episodes from Vidhura-pandita Jaataka occupy a large portion of the right wall of the hall, above and between the second and third cell doors.

By the end of the 5th century AD, the classical upsurge of the Ajanta genius represented by the masterpieces in Caves 16 and 17 and the Hariti Shrine of Cave 2, began to give way to what has been called mannerism which later deteriorated into the baroque style. The latter style may be seen in the Mahaa-janaka Jaataka of Cave 1.

Passing from the classical to the manneristic mode, there were alterations in composition and in figure-drawing and decoration. The broad vision is progressively dissected into compartments so that the narrative is painted, not in an idyllic landscape, but in a maze of pillars of royal pavilions, palace windows, porches and balconies, city walls, shops and monasteries. Scenes became crowded with people, who are sophisticated, though they are not yet affected or effeminate. The men are still manly and the women feminine and they wear simple clothes and few ornaments.

For postures, expressions and movements characteristic of this period, it is difficult to match the episodes of the Vidhura-pandita Jaataka. Mention was made about the Ajanta artist's knowledge of perspectives, and his employing both the normal perspective and the reverse perspective. Certain scenes in the narration are depicted in the reverse perspective.

Since this is one of the surviving paintings where sufficient portions are discernible, let us first narrate the story before attempting to explain the painting.

The Jaataka: Bodhi-sattva was born in Indra-prastha and became a minister under King Dhananjaya belonging to the Kuru dynasty. He was universally admired for his wisdom. Once he delivered a sermon in the court of King Varuna, king of the naaga-s. Impressed with his discourse, the king presented him with a costly piece of jewellery. This was not to the liking of his queen, Vimala. She feigned illness and said she would die unless his heart was brought to her as a proof of his demise. The distraught king proclaimed that he would give his beautiful daughter Irandati in marriage to anyone who would perform this task. The king entreated his daughter to seek a brave husband. Once a yaksha general called Punyaka, while riding past in heaven on his magic horse, heard the sweet song of Irandati, and fell in love with her. He promised to get the heart of Vidhura-pandita. Punyaka was aware of King Dhananjaya’s weakness for the game of dice and enticed him to a game. In the ensuing game Punyaka won from the king Vidhura-pandita as a wager. On the way to the naaga world Punyaka made a number of unsuccessful attempts to kill Vidhura-pandita to get his heart. Finally, Vidhura-pandita volunteered to tell him the way to get his heart. Punyaka, taken aback at his generosity, brought Vidhura-pandita alive to the court of King Varuna. Both Varuna and his queen were enchanted by Vidhura-pandita’s wisdom and made him to stay as their guest for a few days before returning to the Kuru kingdom.

Now let us try to follow the story. We must remember that the painting sequence in Ajanta does not follow a chronological order. This we have seen in other panels as well.

The first three scenes are painted in the extreme right. The first two scenes are shown in Irandati meets Punyaka ( Kbl 130) and the third scene is King Varuna's Palace Scene [Kbl 131]. The following explanation is supported by a line sketch Punyaka’s Arrival [line 13] and the above two colour pictures.

In the centre of the line drawing, and on to the right of Irandati meets Punyaka ( Kbl 130), we find Princess Irandati on a Swing. This is one of the most beautiful compositions of Ajanta. The background is embellished with floral designs. The movement of the swing is aptly conveyed by the curves of the ropes and the streaming flags attached to the swing. The outstretched leg of the girl with toes brought together is a realistic rendering of a pendulum-like movement. According to the jaataka, while swinging carefree, Irandati was also singing in a sweet voice. The swing plays an important role in Indian cultural and social life, and has been extolled as romantic in Indian literature.

Punyaka, a yaksha general, who was flying on the back of a winged horse, hears her song and is attracted by it. Coming down he introduces himself. This scene is painted to the left of the previous scene. The separation of this composition into two scenes follows the Ajanta convention of placing a post or a pillar between two scenes. In this second scene she is standing and leaning against a post, talking to Punyaka. Obviously Punyaka has proposed marriage to the princess. Punyaka’s horse is by his right side.

Now the action shifts to the palace, depicted on the other side of the swing. This part is also shown in the line drawing Punyaka’s Arrival [Line 13]. In this King Varuna's Palace Scene [Kbl 131], we find the king seated along with his son on a slightly raised platform talking to his wife and his daughter on the proposal of marriage. The king and his son have serpent-hoods over their heads indicative of their naaga royalty. The son is in a contemplative mood. Irandati is talking as can be seen from her hand gestures. The rich hair-dresses of the characters and the textiles in this scene, particularly the cloth on the bolster, would provide us information for the study of the textile designs of the day.

Next we witness Queen Vimala and Princess Irandati having an intimate conversation regarding the marriage. This is painted above the third cell door to the right of the scene of Vidhura-pandita giving a Sermon, which is the last scene. The conversation takes place on a balcony. The princess looks coy and abashed, and the mother, we find from her gestures, is giving her some advice and, perhaps, assurance.

After the marriage Punyaka sets out to kill Vidhura-pandita and to bring back his heart. Punyaka knows King Dhananjaya's weakness for gambling.

Left to the second cell door and above it we see four main scenes. each enclosed in a pavilion, depicting Some Episodes from Vidhura-pandita Jaataka [Kbl 129]. Details of each of the scenes are also given and we shall discuss these scenes in a chronological order.

On the left side of the top left panel we have the scene called Game of Dice [Kbl 132]. The scenes are shown as line drawings in Game of Gambling [Line 11]. In this we see Punyaka enticing Dhananjaya to a game of dice and the game being played. On the left we see Punyaka showing a magnificent jewel as a wager to tempt King Dhananjaya to a game. The king is occupying a low seat; the cloth covering the seat is striped. He has made some gesture with his right hand whereas his left hand is resting on the left knee. Vidhura-pandita, sitting to the left of the king, is looking at Punyaka with concern. Below the king on his right is a door-keeper with a moustache and a long coat with tight sleeves. This was possibly the usual livery of doorkeepers, for we find the doorkeepers of Mahaa-hamsa Jaataka in the same cave, dressed similarly as also the one in Conversion of Nanda in Cave 1. In front of the doorkeeper, below the throne of the king there are two more figures, one male and the other female, the latter with the back to us. They are possibly personal attendants to the king. Both of them are in great animation.

To the right, King Dhananjaya and Punyaka are seen engaged in a game of dice. The king is seen on the left and Punyaka on the right with the game-board between them. The king is at the point of throwing the dice. Punyaka is serious and anxiously waiting for the dice to show up. The queen, painted below the king, is appealing to Punyaka, perhaps to stop the dangerous game.

The artist has adopted the technique of reverse perspective to draw the board of dice in minute detail showing clearly all the twenty-four divisions in two rows. The three dice are lying at the end of the king's outstretched hand. Such detail would not have been possible if normal perspective was followed. We have seen another example of this technique of reverse perspective in the building behind the dancer in Mahaa-janaka Jaataka.

The king loses the game, and, thus, his minister. In the next scene, Vidhura-pandita counsels Dhananjaya [Kbl 133], painted below the Game of Dice [Kbl 132], we see King Dhananjaya and his minister in conversation. The king rather hopes that the rules of the game will not allow him to wager his minister. But the truthful minister Vidhura-pandita counsels Dhananjaya that a minister is a legal wager, and thus he had now become the property of Punyaka. The scene has been somewhat damaged and hence the rest of the figures in this scene are not clearly seen.

Before leaving for the naaga-kingdom with his new master, Vidhura-pandita gives a Farewell Sermon [Kbl 134] in the court of King Dhananjaya. This scene is depicted to the right of the scene of Vidhura-pandita counselling his king. Between the two scenes is shown a balcony in which the queen is talking to a maid, perhaps telling her to request Vidhura-pandita to give a sermon before leaving. While giving the farewell sermon the minister is holding a lotus flower on his right hand symbolising his divinity. In this masterly composition, we see Vidhura-pandita as a personification of love and compassion. We have the king before him listening to the sermon in rapt attention. Behind Vidhura-pandita there are three graceful maidens with sorrowful faces at the imminent loss of such a saintly person.

Below this is a long panel depicting the Departure of Vidhura-pandita [Nou 137]. Similar in composition to the marching of Simhala in the Simhala Avadaana panel, the majestic movement of the horses in various hues, the stately gait of the caparisoned elephants, the variety of sthaana-s (body-postures) of the solders, their weapons etc are drawn with great skill.

According to the story, Punyaka takes the minister on his flying horse and flies towards the naaga-loka (serpent-kingdom). On the way he attempts to kill Vidhura-pandita to take out his heart, but fails. It is then that the compassionate Vidhura-pandita explains to him how he may be killed and his heart taken out. Ashamed of his proposed evil act Punyaka brings Vidhura-pandita alive to the naaga court.

The last scene is enacted in the kingdom of the naaga-s. This episode, painted to the right of the scene Game of Dice [Kbl 132], shows Vidhura-pandita in the Naga Kingdom [Kbl 136] preaching before an assemblage of naaga-s, headed by King Varuna himself. The major portion of the scene is given in the sketch, Vidhura-pandita in Naaga-loka [Line 14]. The teacher has, as usual, taken the central position and is practically the pivot of the composition. He is seen seated on a low stool, interlaced with tape. He has a tilak (decorative mark) on the forehead.

In front of the revered guest is the naaga-king with folded hands bowing his head in supplication. The halo of five serpents makes his identification easier. Besides the king we see two ladies, evidently Queen Vimala and Princess Irandati, listening to the sermon. They do not have their hoods now. Punyaka's attention is not on the discourse; he seems to be eyeing his sweetheart, Irandati.

The balcony scene to the right of this has already been described as Queen Vimala and Princess Irandati [Kbl 137]. Below this balcony is a window through which we see a couple. Some identify them as Vidhura-pandita, holding a lotus in his hand, and Queen Vimala.

After such a long description we may go back to the discussion on the art style of this narration. The reason for this extended discussion is that the mural exhibits characteristics of both the classical and the later baroque periods. Interestingly certain aspects of the composition resemble the pre-classical style. Thus in terms of art style this is really unique.

The facial features are similar to those in the pre-Christian era painting of Cave 10, like the Raja with his Retinue [Mjt 106]. The golden-brown complexion, round faces, full red lips, short, but pointed noses are considered to be Southern and non-Aryan. So also the veil over the women's head and the elaborate turbans on the heads of the men-folk. These are found in some measure in this painting. There are no exaggerations like bulging body-curves and elongated eyes. Men and women wear simple dresses and are bare above the waist.

On the other hand the spatial arrangement is not classical, at least in some scenes like the scene in which Punyaka entices Dhananjaya. That the narration takes place 'in a maze of pillars of royal pavilions, palace windows, porches and balconies' betrays its deviation from the classical idyllic landscape. Though a variety of body-positions are shown there is certain amount of monotony. This is seen in the scene, Punyaka enticing Dhananjaya in the panel, Game of Dice [Kbl 132] and in the panel Vidhura-pandita gives a Farewell Sermon [Kbl 134], the two adjacent panels.
