Cave Two

This vihaara, although slightly smaller, has a similar plan to that of Cave 1. The verandah is about 2.5 meters wide and has a length of about 14 meters. The facades of the chapels at each end of the verandah are carved with figures of the naaga kings (serpent-kings) and their attendants, the portly gana-s (demigods). A richly carved doorway flanked by a decorated window on either side, leads to the main congregation hall. The congregation hall is about 14.5 meters square and the roof is supported by twelve massive, elaborately carved pillars. In the View of the Interior of Cave 2 [Kbl 019], we can see the pillars having very minute carvings and painted all over. In many pillars the paintings on the bases are still discernable as may be seen in the View of the Main Hall [Nou 040]. Rich tapestry of the paintings on the ceiling may be seen both the views. There are ten cells off four corridors. At the rear of the hall there is an ante-chamber leading to the shrine.

Main Shrine [Nou 020]: The entrances to the ante-chamber and to the shrine are richly carved. On the frames are sculpted a number of mithuna-figures, and the lower-most figures on both the sides are carved majestic gate-keeping yaksha-s. Towards the ceiling are images of the river goddesses, Ganga and Yamuna. The shrine contains a large figure of the Buddha in the dharma chakra pravartana mudra (turning-the-Wheel-of-Religion-attitude). He is flanked by two figures of Bodhi-sattva acting as chaamara-bearers. The painting on the ceiling of this shrine, Squares and Circle - 3 [ASI 048], is in a better state of preservation. On the ceiling of the ante-chamber we have in the ceiling painting Squares and Circles - 6 [Kbl 139B] whose motif is very interesting.

On either side of the ante-chamber is a chapel, the entrances to which are adorned with delicately-carved pillars. The chapel on the right is the Hariti shrine and is important because of its sculpture.

Hariti Shrine [Nou 035]: The Hariti shrine is the one to the right of the ante-chamber. The story of the conversion of the cannibal Hariti into the guardian-deity of the born infants is represented in a synoptic manner. Hariti and her consort Panchika are seated side by side, with the chaamara-bearers on either side, and a woman with a bird perched on her left hand and a flower in her right hand, between them. Two incidents of the former life of the Hariti are carved in the background, one each on either side above the whisk-bearers. On the right she is aggressively arguing with the Buddha to restore her child purposefully concealed by the Master. In the other she listens attentively to the words of wisdom of the Master, who advises her to give up cannibalism and to take up the protection of the children of rajagriha-s.

On the pedestal is an interesting scene of a classroom revealing the Ajanta artist's sense of humour. Starting from the right we find three sensitive boys engrossed in writing before a teacher holding a cudgel. The next two boys are boxing. And lastly, a group of five boys is goading two rams to fight.

The cave has the best ceiling paintings that have survived. On the ceiling of this shrine we find a beautiful ceiling decoration.

Paintings: The cave contains some very important paintings. The paintings in the ‘Hariti’ shrine are a testimony to the highest classical taste of Ajanta. The narration of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka belong to the baroque period. Finally, the paintings on the ceiling are the best in the whole Ajanta complex.

Among the important surviving paintings are:

Gate in King Brahma-datta’s Palace [Mjt 097], a scene from the Mahaa-hamsa Jaataka of Cave 2, painted on the left wall of the front verandah, is an example of normal perspective.

Nativity of the Buddha, many scenes of which somehow survived, is painted on the left wall. The narration starts with the Future Buddha in Tushita Heaven [Kbl 120], wherein we find the gods request Him to be born as the Buddha. Among the important surviving scenes of this story are, the King listens to the Interpretation of the Dream [Kbl 122T], Queen Mahaa-maaya musing [Kbl 122B] about the astrologer’s interpretation, and, finally, the Birth of the Buddha [Kbl 123].

Ajanta classicism at best can be enjoyed in the painting called Votaries with Offerings [Kbl 126] found on the walls of the Hariti shrine. Belonging to the same period and painted on the same Hariti shrine are the compositions Children playing with Hen [Kbl 127] and Child trying to snatch a Toy [ASI 46].

On the left-hand wall of the ante-chamber of the shrine is painted, Thousand Buddhas [Kbl 118], a scene from the Miracle of Shraavasti of Cave 2. The painting belongs to the period of artistic decline at Ajanta.

On the other hand, the representation of the Images of the Buddhas [Nou 133] is painted on the rear wall of the cave and is artistically superior to the one earlier described.

On the wall of the right corridor are painted a number of scenes from the Vidhura-pandita Jaataka. The scenes are Irandati meets Punyaka [Kbl 130], King Varuna’s Palace Scene [Kbl 131], a compact composition depicting Some Episodes from Vidhura-pandita Jaataka [Kbl 129]. These episodes are Game of Dice [Kbl 132], Vidhura-pandita counsels Dhananjaya [Kbl 133], Vidhura-pandita gives Farewell Sermon [Kbl 134], Queen Vimala and Princess Irandati [Kbl 137] and Vidhura-pandita in Naga Kingdom [Kbl 136]. The last painting is Departure of Vidhura-pandita [Nou 137].

Ceiling Paintings: This cave abounds in decorative designs painted on pillars, pilasters, ceilings, and for that matter, on every available surface. The motifs adopted for decoration are fruits (mangoes, pineapples, pears, grapes, berries, lichies, etc), flowers (lotus, waterlilies, champaka, etc), birds (geese, ducks, cranes, peacocks, parrots, etc), fantastic and sportive figures (dwarfs, buffoons, jugglers, foreigners in exotic garments, etc) and pure geometric patterns. For the geometric patterns, the most popular composition being a square containing a number of concentric circular bands around a central lotus-design, with the four corners - called spandrels - embellished with mithuna-s (couples), birds or mythical beings. The circular bands, in turn, are filled with wreaths of flowers. The best preserved of the ceiling paintings are in the sanctum.

Squares and Circle - 3 [ASI 048] has flying cherubs in the spandrels, while mithuna-s are painted in the spandrels of Squares and Circles - 7 [Kbl 139T] and Detail of Squares and Circles - 7 [Kbl 140]. In the ceiling painting Squares and Circles - 6 [Kbl 139B] the row of geese in a ring is a superb rendering.

As in Cave 1, the ceiling decoration consists of filling up the space with squares and rectangle. Belonging to this mode of decoration is Squares and Rectangles - 2 [ASI 047].

The ceiling also contain some scenes in a lighter vein, like, the Foreigners drinking [ASI 043].
