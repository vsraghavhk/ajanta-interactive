Nativity of the Buddha

Cave 2, 5th Century, AD

Several scenes depicting the Nativity of the Buddha have been painted along the left wall of the hall above the lintel and also along the sides of the first and second cell doors.

The Story: First we shall follow the story of the birth of the Buddha as depicted in this cave. The Future Buddha was living in Tushita heaven. All the gods from the 'thousand worlds' requested Him to be born as the Buddha. The Great Being consented and informed them of the time, place and the family for his final birth. Thus during the mid-summer festival Queen Mahaa-maaya, queen of King Suddhodana of Kapila-vastu, had a dream. In this dream she saw a white elephant entering her womb through her right side. She related this dream to her husband, who consulted the revered Brahmins to interpret the dream. They said that the queen had conceived a male child, who would either become a Universal Monarch or become the Buddha. For confinement Queen Mahaa-maaya proceeded to the residence of her parents. Midway, in the beautiful grove of shaala trees, called Lumbini Grove, she stood holding the branch of a shaala tree and gave birth to a son. Brahma, Indra and other gods descended on to the grove to pay their respects to the newborn. 'The Baby looked around in all directions, and strode forward seven paces, followed by Mahaa-Brahma, holding over him a white umbrella'.

Some of the painted scenes are damaged by fire. The dream of Queen Mahaa-maaya is faintly visible. The scenes of a Brahmin interpreting the dream, the Queen musing about the interpretation and finally, the birth itself are more clearly discernible.

The first scene, Future Buddha in Tushita Heaven [Kbl 120], is enacted in the Tushita heaven, and painted above the second cell-door. Seated in pralamba (sitting-with-legs-hanging) pose hands striking dharma chakra pravartana mudra (teaching-attitude), He is surrounded by other deities and is making the announcement that He will be descending to the earth to be born to Queen Mahaa-maaya. The portrayal of the Master is not one of the best in Ajanta. The direct frontal pose without any inclination of the head is a rare feature in Ajanta art. Further the treatment of the whole composition lacks imagination and creativity. On the other hand the other scenes in the narrative are nor marred by this deficiency.

The next scene in which Queen Mahaa-maaya is dreaming is badly damaged, and is barely discernable. Hence we are constrained to move on to the next scene.

This scene in which the King listens to the Interpretation of the Dream [Kbl 122T] by a Brahmin called Asita, is painted between the front pilaster and the third cell door. (Refer also to the sketch King listens to the Astrologer – Line 15)

The royal couple, Suddhodana and Mahaa-maaya are seated on two thrones and Asita, the astrologer, squats in front of them having made a loop with his fingers. His long twisted moustache is prominent. He is telling them that the queen has conceived a son destined to become either a universal monarch or a universal teacher. There are three female attendants at the back of the throne, the one on the extreme left being a chaawri-bearer. She looks at the queen. The other two are looking in different directions. There are three more female attendants further down. All these nine figures are enclosed in a framework formed by the vertical pillars, the foreground and the architrave on the top.

Moving further right, beyond a pillar used by the artist to start a new scene, we find the figure of Queen Mahaa-maaya musing [Kbl 122B] over the prediction. (This scene is contained in the line sketch.) Here she stands, leaning against a pillar with her left leg touching it. We now see her musing over the exciting augury. That she is baffled is evident from her expression and the loop made by her fingers. Two more attendants look at her with anxiety written on their faces, but the queen appears to be unaware of their presence. In drawing the figure of the queen the artist is chiefly concerned with giving expression to her serenity mingled with a bashful smile expected of a would-be mother, and her anxiety for the safety of the life taking shape within her. Nevertheless, he drew in details the rich jewellery, elaborate coiffure and floral decoration over the chignon. The lady is very scantily dressed, particularly in contrast to the two attendants in this scene. This feature of being ‘dressed in nakedness’ is a common, but intriguing feature at Ajanta. Her legs resemble the trunk of banana-tree, following the established canons of the time.

The Birth of the Buddha [Kbl 123], is the next scene, painted on the space above the third and fourth cell-doors. The holy birth takes place in the Lumbini Grove. We see Queen Mahaa-maaya standing holding on to a branch of a shaala tree. The pose of the queen is very similar to that in the previous scene. In front of the queen are Mahaa-Brahma who holds a white umbrella. To his right is Indra respectfully holding the baby. By the side stands a chaawri-bearer showing great excitement. There are a few more figures in the group. To heighten the effect, the artist has taken liberties to incorporate gateways, buildings, compounds, etc, though these things are hard to imagine in a grove situated along a road.

The next scene in which the Holy Baby strides forward seven paces is badly damaged.
