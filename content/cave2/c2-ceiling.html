Ceiling Paintings

Every available space on the ceilings and on the pillars has been painted. This cave contains the best decorations on the ceilings. The compositions of these ceiling paintings are, like in the other caves, of two major types. One is a square containing a number of concentric circular bands around a central lotus-design, with the four corners - called spandrels - embellished with mithuna-s (couples), birds or mythical beings. The circular bands, in turn, are filled with wreath of flowers. The best preserved of the ceiling paintings are in the sanctum. This kind is referred to as ‘Squares and Circles’ in the present discussion.

The other is filling up the space with squares and rectangles, in which are painted various motifs. These are being referred to as ‘Squares and Rectangles’ in the discussion.

Fruits, birds, animals and pure geometric patterns are the motifs used in the above. The fruits, which have been depicted, are fruits, mangoes, pineapples, pears, grapes, berries, lichies, etc. We have birds like, geese, ducks, cranes, peacocks, parrots, etc. There are also mythical animals of a wide variety. In a lighter vein we have sportive figures, like, dwarfs, buffoons, jugglers, foreigners in exotic garments, etc. And, finally, there are pure geometric patterns.

Apart from the colour harmony and delicacy of the designs, even a casual observer can not miss the movement and plasticity of forms.

The painting on the ceiling of the main shrine, Squares and Circle - 3 [ASI 048], is in a better state of preservation. The central circle contains a large, white and serene lotus, symbolising purity. The ring around it is a wreath. The one next is of some geometrical pattern. Filling up the spandrel are four flying gandharva-s, which arrest our attention. Bare-bodied and clad only in loincloths, these gandharva-s are in a playful mood. Their flight is unmistakable. The cumulous cloud behind and the fluttering ends of their garments point to this. Two of them are wearing socks. Binduja is a technique of vartana, a family of shading techniques adopted by the Ajanta artist to effect an illusion of depth and relief. This method, also known as the ‘dot-and-stipple-method’, uses a variation in the concentration of dots to produce the desired effect. This can be seen on the belly, chin and on the arms of the flying gandharva-s, bringing out the contours of their body.

On the ceiling of the ante-chamber we have in Squares and Circles - 6 [Kbl 139B] a portraiture of a row of geese in a ring. It is a superb rendering. About this Yazdani comments: “Some (of these ducks) have their necks sharply curved, as if they were in an angry mood, some have stretched their necks forward to show their determination to attack, some have bent them low to preen their feathers in an attitude of languor, some have turned their heads gracefully to one side to look amorously at their mates, and some have turned them with a romantic curve to show their feeling of ecstasy. The fine brush work of the artist in the delineation of the features of the birds also deserve a close study. The subject, both with regard to the ingenuity of design and perfection of technique plainly shows the skill of the Ajanta artist in decorative art.” But Narayan Sanyal points out one feature not mentioned by the others. The number of geese in the composition is twenty-three, an inconvenient prime number, precluding the possibility of using a stencil!

Painted outside the antechamber is another beautiful Squares and Circles - 7 [Kbl 139T]. A line drawing of this is given as A Ceiling Painting of Cave 2 (Line ?). Exquisite geometrical and floral patters fill the circular bands. Mithuna-s are painted in the spandrels. In spite of the restricted space the couples are not cramped as can be seen in the Detail of Squares and Circles - 7 [Kbl 140].

As in Cave 1, the ceiling decoration consists of filling up the space with squares and rectangles. Belonging to this mode of decoration is Squares and Rectangles - 2 [ASI 047], a part of the ceiling painting in the rear aisle. The geese, the mythical birds and the floral patterns are remarkable. Skilful shading has given the pure geometrical patterns a three dimensional effect.

The ceiling also contain some scenes in a lighter vein. Two Foreigners drinking [ASI 043] is a detail from the painting on the ceiling of the verandah. From their dress, they look like foreigners. But their dress is very interesting – head-dress, scarves, tunics and socks. The intriguing aspect of this detail as well as similar paintings in Ajanta is the inappropriateness of such drinking scenes in a religious shrine.
