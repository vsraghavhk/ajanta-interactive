Thousand Buddhas

Miracle of Shraavasti

Cave 2, Late 6th century, AD

There are eight places that are considered the most sacred places in the Buddhist faith. They are referred to as attha-mahaa-thaanaani. These places derive their holiness because of their intimate connection with major events in the life of the Master. Of these four are related to the important events in the life of the Buddha: Lumbini, where Siddhartha was born, Gaya, where He attained Enlightenment; Sarnath, where he preached the First Sermon, and Kushinagara, where he obtained nirvaana.

The remaining four are the locations where He is believed to have performed miracles. One of these is the theme of this mural. In this Lord Buddha displays His miraculous powers at Shraavasti, by multiplying himself a thousand-fold to the bewilderment of His critics. This miracle is painted elsewhere in Ajanta as well.

The other miracles are: His ascending to the Heaven of Thirty-three Gods and preaching the Dharma is the theme of a mural called Buddha preaching to the Congregation [nou 197] and the Miracle at Raja-griha of the subjugation of a mad elephant, Nalagiri, both painted in Cave 17. The miracle of a number of monkeys offering Him honey at Vaishaali is not to be found in Ajanta.

Shraavasti was the capital of the ancient kingdom of Koshala and is identified with the present day villages Saheth and Maheth the former in the district of Gonda and the later in the district of Bahraich, both in the state of Uttar Pradesh.

There are excellent portrayals of the compassionate Buddha in Ajanta. But, none of the painters reached the mark of high artistry to be seen in this painting. Conventional norms and formulae, in certain instances, have had preferences over artistic empathy. This painting demonstrates that conventionalism and mechanical representation rather than artistry inspired the scene.

The scene on the right-hand wall of the ante-chamber, consisting of monotonously-reproduced figures of the Buddha in various mudra-s (hand-gestures), represents the Miracle of Shraavasti. Rows of seated Buddhas are also painted on the right and left walls of the shrine. These two compositions belong to different styles.

This miracle performed at Shraavasti became popular and has been painted in a number of caves in Ajanta. In this rows of Buddha-figures, one above the other, came to be painted. The repetitive theme in this Miracle of Shraavasti of Cave 2 reflects the artistic tendency of the age. The paintings lack the vigour, imagination and delicacy of the earlier ones. A fragmentary painted record on the back wall of the antechamber to the right of the shrine-door in Cave 2 may record the donation as that of the Thousand Buddhas ([Kbl 118]. This record is attributed to the 6th century, AD.

Trying to impart some artistic interest to the panel they took care to vary the Buddha's postures and mudra-s. The painters also introduced an element of colourfulness by painting the robes of the Buddha in different colours ranging from white, grey, brown and green to red.

This painting may be considered a part the continuous decline in standards at Ajanta. Also belonging to this period is the painting of Two Figures of The Buddha [ASI 085] in Cave 17. These are painted one above the other, each enclosed in a cubicle.

On the other hand, the Images of the Buddha [Nou 133], painted on the right and left walls of the shrine, is of considerable sophistication. This composition avoids the monotony of repetitive images of the Buddha witnessed in the previous depiction. This has been done by varying slightly the postures, particularly by turning some of the faces slightly, alternating the mudra-s and finally by the expert use of ujjotana (adding-highlights) technique and succeeded in creating a mysterious glow on the face the Master. To add to the effect the artist has provided the bolsters with a variety of designs on them. The same theme, Buddha baffles Critics [Kbl 23], of the Miracle of Shraavasti of Cave 6 is a superior composition, particularly the variation in both the size and the poses of the Master and also the refined portraiture of the worshippers shown below.
