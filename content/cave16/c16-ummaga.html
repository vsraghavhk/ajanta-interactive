Mahaa-Ummaga Jaataka

Cave 16, 5th century, AD

Maha-Ummaga Jaataka is painted on the left wall of the front corridor, and is continued on the adjoining pilaster.

The Jaataka: Maha-Ummaga Jaataka is the story of Bodhi-sattva; who as a mere seven-year-old the boy, Maho-sadha, successfully solved the riddles which were posed to him by the people to test his supernatural powers and preached the Law.

The painting was praised by Griffiths in 1874. He could not copy it because it was covered with mud and grit. It was partially cleaned by the two Italian restorers in 1920-21. However, the gelatine they used to brighten the images soon turned yellow and opaque. The Archaeological Department of the Government of India has recently removed this layer of gelatine.

The explanation of the jaataka is supported by a line sketch Supernatural Child Maho-sadha [Line 10] and a colour picture Mahosadha and Four envious Councillors [Mjt 111], which is a detail of the mural.

Let us look at the line sketch Supernatural Child Maho-sadha [Line 10]. The lake, garden and building shown in the painting are apparently the tank, grove and palace built by the supernatural child, which are mentioned in the jaataka. The top left-hand corner of the picture shows a rider, representing the councillor of King Videha, who was deputed by his master to test Maho-sadha's wisdom. He wears a coat with full sleeves and striped stockings, while a straight sword hangs by his side. One of the horse's hind legs is stretched backward and the other raised forward to convey the feeling of rapid movement. Foreign influence can be seen in the rider's uniform; similar costume and accoutrements are frequently depicted in the other cases.

At the bottom barely visible is a scene depicting ‘the riddle of the son’ where Maho-sadha was called upon to settle a dispute between a woman and a goblin over the motherhood of a child. Maho-sadha ordered the child to be cut into two so that each could have equal part. Realising this would lead to the death of the child the mother of the child gives up her claim and thus Maho-sadha establishes the rightful owner. In the surviving part of the painting we see a man and a woman holding out the child's body while a soldier raises his sword to cut the boy in two.

In a plantain-grove below the royal rider is the scene Maho-sadha and four envious Councillors [Mjt 111]. On the left there are four figures apparently representing the four envious councillors who attempted to destroy Maho-sadha. The two men nearest to the boy have long hair, while the third wears a Persian cap. The expression of the fourth is especially sinister; his hair is dishevelled and his hand is on his sword as he looks at the boy in a wild and threatening way. The boy, wearing a small cap on his head, is gesturing to emphasise a point.

The painting is especially interesting because an illusion of distances is conveyed by depicting the group of men against a comparatively spacious background that is fringed by the conventional bands of hills. The mountains are in perfect harmony with the equally stylised water, which is represented by semicircular arches. The barrel-shaped roofs and heavy curvilinear eves bear a striking resemblance to the rock-hewn caves of the early 5th century.
