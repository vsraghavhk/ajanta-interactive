Finally, we come to one of the most beautiful paintings of Ajanta, popularly known as Dying Princess [ASI 003]. The painting is greatly damaged, and to assist studying the composition a line sketch, Death of the Princess (Line ?), is also included.

The lady is seen in a faint on a couch at the sight of a crown presented by a messenger at the left-hand bottom corner. The entire panel is gloomy and melancholy. The expressions of the characters in the scene, together with their gestures, reflect extreme anxiety and the seriousness of the situation.

The messenger holding the crown is fully aware of the grave news he has brought. His eyes are full of sympathy for the unhappy lady. The other male attendant above him, wearing a light skullcap in the Persian style, has raised his hand in excitement. To emphasise the expression of pathos, the artist places highlights on the nose, lips and chin of these two figures.

The central figure is that of the dark complexioned princess who has fainted on a low throne. Her back is resting on an embroidered cushion and her legs are stretched out in front resting on the floor. She has her left elbow on the cushion, and a female attendant behind the throne holds the lady with both hands so that she may not fall down. The head of the lady hangs limp and is slightly inclined towards the artist to reveal a three-quarter frontal view instead of her profile. Her half-closed eyes indicate extreme mental agony and physical collapse.

A second maid on the right of the lady is eagerly looking at the mistress with her mouth half-open. She has a ribbon tied around her forehead. A third maid, wearing a tight upper cloth is fanning the mistress with a palm-leaf fan. Through utter panic she has twisted her figure in an ungainly and somewhat awkward manner. Her gawkiness and the half-open mouth of the other girl are certainly not things of beauty, but the artist appears to be aware that at moments of tense anxiety even a female beauty may well forget how to look her best.

At the back of the pavilion, a little higher up, there is a roof enclosed by a parapet under a banana tree. Two maids are seen there engaged in intimate conversation. One of them has a veil over her head. She has a pitcher in her left hand and with the right she makes the sign of the figure two, by raising two of her fingers. She is of a fair complexion, has a youthful face, but her left eye is damaged. The other maid of dark complexion has a muffler round her neck, the knot of which is prominent. Her extended left hand wearing a bangle is significant. It appears that the fair girl has raised her two fingers in reply to the other’s question.

On the roof of the pavilion perches a gorgeous peafowl with its head bent low. The peacock with its head raised high and its chest thrown out is an accepted symbol of pride in beauty. But as the theme of the picture is its utter defeat, the peacock is seen with its head drooping in sorrow. The entire panel is gloomy and melancholy.

One wonders why out of the six female figures the princess is practically nude, although the artist has carefully arranged dresses for the other female figures. We come across with the feature of heroines of Ajanta being 'clothed in nakedness' a number of times.

The princess, it may be pointed out, is of dark complexion, this 'black is beautiful', is frequently met in Ajanta. The other black beauties are Shakti Pandara [ASI 031] painted in the Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara Panel in cave 1, Princess Irandati of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka in Cave 2 and the famous Black Apsaras [Kbl 169] of Cave 17. While commenting on this aspect it was pointed out that both Rama and Krishna were dark complexioned. Hasn't Kalidasa extolled that 'black is beautiful' while describing classical beauty in his Megha-dootam?

John Griffths was Principal of J.J.School of Art, Mumbai, and he had supervised copying Ajanta paintings from 1872 to 1855. He has published a monumental two-volume book on Ajanta paintings in 1896. He wrote: “For pathos and sentiment and the unmistakable way of telling its story this picture, I consider, cannot be surpassed in the history of art. A Florentine could have put better drawing, and a Venetian better colour, but neither could have thrown better expression into it.”
