Dancers and Musicians

Cave 10, 2nd or 1st century, BC

In the entourage are a group of Dancers and Musicians [Mjt 107], painted to the right of the panel Raja with his Retinue [Mjt 106]

Bare to the waist, these women appear to be a party of common folk who have improvised an orchestra of their own to please the Raja and also to pay homage to the Bodhi tree. Two in the party have long trumpets and the rest are clapping their hands or dancing. The elegance of these figures conveys an atmosphere of carefree, uninhibited joy.

With their outstretched arms, simple gestures, their obviously improvised movements and the way in which every girl leans in the direction in which the group is moving, produces a delightful rhythm.

To understand the landmarks in style, this Hinaayana masterpiece may be compared with Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091], a scene from Mahaa-janaka Jaataka of Cave 1 belonging to the Mahaayana group done five hundred years later. The later though technically sophisticated and advanced, as can be seen in the elaborate sketches drawn in varying thickness of line and filled in with a variety of bright colours, betrays an affected style.

The personages in this painting are painted in dark brown or burnt sienna, the soft hues of a few colours, red ochre, yellow ochre, terra verde, lampblack and lime white. The themes are forcibly expressed in a style closely related to the contemporary sculpture in the round and in relief at the great Buddhist shrine of Sanchi. The costumes, the veils over the women's heads and the architecture depicted are typical of the Sanchi sculptures of the 2nd-1st century BC. Drawn in long, bold and vigorous curves with great precision, in a line of unvarying thickness, these representations speak of the artist's dexterity of drawing and expression.

An attempt to provide depth and relief is seen in the slight variation in tones, though the number of colours used is limited. Experiences would be gained in the next five centuries to master the art of relief modelling, through various means, like, vartana (shading) and ujjotana (adding-highlights).
