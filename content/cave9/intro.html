Cave Nine

This chaitya (temple) is one of the earliest caves to be excavated and is assigned to the 1st century, BC. The plan of this temple is rectangular, and it has an architectural dignity all its own. It has twenty-one octagonal pillars that are about 3 meters high. The interior dimensions of the cave are 13.5 meters long, 7 metres wide and 7 meters high. The stoopa carved at the end of the hall has a dome one metre high above a cylindrical base and is crowned by a square capital representing the reliquary, a receptacle for relics. The chaitya has a beautifully decorated facade with a berm-railing, windows and lattice frame. The large figures of the Buddha carved along the sides of the cave are later additions in 5th-6th centuries.

The chaitya contains paintings of different periods. Some of these belong to the 1st century BC, while others are assigned to a period as late as the 4th-5th centuries, AD.

A Group of Votaries approaching a Stoopa (Line ?) painted on the left wall is a representative of the era of pre-Christian art.

A painting of a Kneeling Ascetic [Kbl 041] is included as an example of the 5th century AD painting and the variety of hairstyles painted in Ajanta.

A Group of Votaries approaching a Stoopa

Cave 9, 1st century BC

Most of the paintings from the earliest periods have, unfortunately, perished. Also many of the paintings copied by Griffiths in the last century have been consumed by fire. An extant sketch, A Group of Votaries approaching a Stoopa (Line ?) is reproduced to give an idea of the lost treasure.

In the figure is a group of sixteen votaries who are approaching the door of a stoopa. The variety of their postures and gestures and their hairstyles intertwined with strips of cloth are remarkable. The men's headgear, consisting of a topknot with elaborately plaited terminations protruding over the forehead, and their decorative waistbands are also extremely interesting. This very closely resembles the sculptures of Sanchi and Bharhut, both belonging to the same period as this cave. The grouping of the figures is natural, and is typical of the painting of this period.

On the right, the scene depicts the stoopa, which clearly shows the typical architecture of the 1st century BC. The stoopa is enclosed by four walls, which have two gates. The gate on the left side has a barrel-shaped roof with timber rafters, like those of the contemporary caves at Karle and Bedsa, both in the state of Maharashtra. The gateway on the right side is in the style of the Sanchi gates, and this painting is therefore thought by some to be contemporaneous. In the middle stands the stoopa under a number of umbrellas, which are Buddhist emblems of dignity and sanctity. Outside the enclosure can be seen the devotees and a banyan tree.

Kneeling Ascetic

Cave 9, 5th century AD

From the paintings of Ajanta one can get a fairly good account of the life style of people of the period. Kneeling Ascetic [Kbl 041] is included as an example of the style of painting during the 5th century AD painting and the variety of hairstyles painted in Ajanta.

This is a painting done over the pre-Christian era painting. It depicts an ascetic at his prayers. The posture of kneeling, his hands of prayer and, finally, the matted hair, all these are very similar to present day practices.
