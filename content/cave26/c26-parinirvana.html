Mahaa-parinirvaana [Nou 215]: Near the side door, is a colossal figure representing Mahaa-parinirvaana (Final-Extinction) of the Buddha at Kushi-nagara. The Buddha is reclining on the right side on a couch between two shaala trees. The right hand under his head is placed over a pillow. The folds of the robe and the fingernails are realistic. Apart from the softness of the pillow and other realistic details of the limbs and dress what appeals is the bhaava of the composition. Buddha’s Nirvana [Nou 008] exudes calm and total peace.

Below are the figures of His disciples mourning His pari-nirvaana and above are celestial beings rejoicing at the prospect of being with the Lord. At a niche beyond the feet of the Master, Ananda [Kbl 047], the most favoured disciple of the Buddha, is resting his on his right hand and weeping bitterly.

The longest figure of the Buddha in the entire series dominates the composition. The usual problem of frontality has been eliminated by intelligently placing the lower figures with their backs and side profiles towards the viewers. The panel is also important for it makes the beginning of a new method of stuccowork on the sculptures, which are further painted.
