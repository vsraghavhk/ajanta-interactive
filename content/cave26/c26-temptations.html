Temptations of the Buddha: The second, on the same wall, depicts, Temptations of the Buddha - Sculpture (Line ?) by Mara. The Buddha is seated, with his right palm in the bhumi-sparsha-mudra (earth-touching-gesture) under the Bodhi tree at the centre. On the left is Mara on an elephant accompanied by his host of demon-forces attacking the Buddha. On the right is the retreat of Mara. The lower panel shows the three Daughters of Mara (ASI L), Rati (lust), Trishna (thirst) and Arati (delight) tempting the Buddha with their bewitching charm. Their coquettish gestures, attractive dance and melodious music - all failed to tempt Gautama and swerve him from the path of Enlightenment. Here the Ajanta-sculptor excels in the modelling of the danseuse in her tribhanga (triflex) pose with intricately carved headgear, earrings, necklaces and her orchestra comprising the percussionists and a lady playing on cymbals. They are then shown seated on the right lower portion with their daughters, dejected at the failure of their mission.

The story-telling quality of the sculpture, the unity of the composition and the ingenious disposition of the figures attest to the mastery of the sculptor in his art equalling that of his brother artist in painting. The Ajanta painter has narrated the same scene, Temptations of the Buddha [Nou 048] in Cave 1 and a comparison between the two would be rewarding. This panel sets the trend towards the bas-reliefs in the round as seen in Ellora and later at Elephanta.
