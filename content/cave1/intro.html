Cave One

One of the finest vihaara-s (monasteries) among the cave temples of India, this cave has been dated to the end of the 5th century AD on the evidence of its architecture and sculptures.

The cave has the usual plan, with a verandah, a great congregation hall and a shrine. The verandah, with a cell at each end, is about 20 meters long, about 3 meters wide and about 4 meters high. A central door with carved jambs and entablature leads into the main hall. The ceiling is supported by twenty exquisitely carved and beautifully painted pillars, which are surrounded by an aisle. Facing the entrance, in the rear of the hall, there is an antechamber, leading into the shrine, which is 6 meters square. The vihaara has fourteen cells for the monks to stay.

The front porch has crumbled, but it still contains several bands of fine carvings. Among these are scenes from the life of the Buddha, elephant fights and hunting expeditions. The columns of the verandah and hall are also profusely carved. The shafts have vertical and spiral fluting encircled by bands of lovely tracery. The bases of the capitals are generally ornamented with mythical animals, religious stories and a great variety of floral designs.

Sculptures: The following are some of the important sculptures in this cave:

The Buddha in the Sanctum: The colossal figure of the Buddha in the garbha-graha (sanctum) is a fine specimen of Ajanta sculpture. The Buddha is seated in the padmasana (lotus-posture) in dharma chakra pravartana mudra (setting-wheel-of-Dharma-in-motion-attitude) is scooped in relief.

On either side is a standing chamara (fly-whisk) bearer. Both the figures are profusely ornamented, while their luxuriantly coiled tresses fall gracefully on one side. The vidyaadhara-s (flying-celestials) with garlands in hand on either side of the halo amidst clouds represented by ringlets are also well conceived figures.

Below, on a double-lotus pedestal is carved the Wheel of Law in profile. This is flanked on either side by a deer to suggest the Mrigadava (present day Sarnath) sermon. In the figures of the five monks flanking the left deer can be recognised pancha vareeya bhikshu-s (first-five-disciples).

The halo and chhatra (umbrella) over the head are painted. The door jambs of the shrine are elaborately carved with scroll-work, floral motifs, naaga-s (people belonging to the serpent world), mithuna-s (couples), female figures standing on makara-s (crocodile motifs), etc. The pillars of the ante-chamber have beautiful bracket-figures of women with attendants.

The composition is extremely well integrated and the attention of the onlooker is focused on the main object of worship. The sculpture, however, tends to be rather heavy and lacks the grace of that on the facade of Cave 19, and the grace of the sculpture of the Gupta period.

The Great Encounters: Three out of the four encounters, usually called the Ominous Sights, in the life of Gautama which prompted him to forsake the life of pleasure are carved over the cell to the left of the entrance. The panels to the right depict the young prince Gautama on a horse-driven chariot going for a ride, where scenes of death (extreme left), old age (left centre) and disease (right) confront him. At the centre he is seen listening to the music of a lady holding a veena on her lap. Within the three chaitya-arches below, musicians, playing the vina are shown in different poses.

Four Deer with a single Head: The famous ‘four deer with a single head’ can be found on the central column of the right row of the hall. The rendering of the four deer with one head is so cleverly done that it looks consistent and realistic.

Paintings: Large areas of the walls and the ceiling, once covered with paintings, are much damaged. But, even so, this cave along with Cave 17 contain more surviving paintings than others. The paintings below are listed following the clock-wise circumbulation (pradakshina). Some of the important paintings, which are taken up for explanation, are given below.

As one enters the cave through the main doorway and turns left the first painting one sees on the inside wall of front verandah between the main doorway and the window to the left of it, is Shibi Jaataka of Cave 1, an exquisite composition. The scenes Royal Pavilion [Kbl 072] containing the details, Prince And His Consort [Kbl 075] and King weighs Himself [Kbl 074], containing among the grieving spectators Wailing Women [Mjt 051]. Another episode from the same jaataka is painted in Cave 17.

Bhikshu [Kbl 079], Janapada-Kalyaani [ASI 006], Maid with a Tray [Kbl 081L] and Maid tries to revive The Princess [ASI 007], are scenes from the narration of the Conversion of Nanda of Cave 1. This story is painted above the window on the inside wall of the front verandah to the left of the main doorway, beyond the narration of Shibi Jaataka.

An unidentified scene known as Palace Scene of the Naga Kingdom [Nou 076], of which a Dancer in the Naaga Court [Mjt 046] is painted on the left wall of the inside of the front verandah.

Shankha-paala Jaataka is painted on the left side of the mail wall. Most of the scenes are obliterated. Among the important surviving scenes are Shankha-paala listens to the Sermon [Kbl 082], of which Woman ListensTo The Sermon [ASI 009] is a detail, Hunters Drag the Serpent [Kbl 083T/L] and Shankha-paala in Conversation with Alaara [Kbl 083B/L].

A major portion of the left wall contains the narration of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka, following the story of Shankha-paala Jaataka. The following are the principal scenes of this jaataka. In the first act there are three scenes. These are Queen captivates Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L], Dancing Girl With Musicians [Kbl 091] and Mahaa-Janaka Going Out Through The Palace Gate. In the next act there is one scene in which Mahaa-Janaka Meets An Ascetic [Kbl 085] in the Himavali Hill. In the first scene of the third act Mahaa-Janaka Announces His Decision [Kbl 087R] to abdicate. Reconciled Queen [Kbl 089] and bewildered royal Household [Kbl 088] are its details. In the next scene of the third act Mahaa-Janaka Leaves The Palace [Kbl 096] on a horseback. The Three Maids [Kbl 097] are among the royal entourage. The last scene painted is the Shipwreck Scene, which is greatly damaged. Sufficient portions of this narration still remain to stand testimony to the glorious baroque period at Ajanta.

The panel Lustration and Renunciation comprises three frames, Preparation for the Ceremony [ASI 019], Ceremonial Bath [ASI 020] and Royal Household pays Homage [ASI 017] and some consider this depiction to be part of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka, painted on the northwestern wall, exhibits the Ajanta artist’s ‘multiple vision’ technique of perspective.

The two Bodhi-sattva-s, Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069] and Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshwara [Kbl 066], painted on either side of the wall leading to the shrine, particularly the former, are masterpieces. The Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani Panel is also a grand composition, the Padma-paani’s Consort [Kbl 068], the Mithuna Figures [ASI 022] and a Kinnara playing the Lute [Kbl 071T] are important from the stylistic and historical points of view also. On the other hand the Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshwara Panel contains one of the most beautiful women of Ajanta, Shakti Pandara [Mjt 078].

The Temptations Of The Buddha [Nou 048] is painted over the entire left wall of the ante-chamber. In this, the activities of Maara, the Buddhist devil, are interpreted with a vigour and liveliness which has few parallels, the only competitor being a sculpture on the same theme in Cave 23. The characters used by the devil to distract the Buddha Daughter of Maara [ASI 027] and Maara’s Attendent [Nou 050] are two of its details.

Champeyya Jaataka is painted on the wall of the rear verandah to the left of Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara above the doors of the two cells. In this compact composion five scenes are painted,. They are: Naaga King repents his Life of Pleasures [Kbl 105], King Uggasena watches Snake-charmer's Tricks [Kbl 106T] along with the Courtiers watch Snake Dance, [ASI 036], Appearance of Champeyya's Wife and Child, and, finally and, Champeyya Gives A Sermon [Kbl 106B]. Three Maids listening to the Sermon [ASI 038], an exquisit portrayal of women in beautiful poses, is a detail from the last mentioned scene.

An unidentified solo painting Queen in a Palace Scene [Nou 073], painted on the right hand wall of the main hall, is an example of the composition of the period of declining artistic standards. The woman holding a Lotus [Mjt 034] is a detail of the composition.

Another unidentified scene depicts Some Foreigners [Kbl 109], important from the point of view of Ajanta art history, for this painting is of little artistic merit intrinsically.

Ceiling Paintings: The ceiling, and some of the pillars, contains some exquisite animal and geometric motifs. Fighting Bulls [Mjt 013] painted on the capital of the second pillar in the left hand corner of the hall is one of the most forceful and vigorous drawings at Ajanta. The Charging Bull [ASI 030] and Elephant [ASI 028] are splendid examples of depiction of animals by the Ajanta artist and they show how conversant he was with the technique of kshaya-vriddhi or foreshortening, a technique of showing depth in drawing.. Squares and Rectangles - 1 [ASI 025] is a sample how the Ajanta artist composed the ceiling paintings with squares and rectangles filling them with all kinds of secular motifs, like Clown [ASI 008], Mythical Birds [ASI 005] and Cherubs in playful Mood [Kbl 114B]. One may consider themes like royal Harem [Kbl 108] out of place in a religious place.
