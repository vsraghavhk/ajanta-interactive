Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara Panel

Cave 1, Second half of 6th Century, AD

As in the composition of the Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani Panel [Line 03] on the left side of the wall leading to the ante-chamber, is the painting Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara Panel [Line 04]. In it we find, overwhelming his rich surroundings by his personal splendour, stands the Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara [Kbl 066].

On the top left near a gate is a standing couple absorbed in religious conversation, as is apparent from the mudra of the right hand of the male figure. A dark lady of singular charm, commonly known as Shakti Pandara [Mjt 078] or Dark Princess in the left lower corner, accepts lilies offered by another lady. Above this is a royal figure making an offering of lilies to the Bodhi-sattva. Though richly attired, his glory fades into insignificance in the presence of the divine Bodhi-sattva.

Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara [Kbl 066], earlier identified as Bodhi-sattva Vajrapani, is regarded as having emanated from Amitabha and his Shakti Pandara. He is the most esteemed Bodhi-sattva of the Mahaayana pantheon, being styled sangha-ratna (Jewel of the Order).

He poses with a Divine Buddha on the crest of his immense bejewelled diadem. Bodhi-sattvas are identified by just such a Buddha-sign on the crest. His dark complexion points to his southern origin. The elaborate jewellery of the Bodhi-sattva reflects southern customs and fashions. On the other hand the northern influence is still predominant in Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069], which has Aryan features and a fair complexion. The dark skin, round face and fuller lips that were prominent in the Ajanta paintings of the pre-Christian era reappear in the Deccani-type ethnic structure of the Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara [Kbl 066].

In the drawing and colouring the artists seem to have used similar techniques as were used in the Padma-paani panel. There is, however, a greater relief effect in the drawing of the Avalokiteshvara, chiefly because of the larger area of greenish background to the figure.

Shakti Pandara [Mjt 078], the lady on the right of the pair shown in the bottom-left corner in the panel, accepting lilies from the other lady, is one of the most famous of Ajanta paintings at Ajanta. In the opinion of Yazdani it is one of the finest works of art in the world. Popularly known as the Dark Princess, it is probably a representation of Amitabha's Shakti Pandara.

Its fine modelling, exquisite ornamentation, tranquillity and thoughtfulness are beautifully brought out by the dark complexion against a lighter background. Certainly there is no other portrait in Ajanta with such perfect eyes; the hazel-brown of the pupils and the red spots in the corners of the eyes radiate an astonishing natural friendliness. It is truly an unusual painting, considering the late period to which it belongs, when the elongated and exaggerated eyes of a declining art were already in vogue.

It may be recalled that in an earlier discussion it was pointed out that most of the beautiful women of Ajanta are depicted as black beauties and this detail was taken as an example. Note that Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara [Kbl 066] depicted in this panel is also of dark complexion and this was attributed to the southern influence.
