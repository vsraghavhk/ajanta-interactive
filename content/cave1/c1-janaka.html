Mahaa-janaka jaataka

Cave 1, End of 6th Century, AD

Renunciation is a favourite theme of the jaataka-s. This painting is generally considered as referring to the renunciation of King Mahaa-janaka. Scenes from this jaataka are painted on the left wall of the main hall starting from the top of the door of the second cell and covering the rest of the wall. Some scholars also consider the renunciation scenes, titled Lustration and Renunciation [Line 18], painted on the rear wall as belonging to this jaataka.

The mural is of great importance from the point of view of art history. Due to the fortunate survival of the major portion, depiction of this jaataka offers scope for a detailed study of both the art style and method of narration of this period. In terms of the art style, these paintings show the beginning of deterioration from the classical elegance to baroque ostentation. A detailed study of these paintings would be rewarding as it could provide a wealth of information regarding jewellery, textile and dress, musical instruments, architecture, etc.

In terms of the art style, these paintings herald a new phase of Ajanta art, that is the beginning of baroque ostentation. The classical phase, of which the paintings of Cave 16 like Dying Princess [ASI 003], Votaries with Offerings [Kbl 126] of Cave 2 and the most magnificent composition, Simhala Avadana of Cave 17 are proud examples, gave way to mannerism witnessed in the superb narration of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka. The baroque sophistication that followed shows the beginning of deterioration as may be seen in the scenes of this jaataka. The eyes-slits are stretched out of proportion to the face, men look effeminate, and women exaggeratedly feminine, with bulging curves. Both men and women wear excessive ornaments, and strings of jewellery hang from the pillars and pavilions of royal chambers.

The Jaataka: Bodhi-sattva born Mahaajanaka was the son of Aritha-janaka, who was banished from his kingdom Mithila by his brother Pola-janaka. Mahaa-janaka went all over the world as a merchant and amassed enough wealth to help him regain his father's kingdom. In the meantime his uncle Pola-janaka died survived by his most beautiful and haughty daughter, Shivaali. Before his death he expressed the wish that Shivaali should marry only that person who could fulfil three conditions. Thus he set about three almost impossible conditions.

Mahaa-janaka fulfilled all three conditions and married Shivaali without knowing that he is marrying his cousin. However, the pomp and luxuries of the palace life were not to his liking. In spite of the pleadings from his queen, Mahaa-janaka finally renounced the world and went to the Himalayas to meditate upon the Truth.

Before we proceed to follow the mural let us keep in mind certain Ajanta conventions. Firstly, scenes in Ajanta murals are not separated from each other by frames, but tactfully placed rocks, gateways and pillars which serve as vague dividers that do not irritate the eye. Secondly, the narration may not be painted chronologically. Thirdly portraits of the same character resemble one another very closely.

The painted narration starts only after the marriage of Mahaa-janaka. Fortunately in this case the scenes are painted mostly in the chronological order, excepting that one of the earlier scenes of shipwreck is painted at the very end.

The story is narrated in three acts not taking into account the shipwreck scene. The following description is supported by two line sketches and a host of colour pictures.

Starting from the left, in the first act we have three scenes shown in the sketch Mahaa-janaka in Dilemma (Line ?). On the extreme left we have the first scene of Queen captivates Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L]. Beyond the pavilion and the pillars is a scene where a Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091] attempts to divert the attention of the king. Undeterred by all these, we see in the next scene, Mahajanaka leaves the Palace through the palace gate. The gate perhaps divides this act from the next.

Let us start with the scene of Queen captivates Mahaa-Janaka [Kbl 090L]. In this scene, the king is seen seated on a bed. He wears a crown adorned with gems. On his left is Shivaali, his queen, leaning against him. She has either nothing on, or else her dress is so diaphanous as to be invisible. She is one of Ajanta's women ‘dressed in nakedness’. She has her right elbow nuzzling the lap of the king. Every line of her figure expresses her attitude of passionate yielding. The king does not look at his wife. But, he has a wild and vacant look. His right hand suggests despair.

In the pillared hall there is a bevy of ten maids-in-attendance. All are looking at the couple, except three, who are watching the dancing in front of the pavilion. Their looks invite the attention of the viewer to the focal part of the painting, the pathetic king. In front of the king is an urn and at his back is a striped bolster. The pillared hall is approachable by a flight of steps. A dwarf, who is to be found in most royal scenes in Ajanta, is in attendance sitting on the ground to the right of the king. Though the sitting posture is the same in this scene and in the later scene where Mahaa-Janaka announces his Decision [Kbl 087R] to abdicate, the emotional atmosphere is different.

Beyond the pillars in the forecourt we see in the next scene in which a Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091] is entertaining the dejected king to draw him to the pleasures of the world and to divert his attention from spiritual pursuits. Dancing must have been a sophisticated art during this Mahaayana period, as can be seen in this scene. A professional dancer strikes a graceful pose of tribhanga while her hands eloquently express the mudra-s (hand-gestures). She is attired in a long jacket with full sleeves of a different colour from her close-fitting blouse. Her striped skirt is loose and long, allowing her full freedom of movement. The thumb-ring set with a miniature mirror, the ear-rings of elaborate design, and the head-dress consisting of strings of gold beads or pearls are all carefully depicted, and the dancer's plaits are ingeniously entwined with flowers.

She is surrounded by a group of five musicians: two are playing flutes, two playing cymbals, one a pair of standing drums, another drawn in full profile is playing a bell-shaped drum with a narrow ring for a grip, and the fifth, with her back to us, a stringed instrument. Like the dancer, all the accompanying musicians show great dynamism and movement.

The palatial surroundings and decorations, the accompaniment of a host of musical instruments, the fantastic garments and elaborate ornaments have all successfully contributed to staging a spectacular scene.

The queen was not successful in her attempt to hold back the king and Mahaa-janaka's mental turmoil has not ceased. He seeks the advice of a saint. In this next scene Mahaa-janaka goes out through the Palace Gate on an elephant.

In the second act we have the scene of Mahaa-janaka Meets An Ascetic [Kbl 085] in the Himavali Hill. The ascetic with matted hair and a rosary in his hand is delivering his sermon from a stone platform. Among the large number of listeners we find the king with his hands folded in deep reverence. This detail is given in Mahaa-janaka listens to a sermon [Kbl 087L]. True to the spirit of the Buddhist faith, we have at his feet a pair of buck and doe poised with faces tilted up, as if in rapt attention. By the way, the corkscrew curls falling on to the shoulders of the ascetic are a baroque mannerism.

In the third and the last act, shown in the sketch Mahaa-janaka Abdicates (Line ?), the drama shifts back to the palace. On the left Mahaa-Janaka announces his Decision [Kbl 087R] to the Reconciled Queen [Kbl 89] and to the Bewildered Royal Household [Kbl 088] and then Mahaa-Janaka Leaves The Palace [Kbl 096] along with his entourage including Three Maids [Kbl 097].

In the first scene of the second act the enlightened Mahaa-Janaka announces his Decision [Kbl 087R] to abdicate. This scene is full of pathos and feeling.

To the left of the first scene of this panel - refer to Mahaa-janaka Abdicates (Line ?) - on the extreme left we see the queen-mother is sitting on a stool with the queen kneeling before her and an animated discussion is on. Perhaps the queen-mother is admonishing the queen. Two maids are to be seen witnessing the proceedings with great anxiety; the one behind the queen-mother, a chaawri-bearer, wearing a colourful dress, strikes a sculpturesque pose of great poise.

At the centre of the scene is the king announcing his decision to renounce the world in spite of the pleadings of Queen Shivaali. Perhaps the queen has reconciled herself to the situation. There are a few attendants around the royal couple, two are waving the whisk. The girl behind the queen is stunned with her eyebrows drawn and eyes dilated, and the girl above the queen is musing with a finger on her cheek. The dwarf is still holding on to the same vantage position.

Now we come to the last scene of the drama. Mahaa-janaka leaves the Palace [Kbl 096] on horseback through the palace gate, which starts a new act in this drama. The king is seen leaving the palace in full regal splendour, led by a procession of his loyal subjects. The royal umbrella is held aloft. A large number of palace musicians are in attendance. The entire scene is full of animation. In the musicians leading the procession are, in addition to a flautist and a cymbal-player, the two instruments we have seen before in the last scene, a conch-blower, one playing a double-sided drum like the modern day pakhawaj of the North or mridangam of the South. The whole scene looks like an excellent snapshot.

The panel that follows is the scene of shipwreck and its sequel and is greatly damaged. Further, as mentioned before, this scene is a very early scene when Mahaa-janaka went on a ship voyage to amass wealth.

Let us study the portrayal of characters. The king is depicted three times. They are in the scenes Queen captivates Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L], Mahaa-janaka Meets an Ascetic [Kbl 085] and Mahaa-Janaka announces his Decision [Kbl 087L]. Though the sitting posture in all the three scenes is the same, the emotional atmosphere is different. Let us compare these scenes from this angle.

In the first scene of Act I, the newly-wed king was in regal splendour - two rows of necklaces sparkling with diamonds and pearls, one is fitting round the neck and the other an elaborate one is slightly longer. These are in addition to the pearl-studded sacred-thread. When he met the ascetic the elaborate necklace was replaced with a less ostentatious one. (But why is the sacred-thread now around the left elbow, which in Hindu practice, is worn only during ceremonial ablution to the dead forefathers? Was the Ajanta artist careless?) And finally, when he announces his decision to renounce, he has cast off every-thing including the sacred-thread, excepting the close fitting beads. Is the removal of the sacred thread suggestive of the impending sanyaasa?

In the first scene the king has a vacant look reflecting his mental conflict and his hands showing his inner pain. While sitting before the sage his whole deportment is one of humility and utter surrender, his hands folded in prayer. But in the last scene his visage is serene and full of compassion and love, but of firm determination. His hands compose dharma chakra mudra (preaching-attitude) to show that he has now found the path.

The change in the attitude of Shivaali is even more pronounced in the two scenes that she depicted in, when pleading with the king in the first scene and when being informed of the king’s renunciation. From a conceited queen, ‘dressed nakedness’ determined to win over her husband in the earlier scene, she is now a well-draped lady sitting before the king "erect like a candle undisturbed by air" ready to receive the heart-breaking news.

It is interesting also to note that the continuity in dress and physical features of the characters is maintained, while bringing out the contrast in the expressions of the characters.

Musicians playing musical instruments have been shown many scenes in Ajanta. A variety of musical instruments have been depicted, both in paintings and in sculpture. These cover the period from the 2nd century, BC to the about 6th century, AD. So Ajanta offers scope for study of development of the musical heritage of India. We can see both the continuity and change over the period as presented in Ajanta itself.

First let us compare the musicians present in the two scenes in this jaataka. The accompanying musicians of the dancer in the earlier scene were cymbal-players, flautists, drummers and a musician playing a string-instrument. We find from this painting and also elsewhere in Ajanta that side-flutes were in vogue even during this period. It may be mentioned that side-flutes came into use in the West only in the 14th century. In the same way cymbals continue to keep time measure in dance performances even today. The bell-shaped drum with a narrow ring for a grip looks more like some of the drums used in folk music. The standing drums are no more found in India. We are not able to make a judgement on the string-instrument as this part of the painting is somewhat mutilated.

A slightly different group of musicians accompany the king when he abdicates. The musician blowing a conch-shell is shown with puffed cheeks and popping eyes. Conch is still being used for heralding the arrival of important people like the king or during the ceremonial procession of deities and pooja-s in temples and houses. The mridangam (or pakhawaj) player is shown very realistically in the midst of executing a complex rhythmic pattern with both the hands. His instrument and playing technique are the same as those used today. The playing posture as well as fingering of the flute player look natural, and we now realise an unbroken musical tradition of not less than 15 centuries.

We can witness another set of musicians in the composition Descent Of Indra [Kbl 164] in Cave 17. In the Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani Panel [Line 3] in this cave itself we come across the kinnara-s playing musical instruments, one of the instruments resembling the modern mandolin.

The dancer in the first act striking a graceful pose of tribhanga was already mentioned; her dress and ornaments were described in detail. Dancers have been portrayed also in other scenes in Ajanta. For example, Dancer in the Naga-Court [Mjt 046] in this cave is one, where the principal dancer is drawn matching the expertness of the dancer in this story, though not as elaborately. Fortunately we have a composition depicting dancers accompanied by musicians belonging to the earlier period, namely of the 2nd-1st century, BC in the Dancers and Musicians [Mjt 107] painted in Cave 10, though badly damaged. One can feel the unaffected gay abandon in the Hinaayana masterpiece, as against the Mahaayana painting which is more stylised. The scanty clothing and the few ornaments, such as bangles of conch-shell modelled on traditional folk ornaments of the pre-Christian era painting is in contrast to the beautifully designed garments, the variety of glittering trinkets, and bewildering coiffure of the dancer in the Mahaa-janaka court. The costumes, the hair styles, the shape of the eyes and the general atmosphere all epitomise the final phase in the development of the Ajanta style of painting.

We shall consider the various techniques used in Ajanta to produce an illusion of the third dimension. Perspective is a technique of drawing objects on a surface giving an illusion of distance and solidity. In this the farther objects look small. This may be called true perspective or normal perspective. Another way is the reverse perspective. The building in front of which the dancer is performing with her orchestra is not shown in the true perspective, but is drawn in the reverse perspective. This cannot be ascribed to the Ajanta artist's carelessness or to his lack of knowledge of drawing in perspective, for he has executed some remarkable paintings in true perspective. The paintings showing Gateway to Brahma-datta’s Palace [Mjt 097] in Cave 2 and the Monastery [Kbl 227R], a scene from Shibi Jaataka of Cave 17 are excellent examples for true perspective. We should imagine, then, the artist composed the scene to suite the theme rather than exhibiting realism. We come across with reverse perspective in the depiction of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka of Cave 2. We will have some more to say on the technique of showing distances while explaining Lustration and Renunciation [Line 18] scene in the same cave.

Another method of creating an illusion of the third dimension is producing depth and relief while portraying characters. One of the methods used for this is called ujjotana (adding-highlights). In the execution of the figure of Mahaa-janaka and other characters, the artist has made use of the ujjotana technique, that is, by adding white patches appearing on the chin, breasts, arms, legs or wherever an elevation of form was desired. This method is different from vartana (shading). Adding to this was the use of blue colour, especially for the background to create an illusion of depth in contrast to the warm red and brown colours.

A noteworthy feature of Ajanta is the expert draughtsmanship. The perfection of painting technique is displayed in the extraordinary breadth and confidence of draughtsmanship - the varying thickness of line drawn with a free flowing sweep of the brush to depict the oval faces, arched eyebrows, aquiline noses, and fine sensitive lips. The lines have become darker to serve as a suitable accessory to the vartana (shading) and ujjotana (adding-highlights) techniques for creating an illusion of depth. The visual depth is enhanced by the judicious use of blue - of lapis lazuli - by contrasting it with the warmth of the red and brown tones. The illusion of spaciousness is also evident where the reddish ochre, yellow ochre and dark brown tints applied to the dancing girl and her imposing costume stand out against a plain bluish-green background.

This draughtsmanship is evident in the variety of body positions of the characters portrayed. An important feature of the Ajanta style is the consummate skill in the use of variety of sthaana-s (body positions) while portraying characters in Ajanta. In all the scenes depicted in this narration, every one of the women is shown in different positions - from the rijvagata (frontal) to parshvagata (strict profile) to anriju (back view).

The Ajanta artists followed certain conventions in separating scenes. A gate is often placed to herald the beginning of an act. In this panel, we find the king on an elephant starts a new act. One will not fail to notice that the king passes through the gate once more in this narration, that is, when finally Mahaa-janaka leaves the Palace [Kbl 096] after abdication. Within the first Act the pillars of the pavilion separates the first scene from the following one of dance performance. So is the case in the last act.

Ajanta, as said before, is a treasure house to study the contemporary fashion in textiles, jewellery, etc. As an example, the chamara-bearing girl sports an upper-garment with rows of geese printed on it - a fashion referred to in the contemporary literature. Note the architectural details of the palace in this as well as in a later scene. This is characteristic of the baroque period - too many pillars and rather excessive decoration on the pavilion.
