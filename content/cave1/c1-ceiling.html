Paintings on the Ceiling

That the Ajanta artist has not painted religious themes on the ceilings was mentioned elsewhere.. This perhaps gave him ample opportunity for his creative abilities. He gave expressions to his fantasies and rich imagery. In this cave, the ceiling, and some of the pillars, contain some exquisite animal and geometric motifs. The ceiling of the ante-chamber is embellished with a large a circular lotus-motif. The mail hall and elsewhere the decorations consist mostly of a number of rectangular panels filled with decorative motifs framed by smaller squares or rectangles with various decorative patterns. These decorative elements are of fruit and floral forms, animals, mythical forms, cherubs and human beings. Some of these motifs are repeated a number of times so that the effect is that of an enormous printed textile spread over the sanctuary. A selection from these individual motifs is given below.

Fighting Bulls [Mjt 007], painted on the capital of the second pillar in the left-hand corner of the hall, is one of the most forceful and vigorous drawings in Ajanta. The two bulls convey a tense fury, and this effect is enhanced by the delineation of their bodies in identical postures; their heads are lowered, muscles contracted, tails raised, and the curves of their necks and humps clearly delineated. We know of the same subject depicted in the Bhaja Cave, and one thousand years later a representation of two bulls engaged in combat painted in Mahal-i-Khas in Fatehpur Sikri, but neither of these paintings reaches the power of pictorial expression attained at Ajanta. To appreciate the artist’s confident draftsmanship a line sketch of this painting, Fighting Bulls (Line ?) is included. The placid Resting Bulls [Kbl 174] painted on the ceiling of Cave 17 is a study in contrast to these furious bulls.

A similar expression can be seen in the Charging Bull [ASI 030]. Ajanta artists have employed a number of techniques to give a feeling of depth. Showing farther objects in diminished size called foreshortening, is one such technique. This painting is a splendid example of how conversant the artist was with this technique. A totally different approach is the use of vartana (shading) techniques. One of them is called patraja wherein the depth is brought out by drawing lines of suitable thickness to follow the contours of the object. We can see this technique used in this painting, and in the painting of an Elephant [ASI 028] on the ceiling of this cave. Both these paintings illustrate the skill of the artist in composing animals spaced in flowers and plants.

Another repetitive motif was painting mythological creatures like the Mythical Birds [ASI 005]. The colour scheme is most pleasing.

The artist had ample scope for mixing what is real with what is totally imaginary. He found uninhibited space to record his sense of humour on the ceilings. For example the Cherubs in Playful Mood [Kbl 114B] is one of the many such cherub pairs we find on the ceiling. Clown [ASI 008] is an interesting study. The dwarf, cradled amongst flowers, uses the creeper as a swing, but the artist did not consider it inappropriate for a slender vine to support a man.

But it is the bacchanalian scene Royal Harem [Kbl 108] which takes the cake for its incongruity in a religious shrine. The characters are obviously from a foreign country. From their attire it may be deduced that they are from the Northwest of India. Some identify the main characters with the Persian emperor Khausrau and his queen Sirin.

The effect of such an aesthetically pleasing way of filling up the space with idyllic colour designs can be seen in Squares and Rectangles - 1 [ASI 025]. On the top two corners we have lotuses. The central square of the top row and the left corner of the bottom row are fruits that are all still life portraitures. The charging bull at the lower right corner is well animated. The central rectangle is a masterly composition. The motifs filling the space are a goose-like mythical bird, a parrot with a happy cherub at the centre, all in great harmony with the surrounding flowers.
