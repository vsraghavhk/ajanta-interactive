Palace Scene of the Naaga Kingdom

Cave 1, Early 6th century, AD

Palace Scene of the Naga Kingdom [Nou 076] is from an unidentified scene, and is painted on the left wall of the inside of the front verandah. The line sketch Naga Court (Line ?) also helps us in appreciating the composition.

The king, in whose court a dance is being performed, is apparently naaga raja (the serpent-king), since he and his queen have halos of cobra-hoods over their heads. The crowded scene, composed of beautifully attired ladies of the court, female attendants, dwarfs and other courtiers, is typical of the late period.

The principal Dancer in the Naga-Court [Mjt 046] is sketched in extremely graceful curves, with long sweeping brush-strokes, creating a perfect rhythm and balance. The silky shine of her blue jacket is complemented by her pinkish complexion, and her bangles and coiffure are depicted with great care. The colour scheme of clear blue, deep purple-brown and rich malachite-green with touches of clear red, is very beautiful. She is drawn in the paraavrita ("head and one shoulder turned backwards") described in the post-Gupta treatise, Vishnu-dharmottara.

A new colouring technique in the use of lapis lazuli, a cool transparent blue, came into use in Ajanta only in the 4th-5th centuries AD. This is employed during the Mahaayana period to considerable effect in a number of paintings to produce a sense of distance and depth, and thereby to enhance the feeling of movement and animation.

The attire of the dancer, her entourage and the dancing pose she strikes may be compared with those of Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091], a scene from Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in the same cave.
