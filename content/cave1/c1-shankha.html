Shankha-paala jaataka

Cave 1, 4th century, AD

Shankha-paala Jaataka is a long story painted on a small compact space on the left side of the main hall.

The Jaataka: Attracted by the wealth and splendour of the naaga-world that he happened to visit, Bodhi-sattva born King Duryodana of Magadha, wished to be born as a naaga king in his next birth. He was, then, born as Shankha-paala, king of the serpents in his next birth and inherited enormous wealth. But soon he got tired of all the luxuries of royal life. He became enlightened on hearing a sermon by an ascetic, who was the king of Magadha before he became a saint. Shankha-paala decided to end this aimless life and went and lay on an ant-hill. Some hunters spotted the huge snake, caught him and treated him cruelly. Alaara, a compassionate merchant took pity on the snake and released him paying some compensation in the form of cattle to his captors. The grateful Shankha-paala took Alaara to his kingdom and treated him as a royal guest. Later Alaara became a monk and proceeded to the Himalayas to preach the Law.

Worship of serpents and mythological stories connected with the kingdom of the serpents called naaga-loka must have been very ancient. The Bodhi-sattva taking birth as a serpent has been narrated often. In this cave is painted Champeyya Jaataka, in which Bodhi-sattva incarnated as a naaga king and deserted the kingdom in similar circumstances.

The major part of the narration of this jaataka is given as a sketch Ascetic gives Sermon [Line 2] and also in the colour picture Shankha-paala listens to the Sermon [Kbl 082]. The ascetic giving a sermon was the former king of Magadha. The head of the ascetic is completely obliterated. At the centre, the serpent-king Shankha-paala listens to the sermon. Shankhapaala’s figure is better preserved. He is sitting on a cushion with a bolster at the back with interesting patterns. His countenance speaks of deep devotion and his folded hands show his humility. A group of listeners, young women and men, aged people, birds and beasts sit around the ascetic.

The Ajanta artist was adept in portraying human figures in a variety of poses, sthaana-s. Here is an example. Among the persons present is a Woman listens to the Sermon [ASI 009]. She is squatting with her back to the artist in a graceful pose, leaning to her right. She is supporting her head with her right hand, resting her elbow on her knee. This pose is typical in India, even today. A composition of great observation and draughtsmanship!

To the left of the naaga king is depicted a very elegant lady in tribhanga (triflex) pose, listening to the sermon with great devotion and attention.

In the lower panel we see Hunters drag the Serpent [Kbl 083T/L] along the road. The size of the capture when compared to those of his persecutors suggests that Shankhapaala, the naaga king, is willingly enduring the pangs, being sworn to ahimsa. The effort of pulling such a huge snake has been very realistically shown.

Below the serpent we see the compassionate Alaara offering Money and cattle as compensation for freeing the tortured snake. Though damaged, ewe can still identify a few cattle, standing testimony to the artist's acute observation.

We find Shankha-paala in Conversation with Alaara [Kbl 083B/L], his rescuer at the bottom left of the panel. The royal features of Shankha-paala with his naaga hood and the rustic demeanour of Alaara, the merchant, are studies in contrast.
