Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani Panel

Cave 1, Second half of 6th century, AD

Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani, [Kbl 069] the central figure of this panel, is the most popular painting of Ajanta and no collection of Ajanta painting can be complete without this masterpiece. This panel is painted on the back wall, to the left of the antechamber, symmetrical with the Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara panel on the other side of the antechamber.

The painting belongs to the post-classical period of mannerism, to be followed by baroque ornamentation deviating from the classical idealism, as may be seen in the narration of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in this cave. Known for its brilliant composition, this panel compels the beholder's eye towards it and one is struck by the general ensemble and unity of the theme. The remarkable fact is that the massive proportions of the figure of Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani - it measures 1.8 metres across the shoulders - in no way detracts from the harmonious composition. The panel may be studied making use of the line sketch Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani Panel [Line 03]. Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069], Padma-paani’s Consort [Kbl 068] shown on his left, the Mithuna Figures [ASI 022] depicted on the left above the main figure, and, finally, the background itself are the details in colour.

Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069] is a personification of apparently contradictory qualities of perfect compassion and perfect indifference, both existing simultaneously. Out of compassion for the countless creatures in the cosmos, he renounced the pursuit of Buddha-hood and nirvaana, so that he might continue preaching the Buddhist doctrine until the last being in the cosmos received enlightenment and thus was released from the chain of rebirths.

On the other hand, this dispassionate Bodhi-sattva has, paradoxically enough, all the qualities that youth, high birth, noble lineage and religious ardour can produce: strong masculine limbs, a broad chest, a well-set neck, a high philosopher's brow, large meditative eyes and firm lips. The supple body is full of vigour, but the plastic grace is almost feminine and at the same time detached from the distracting environment. The Bodhi-sattva, lambent with the glow of wisdom, and marked out for the spirituality of Buddhahood wears a rich diadem on his forehead and rows of necklaces adorning his neck. Through the extraordinary confidence of the line and the softness of the shading the countenance reflects a lyrical and pensive abstraction. The flawless, opalescent smoothness of the skin, the beautiful proportion of body to face, and the eyes half-closed in meditation radiate a celestial beauty and purity. The textures and muscular structure are scarcely indicated so that the figures symbolize the divine anatomy of supernatural beings.

Thus, the Padma-paani figure is the finest example of the realisation of the classical ideal through symbolism based on animals and plants, as laid down in the shastra-s. Usually the limbs of the cult image are related to the shapes and forms of the vegetal and animal worlds. For example, the eyes of the Bodhi-sattva are likened to lotus petals, the curving brow-line to that of the arching bow and the ovoidal face is supposed to resemble an egg. Further, his shoulders and torso are compared with the domed head of an elephant and the body of a lion respectively.

During this period a new technique of luminosity called chaaya-tapa (shade-and- shine) was introduced. A soft glow that can be seen on the outline of the body against the slightly darker background. This technique has succeeded in casting that unreal, mystical light which makes the sacred countenances of the Buddhas and Bodhi-sattvas radiate with a mysterious glow.

The illusion of ethereal light gently thrown on the Bodhi-sattva from his left produces a soft shadow on the right side of the face and neck. But unlike normal chiaroscuro, the left side of the face is also treated with a deeper brown tone than the softly highlighted right cheek and nose. In conformity with the Ajanta tradition, there are no cast shadows. These are scrupulously shunned by the artists who realised, as did Leonardo da Vinci in his time, that direct light and cast shadows destroy the representation of true relief or volume.

The luminosity is further enhanced by the masterly handling of blue, lapis lazuli, sparingly employed on the necklace beads and delicately applied on the fringes of the fresh lotus. This is reflected in the warmth of the golden brown and pearl grey of the Bodhi-sattva's complexion. At the same time, perspective is created in the background by the relative depth in space of the blue jacket of the woman behind the Padma-paani's left arm, and of the blue birds in the conventional bands of hills in the top left-hand corner of the picture.

To the left of the Bodhi-sattvaa is painted Padma-paani’s Consort [Kbl 068]. She is holding a lotus to indicate that she is his consort. She is one of the most graceful women of Ajanta, exuding serenity and love. Also another instance of ‘black is beautiful’!

On the left part of the panel are the Mithuna Figures [ASI 022] watching the Great Being from their hill resort as they sit in an amorous mood, exhibiting an incredible bhaava-madhura (tender feeling) of reciprocal love. The elegance of the contentment and modesty of the figures is stressed more by the almost symbolic tenderness of the soft caress, than from the facial expressions.

A number of mithuna figures have been painted below the eight Buddhas in Cave 17. This painting may be compared with prince and his Consort [Kbl 075] a scene from Shibi Jaataka which depicts the same subject in the same cave, but provides a complete contrast in the manner of drawing and colouring. Prince and his Consort [Kbl 075] is a good example of the animnonnata (flat) style of shading consisting of flat irregular planes of primary colours. But Mithuna Figures [ASI 022] is done using the nimnonnata (high relief) treatment of painting, retaining the softness and roundness of the limbs, demarcated only by a slight deepening of the colours along the edges.

The restraint of the sacred style of painting in the Bodhi-sattvas is beautifully balanced by the lively background and the warm atmosphere of the secular scene. The background is infused with the colours of the luxuriant foliage of the areca palms and the ashoka trees, the strong red squares like pillars and cross-pieces which are a convention for the ridges of hills, the clear blue of the bird's feathers, and a variety of other colours which enliven the drapery and jewellery and even the skin-tones of the men and women.

There is a female chaawri-bearer wearing a long blue coat, while on the right is a dark mace-bearer in a long white coat. In the background monkeys are seen frolicking about and a pair of peacocks shrilling in joy. In the left corner is a Kinnara playing the Lute [Kbl 071T] quite similar to the present day mandolin, and cymbals, giving us an idea of the kind of musical instruments used at that time. We come across musicians and contemporary musical instruments in the scenes of Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091] and Mahaa-janaka leaves the Palace [Kbl 096] the narration of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka of Cave 1 and in the painting Descent Of Indra [Kbl 164] in Cave 17.
