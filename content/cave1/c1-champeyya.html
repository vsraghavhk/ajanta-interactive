Champeyya Jaataka

Cave 1, 4th century, AD

The story of Champeyya is painted on the wall of the rear verandah, left of Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara above the doors of the two cells.

The Jaataka: The Bodhi-sattva was once born as Champeyya, the naaga king. He felt unhappy with his wasteful life, and attempted to take his life. His wife Sumana helped to revive his interest in life and was his constant companion. Once he was caught by a cruel Brahmin, and taught him dancing. The Brahmin made the serpent-king dance before King Uggasena. Meanwhile, Sumana, who was searching for her lost husband, finally found her husband in the court of King Uggasena entertaining the king with his dance. On her plea the king released the serpent from captivity. It is then that King Uggasena came to know that the snake was none other than Bodhi-sattva born as the naaga king. Bodhi-sattva took King Uggasena to the naaga-world, where he gave him a sermon. Later he helped the king to vanquish his enemies.

The artist has delineated the story in five scenes, arranged in two rows. The top row depicts three scenes and on the bottom row the remaining two scenes. The entire jaataka is given in the sketch Story of Champeyya [Line 17], which may be referred to while following the individual scenes.

In the first scene, Champeyya Repents his Life of Pleasures [Kbl 105] shown at the extreme left of the top row, we see the Bodhi-sattva, born as naaga-king, Champeyya sitting on a throne repenting of his life of pleasures. Sumana, his queen is sitting by his side and is looking up eagerly towards her husband. As usual, the court dwarf is in attendance, this time holding a tray of fruits, flowers and cosmetics, but the way he looks up eagerly at the king shows his concern. There are two more maids-in-attendance in this scene.

To watch the next scene, King Uggasena watches Snake-Charmer's Tricks [Kbl 106T] we must go to the extreme right. The snake-charmer, we find, is on the extreme right. His face is damaged. The serpent is in front of him, uncoiling out of the basket. King Uggasena, with his head completely missing, is seen seated on a throne. The folds of his upper garments are artistically drawn and as usual the bolster at his back has interesting motifs on it. With his right hand he has made a gesture, expressive of wonder. His minister is sitting on his left. The serpent-king, Champeyya, while showing his dances, suddenly sees something that makes him stop midway in his dance.

As a part of the second scene we find some courtiers also watching the snake show. Among the Courtiers watch the Snake-Dance [ASI 036] there are two very interesting figures, whose racial traits and dress are very characteristic. The features of one of them, the moustache, and the tuft of hair on the crown of his head, are so typical that even today he might be taken for a citizen of Varanasi. His upper-cloth is of silver brocade, with floral patterns of silk worked on it, is reminiscent of the dress and the famous brocade industry of the place.

The features of the other figure are like those of a Brahmin of Orissa. The style of his hair and the manner in which he has thrown a part of his dhoti across his shoulder are also characteristic of the Orissa people. The poses of the figures are extremely interesting, particularly the homely way in which the Oriya is sitting and looking at his Varanasi comrade.

Between the two panels described above, we have the Appearance of Sumana and her Child. A mother with her child is seen appealing to a doorkeeper for admission to the court. This, in fact, was the reason why the naaga-king had halted his dance, for he suddenly recognises them as his own wife and son.

The part of the story of the king's recognition of the serpent as the naaga-king and the reason why he came to such a pitiable state has not been painted in this panel. The remaining story is painted in the bottom row.

Moving right, we come to a royal hall full of ladies and attendants, where Champeyya gives a Sermon [Kbl 106B] initiating Uggasena into the mysteries of the faith. Of the female figures, the most charming is that of Queen Sumana, standing above the left shoulder of Champeyya having a serpent-hood over her head. Her features are exquisite and her face has a delicate lambent grace.

There are as many as seven maids-in-attendance in different poses and attitudes. The Three Maids listening to the Sermon [ASI 038] are typical Ajanta beauties in graceful poses. One of the three male figures is our inexorable dwarf. The dwarf has raised his eyebrows to show his indifference to the serious sermons of the Bodhi-sattva.

The second male figure is that of an old servant standing on the right of Uggasena near the pillar. Here again we find the artist in a lighter vein, for the servant is on the point of helping himself to some fruit from a tray held by a girl who is too rapt in listening to the sermon to notice the theft. Another girl appears to be apprehensive of the mischief and without turning her head is glaring at the thief out of the corner of her eyes.

The third male figure is that of the naaga-King's bodyguard. Dressed in a long silk-coat with flowers interwoven in it and with tight sleeves, he is armed with a sword having a jewelled handle suggesting that he is the king's bodyguard. He has a cap on his head with a round brim and peaked top. Most likely, he is a foreigner. The scene also illustrates the court-architecture.

The Ajanta artist has a typical sense of humour. He portrays incidences in a lighter vein in rather serious situations. One instance was referred to in this narration. There are a few more of examples. In Cave 2, a humorous scene is sculpted under the figure of Hariti. On a pedestal we find a group of five boys goading two rams to fight, a common mischief of the ubiquitous backbenchers and truants even today!
