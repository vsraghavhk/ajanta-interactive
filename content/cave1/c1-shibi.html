Shibi Jaataka

Cave 1, 6th century AD

The story of the generous King Shibi must have been very popular. Anecdotes of this king are found in Hindu and Jain lore. In Buddhist literature also several stories are attributed to the charitable King Shibi. Two incidents are painted in Ajanta, the story of the king rescuing a pigeon in this Shibi Jaataka of Cave 1, and, the story of Shibi donating his eyes to a blind person in Shibi Jaataka of Cave 17. The full ‘pigeon and hawk’ anecdote, with scales for weighing an equivalent weight of flesh, is not to be found in the earlier versions of the jaataka stories.

The Jaataka: Bodhi-sattva was born as King Shibi, the benevolent monarch. Gods Yama and Indra wanted to test his magnanimity. Indra took the form of a pigeon and Yama that of a pursuing hawk. The pigeon sought protection from the king. But the hawk claimed the pigeon as his legitimate prey and appealed for justice. The king, in order to protect the surrendered pigeon, offered a portion of his own flesh equal in weight to that of the pigeon. As he started cutting his flesh and putting it on the scale, the pigeon became heavier and heavier. Finally the king himself had to climb on to the scale. It is then that the gods revealed their true forms and showered great boons on him.

The story in this cave is depicted in three episodes on the inside wall of the front verandah, between the main doorway and the window to the left of it.

The first episode starts on the left. This shows King Shibi in a palace surrounded by ladies of the court when the pigeon alights on his lap to seek refuge. This part of the painting is greatly damaged. Towards the right there is Royal Pavilion [Kbl 072] containing the detail, Prince And His Consort [Kbl 075]. The second and main episode, King weighs Himself [Kbl 074], begins immediately to the right of this pavilion. In this scene the king is standing by the side of the scales and appears to be on the point of sacrificing his own flesh to save the life of the pigeon. The three lovely figures in Wailing Women [Mjt 051] are among the five figures on the right-hand side of the Prince. The Bodhi-sattva's ordeal is the cause of their lament.

The third episode, which is painted below the two just mentioned, is damaged almost beyond recognition; but a few fragments show that it is the climax in which the gods, human beings, animals and birds are rejoicing at the Raja's success.

In both the Hinaayana and the Mahaayana phases of development inflection of line and gradation of tone achieve the illusion of volume. The Ajanta artist could choose between two techniques to create an illusion of depth and relief. One was animnonnata (flat) style of painting, where representation is achieved by simple juxtaposition of flat areas of contrasted colours in well-defined planes and, the other was nimnonnata (high-relief) style, in which this effect was brought out by various methods of shading (vartana). But what is more noteworthy is that the two styles, as a number of painting show, seem to overlap at every stage of the development of the art. This is in contrast with the Western tradition where the development of art is generally linear.

Wailing Women [Mjt 051] and Prince And His Consort [Kbl 075], details in this narration, are examples of this animnonnata style. This has a remarkable parallel with the ‘cloisonnage’ of Gauguin. These figures, set against the green leaves of a peepal tree beyond which the vermilion horizon can be seen, produce a pleasing colour pattern.

In Shibi Jaataka of Cave 17 another episode from the life of the benevolent king where he gave his own eyes to a needy blind Brahmin is painted.
