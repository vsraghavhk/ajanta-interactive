Conversion of Nanda

Cave 1; 5th Century AD

Conversion of Nanda of Cave 1 is an episode from the life of the Buddha and some scenes from it are painted above the window on the inside wall of the front verandah to the left of the main doorway, beyond the narration of Shibi Jaataka of Cave 1.

The Story: After attaining Enlightenment, the Buddha converted both his wife and his son to the monastic order, to lead a life of compassion and austerity. But his cousin, Nanda, was weak at heart and was initially reluctant to join the order, but later converted to monkhood. He had his head shaved and went about begging as a mendicant. On hearing of this his wife Janapada-Kalyaani swooned. However Nanda was not really happy being a monk and was brooding over his beautiful wife. The Buddha wanted to show him the right path. The Buddha took him to the heavenly abode of Indra, the lord of the gods. Nanda could see women more beautiful than his wife and wanted to stay there permanently. He was then ridiculed by the others for favouring a life of pleasure that is transitory. Bitten with remorse Nanda came to his senses. Coming back to the earth he lived the life of a monk following the Law.

There is also a view that the scene depicts the happenings at Kapila-vastu, when the Buddha paid a visit to his father and then went to the chamber of his wife Yashodhara to beg alms of her.

The mural is greatly damaged, but from what is surviving we have a fair idea of the composition from the line sketch of Bhikshu at the Palace Door [Line 19].

We have in this scene Nanda now a Bhikshu [Kbl 079] standing at the door of his own house begging for alms. He is calm, dignified, and radiating a lambent glow of compassion. He has come to his own house seeking food.

Inside the apartment on the left, an attendant is seen announcing the arrival of Nanda as a Bhikshu to Princess Janapada-Kalyaani, [ASI 006] Nanda's wife. The attendant seems very excited. He kneels down deferentially to announce the Bhikshu. His dress is fascinating, almost modern, and we come across this dress in other scenes. Perhaps it was the standard livery for palace attendants! To his right is a Maid with a Tray [Kbl 081L] in her hands. Despite the damage, her poise is exquisite. There is another female figure to her right sitting face-to-face with the princess. With her right hand she is seen explaining something to her mistress.

Janapada-Kalyaani [ASI 006], sitting on a throne with down cast eyes, has a melancholy expression. She is extremely graceful and dominates the entire scene. The graceful fingers of her left hand show the agitation in her mind. The dark-green backdrop presents a contrast to the reddish brown and yellow colours of the central figure. The colour used for backwash, it can be seen, is evenly laid without many nuances, so as not to distract from the main figure.

There are two more damsels in a dejected mood behind the princess.

The custom of women decorating their forehead with a tilak, also known as bindi, is a common sight today. But, in the Ajanta portraiture we find women sporting tilak only infrequently. Perhaps this became mandatory in the Hindu way of life later. In this mural we find that both the princess and the lady-attendant with a tray are both wearing the bindi.

The confusion and excitement created within the palace and witnessed in the expressions of the attendants on seeing the king in the garb of a beggar, the agitated and melancholy look of the princes, all combine to tell the same story.

There is one more detail of this story in the greatly damaged painting. In this we see a Maid tries to revive the Princeess [ASI 007]. A well-built maid with a glowing complexion tends her mistress, not included in the picture, a suffering princess reclining on a couch. She bends over the rails holding a pitcher in her hands; while water and flower-petals pour out, she literally pours herself. The scene is saturated with pathos. It is an example of one of the many damsels ‘dressed in nakedness’ in the Ajanta imagery. The painter has utilised all artistic means in order to emphasise the theme in its minute details, making all the accompaniments support the emotional content of the motif.

The circular lines of the pitcher balance the curves of the maid's body, though all the curves stroking the body-line, head or bust, are downward and heavy-laden. The anxious eyes of the attendant shower benediction. The upper limbs of the body, the neck, the bust and the waist, and the hips, are delineated in great detail. The pitcher-like breasts stand in incredible contrast to her slim waist. The colours are soft, and the artist has avoided sharp contrasts keeping in line with the melancholic mood of the scene.

Conversion of Nanda story must have been popular then, as we find this story is depicted in Cave 16 as well. We will be witnessing a few very poignant scenes of the narrative in this cave, like Dying Princess [ASI 003] wherein the same scene is depicted with even greater force and feeling. The composition is also different. The Ajanta artist was not monotonous.
