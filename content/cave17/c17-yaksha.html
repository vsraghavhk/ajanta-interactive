Yaksha Couple and Black Apsaras

Cave 17, 5th century, AD

To the right of the central door we see a few gandharva-s and kinnara-s being a part of Indra's train, floating through clouds towards the earth to worship the Buddha. Among these celestials proceeding to pay homage to the Buddha are a Yaksha-Couple [Kbl 168] and a Black Apsaras [Kbl 169] flying effortlessly through clouds, strong winds swaying their ornaments and tassels.

Flying celestials are common in Indian paintings and sculpture. Flying vidyaadhara-s are frequently seen behind the main deities, on the brackets, and in any place the artist feels would highlight the theme. We saw Indra descending to the earth to adore the Buddha. The treatise Vishnu-dharmottara prescribes the qualifications for the painter. It says: "He who paints waves, flames, smoke, streamers fluttering in the air, according to the movement of the wind, should be considered a great painter." Accordingly Ajanta painters took great pleasure in composing scenes involving movement with great zest.

The floating clouds, the swaying foliage and apsaras and gandharva-s flying swiftly through the air, produce a fantastic movement and excitement, and every connoisseur of art was intoxicated by its beauty. Often this depiction is considered to have been influenced by Kalidasa's poetry. Or is it the other way about?

We shall start with the Black Apsaras [Kbl 169]. A line sketch Black Beauty (line ?) may also be referred to. One of the most beautiful single heads in the entire Ajanta complex is that of this flying apsaras, commonly known as Black Apsaras (nymph). She drifts through the air while beating out a measure on tiny cymbals. Her beautiful jewelled ornaments would seem to place this picture in the class of painting described in the shaastra-s (canons) as most admired by women. Around her neck is a necklace of diamonds, sapphires, and pearls, its heavy pendants swaying with the nymph's movement. She wears matching strings of jewels in her hair. The pendulum movement of all the dangling ropes of precious stones to the right is echoed in the direction of the stiff white riband behind her head: the direction of all these ornaments subtly indicates the apsaras's flight towards the left of the composition.

The face of this apsaras is in the form of an elongated ovoid, almost the leaf shape often recommended in the shaastra-s. This, along with her narrow eyes and tiny mouth, suggests wistfulness and ethereality. The head has a haunting evocation of sensuous warmth, and, as so often at Ajanta, the hands pliant, elegant and infinitely graceful. Both in the lyrical beauty of the physical types and rich contrast between the dark flesh and the shining jewels, the paintings of Cave 17 are close in style to the murals at Bagh, in the state of Madhya Pradesh, some 250 km away.

Regarding the dark complexion of this apsaras, it is worth recalling that we come across with many black beauties in Ajanta. In fact, most of the beautiful women we see in Ajanta are dark complexioned. Thus, Shakti Pandara [ASI 031], the dark princess, painted in the panel of Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara in Cave 1, Princess Irandati of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka in Cave 2, The Dying Princess [ASI 003] of Cave 16 and this famous Black Apsaras, who are all painted ‘as dark as the night’ have already been referred to. This may be, it was felt, due to the southern influence. This line of thinking was due to the fact that Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshwara depicted in the same panel as that of the dark beauty, Shakti Pandara [ASI 031], is attributed to the southern influence.

Perhaps, at some time in the past Indian aesthetics accepted black as beautiful. Weren't both Rama and Krishna dark complexioned?

In the entourage we find a Yaksha-Couple [Kbl 168]. The composition is really sculpturesque!

This painting is another example how the Ajanta artist has handled the various techniques for plastic modelling, particularly in making a choice between vartana (shading) and ujjotana (adding-highlights) to suit a given theme. The sparkling diamonds, sapphires and pearls in her necklace and other jewellery proclaim this. The ujjotana method of highlighting applied to the chin and cheek is soft and subtle, keeping in view the mood of the subject matter, an example of classical restraint. This can be contrasted with a more liberal application of white patches to the characters of Vidhura-pandita Jaataka in Cave 2, heralding the beginning of mannerism in Ajanta.
