Vishvaantara Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th Century, AD

A number of episodes from the Vishvaantara Jaataka are painted in this cave and cover a large portion of the wall on the left aisle, except for the first scene which is painted on the left side of the main verandah. Belonging to the classical period of Ajanta, the lovely composition provides a wealth of information about the prevailing taste of this period.

The Jaataka: It is the story of Bodhi-sattva born as Prince Vishvaantara who became famous for his generosity. It may be mentioned that as per Buddhist belief this was the last Bodhi-sattva incarnation before being born as the Buddha. Vishvaantara was the son of King Sanjaya and queen Phusati in the city of Jetuttara. He exhibited his generosity from the time of his birth. But what angered his subjects was his gifting of his magical elephant, which could bring rain to the neighbouring kingdom of Kalinga, which was going through severe famine. To assuage their feeling King Sanjaya was constrained to send his only son in exile. Vishvaantara, who left the kingdom along with his wife Maadri and two children Jaali and Kanhajina gifted away his chariot and horses to needy persons. A Brahmin, Jujuka, then, begged him to give away his sons to alleviate his sufferings. The benevolent prince complied. Jujuka ill-treated the boys and took them to Jetuttara, where people recognised the captive boys as the grandchildren of their king and felt deeply remorseful for banishing their prince. The king summoned Vishvaantara and Maadri from their hermitage. He paid a handsome ransom to the greedy Brahmin to release the two boys. Thus the family came to be happily reunited.

The drama starts with Vishvaantara distributes Alms [kbl 159] painted on the left side of the outer verandah. A large congregation of beggars, ascetics and attendants surround Prince Vishvaantara and Princess Maadri. Dark in complexion Vishvaantara is sitting on a low stool. The lower part of his figure is damaged. The expectation in the face of the assembly is well portrayed. Of some interest are the square shaped umbrellas some of the people are carrying. The scene faithfully depicts the usual commotion associated with any alms giving ceremony and the attempt by the royal attendants in trying to maintain order.

The second scene of Vishvaantara informing Maadri of his banishment and subsequent departure is painted above the entrance to the first cell. This is shown in the line sketch Banishment of Vishvaantara [Line 06] and two colour reproductions Vishvaantara consoles his Wife [Kbl 161] and Vishvaantara’s departure [Kbl 162].

In this scene, Vishvaantara consoles his Wife [Kbl 161], we find the prince, seated on a low throne in the pavilion is supporting Maadri, his wife, with his left hand and with the right offering her some stimulating drink. The expression of the couple shows anxiety and concern. The demeanour of the princess is extremely graceful. She leans on her beloved with complete submission. Her face is ravishing. With her dreamy half-closed eyes she looks obliquely at her consort. Her distress is indicated by her clinging to her husband for support and the prince's concern by his proffering her a cup of wine. The figure of the prince, badly damaged, registers a look of anxiety.

Beside the royal couple there are three figures inside the pavilion. The ubiquitous dwarf holding a vessel with raised eyes is standing in front. We have seen similar dwarfs in almost all the royal scenes. The third attendant in the composition is the one standing beside the pillar, wearing a greenish jacket and a Persian skullcap, and holding a liquor-jug, ready to pour out the contents into the cup of the prince. It will be noted that the glances of all the participants of this high drama converge on the hapless prince. There is one more female figure squatting Indian style with a water-pitcher on her lap.

The pavilion is supported by four round pillars. The wall and the pillars are reddish, while the parapet is yellow and the floor green with a sprinkling of white flowers here and there.

The whole scene exudes melancholy.

What follows is the scene of Vishvaantara’s departure [Kbl 162] to the left of the earlier picture. In this we witness the young royal couple leaving the palace watched by the attendants.

The central figures are identified by the umbrellas held over their heads, signifying their royalty. Also the physiognomy of Vishvaantara and Maadri remains unchanged: Vishvaantara depicted as dark complexioned with hair falling over the shoulders and with hair-band. The princess has two maids-in-attendance on either side, in addition to a male attendant with a drooping moustache. The prince is barefooted and clad only in a loincloth and some flowers in his hair.

Among the people present we see prominently a beggar with a crooked staff, possibly testing the generosity of the prince. Between the pavilion and the garden is painted a window, through which the king and the queen watch the departure of their beloved son.

The narration continues above the space between the third and fourth cell-doors, with Vishvaantara and Maadri taking leave of the king in one pavilion and then the queen in another pavilion.

To the left of the previous scene is depicted Vishvaantara informs his Wife [Kbl 186]. This is a repetition of an earlier scene. Here the artist has tactfully placed them at right angles to each other, as if they are seeing each other. Why this scene was necessary, when the same scene was enacted before, and more forcefully, we may never know! It may be that the story is told again starting from this scene. This guess is prompted by the fact that the narration is continuous from here to the end of the jaataka. The portrayal of both Vishvaantara and Maadri is superb bringing out the compassionate and gentle nature of the royal couple.

The Ajanta artist did not just paint scenes of religious themes on the wall, but embellished them with beautiful details of everyday happenings which may in no way be connected with the main story. For we have an exquisite painting of Maid braids the Hair [Kbl 187] of a woman.

In the middle of the wall between the second and third cell-doors, that is somewhat below the previous scene, the prince and his family are seen departing in a decorated carriage drawn by four horses. Among the crowd are disconsolate Citizens watch Vishvaanta’s Departure [Kbl 188R] portrays the departure of the prince and his family leaves the city. The composition is one of the masterpieces of Ajanta.

Let us now follow the procession. In front of the chariot are four Brahmins. The composition is interesting for the depiction of the market place through which the chariot passes. We see a shop with the milkman with two pails of milk and a basin of curd. There is shop where the oil-vendor is ladling oil into a cup. A weighing machine can be seen in the next grocer's shop. The buildings are two storied with shops in the ground floor and residences on the upper floor. Women are watching the departure of the prince from the open windows of these upper-floors. This reminds one of a similar scene in Mad Elephant Nalagiri [Kbl 172] in the episode called Miracle at Raja-griha, also in this cave, where a market scene is depicted.

In the next scene we find the royal family standing on the ground, and we should presume that the chariot has been donated to the four Brahmins we saw in the last scene.

Then follows the depiction of their life in Mount Vamka. In the damaged mural we can barely see the daily chore of Maadri, like collecting fruits in the wilderness, etc.

The next scene depicts the gifting away of his sons to a greedy Brahmin, Jujuka. Vishvaantara is seen in front of a hut made of leaves (parna-shaala). Vishvaantara informs Maadri of the development. Vishvaantara is sitting in front of the same hut and the figure of Maadri is drawn with the greatest care. She is a personification of sorrow.

In the next scene of happy reunion of the royal family, painted above the fourth cell door entrance, we see the events that take place in the court of King Sanjaya. The description of this scene is supported by a sketch King Sanjaya’s Court [Line 09] and a colour picture Restoration of Children [ASI 071].

At the left bottom corner we see the earlier sequence that of the guard enquiring of Jujuka, the greedy Brahmin, about the children. The guard is adorned with jewellery and the bejewelled sword hanging from his belt is very prominent. The posture of this guard recalls that of the guard of Avalokiteshwara in Cave 1. King Sanjaya is seated on a low throne with a rich diadem over his head and some precious jewellery on his person. He has a conventional lion-waist (simha-kati). In front of him Jujuka is seen receiving the ransom from the royal treasurer with his hands extended. To the left of the king is seen Queen Phusati seated on another stool. Between the king and the queen the artist has drawn Jaali, one of Vishvaantara’s children. The figure of Kanhajina, the other child, is on the left of the queen. At the foot of the king there are three noblemen apparently discussing among themselves the miraculous return of the children.

The finale of Coronation of Vishvaantara [Kbl 190] is mostly damaged, and what little remains, it is claimed, seems to have been retouched in a later period. The coronation scene, however, is well conceived. Vishvaantara is seen sitting in a throne while holy water is being poured over him. Queen Maadri is watching it from a pavilion on to his right. There are two women carrying offerings on a tray, both of them in striking graceful tribhanga (triflex) poses.

While describing Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani [Kbl 069] in Cave 1 it was mentioned that, according to the canons of Indian painting, the limbs of human characters are related to the shapes and forms of the vegetal and animal worlds. Among the examples cited was the comparison of torso with the body of a lion. The torso of King Sanjaya in this panel is lion-waisted (simha-kati), and is an example of such sadrishya (similitude).

The figure of Jujuka in this series of paintings shows the mastery of the Ajanta artist in character-study. The artist has delineated all his ugly features - the broken front teeth, the goatee beard, the bald forehead and the spiky hair at the back of his skull, the parrot-like nose and small uncanny eyes, combined with the brightness which has come over his face at the jingle of the money which the royal treasurer is pouring into his outspread scarf.

Two more points arrest attention. Firstly, the continuity of costume and of make up. The artist has not forgotten that a corner of the Brahmin's umbrella is torn! This has been repeated carefully in all the scenes. Secondly the posture of one's ‘hair standing on end in expectation’ has been literally given expression to while drawing Jujuka at the moment of receiving the golden coins.
