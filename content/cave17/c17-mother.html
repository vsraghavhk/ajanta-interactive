Mother and Child before the Buddha

Buddha in Kapila-vastu

Cave 17; 5th century, AD

This mural, Mother and Child before the Buddha [Kbl 200], is considered the best piece of art at Ajanta. It is most unfortunate that this fresco has been damaged to a great extent during the last one and a half centuries. Splendid reproductions of this picture have been made by some of the pioneer visitors. The scene is painted on the rear wall of the ante-chamber to the left of the door leading to the shrine.

The scene shows Yashodhara and Rahula, wife and son of the Buddha before he renounced the world, meeting the Lord, when the latter came begging to the palace-door during his sojourn at Nigro-dharama Vihaara on the outskirts of Kapila-vastu.

The sketch The Buddha in Kapila-vastu [Line 12] and the Mother and Child before the Buddha [Kbl 200] and its detail the Mother and Child [Kbl 201] a reproduction in colour aid in following the description of the mural.

To the left is the massive standing figure of the Buddha measuring about 3 metres in height. The features of his face are obliterated but his extended hand holding the begging bowl and the angle of his head towards Yashodhara, in fact his entire gesture shows his compassion mixed with love. The orange colour of his long robe contrasts well with the dark tint of the background, which is again relieved here and there of its sombreness and monotony by the sprinkling of white flowers. The golden-brown head stands out almost in relief against the light green of the halo. The same effect of alto-relievo sculpture is noticeable between the jade-green begging bowl and the orange robe and the brown head.

In front of the Buddha stands Yashodhara with young Rahula. Rahula was only seven days old when his father renounced the world. Now he is a boy of seven years. He looks up at his father for the first time in his life. In his look we find awe and wonder mingled with love and respect. The figure of Yashodhara is superb. The artist has attributed to her all the charms of natural beauty. She has bedecked herself profusely with ornaments, perhaps with the faint hope of enticing Him to return to her.

It is true that we cannot say, from the faintness of the figure, whether the Blessed One notices in front of him his wife and child, or just a mother and a boy - just two entities worthy of receiving His universal love and compassion. But Yashodhara's look speaks more of love than of wonder. The poses of the heads of the mother and the child in the direction of the figure of the Buddha exhibits purity of form.

The artist has deified the Buddha by increasing the dimension of the figure, but he has added some mystic effect by not drawing the outlines with any firm and distinct brush-work. The Buddha thus appears more like an ethereal entity, more like a formless idea than a human being. In contrast, the figures of the mother and the child, though diminutive in size, are clearly drawn in detail. They belong to the earth, full of worldly emotions and feelings.

Laurence Binyon said: "I remember no picture anywhere more profoundly impressive in grandeur and in tenderness."

The scene is depicted in relief sculpture in Cave 19.
