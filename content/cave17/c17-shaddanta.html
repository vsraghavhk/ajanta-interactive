Shad-danta Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th century, AD

The Shad-danta Jaataka is painted in Cave 10 during the pre-Christian Hinaayana period. The same jaataka has been narrated in this cave, after a gap of about six hundred years. A comparative study of the two compositions would be rewarding, as this brings into focus both the continuity and the variation in the artistic styles over a period of these six hundred years. The painter has drawn Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 17 on the outer wall of the front corridor to the left of the main entrance.

The Jaataka: The jaataka is the story of Bodhi-sattva born as an elephant-king with six tusks, hence called, Shad-danta. While playing with his two wives, one of them, Chulla-subhadda, becomes angry as she feels that her husband neglects her in preference to the other queen. She, in the next birth was born as the Queen of Varanasi. She commands her men to get her the tusks of Shad-danta, in revenge. But when the tusks were actually brought, she breaks down and repents.

The story is depicted in four scenes: first, the queen's bedroom scene in which Queen plans her Revenge; second, the lotus lake in which we see Sporting Shad-danta [Kbl 179] with his herd; third, Sonuttara brings the Tusks [Kbl 180T] of Shad-danta to the royal court; and fourth, the Queen faints [Kbl 180B] on seeing them. The second to the fourth scenes are shown in the line sketch Shand-danta Story of Cave 17 [Line 01].

In the second scene, shown in the middle we find sporting Shad-danta [Kbl 179] with his herd in the beautiful lake. The lotus flowers on the surface of the water, barely visible at the bottom of the left side, vividly suggest the animals' carefree life in the jungle.

In the lower portion of the right hand panel is depicted the third scene. First, we find the six-tusked Shad-danta and his followers. The hunter, Sonuttara, has been drawn thrice. First, he stoops low before the Bodhi-sattva, in great reverence. In the second, he looks back at the elephant with gratitude while balancing the tusks on a pole. In the third, Sonuttara brings the Tusks [Kbl 180T] from Varanasi.

In the last episode, painted to the left, the Queen faints [Kbl 180B] at the sight of the six tusks. It appears as a framed picture in the mural.

In terms of style this composition is vastly different from the great mural narrating the same jaataka, Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 10. While the arrangement was horizontal in the earlier mural, it is executed in a zigzag fashion here. The composition of the scene in the two paintings is different. While she was seated in Cave 10, we find her fainting in the arms of the king here, like Maadri reclining on Vishvaantara in the scene Vishvaantara consoles his Wife [Kbl 161] in the narration of Vishvaantara Jaataka, painted on the outer verandah in the very same cave. Further, the drawing of the royal bedchamber within a frame is unusual even by the standards of the day. Continuity is also witnessed from the painting of elephants from the Hinaayana painting of the 2nd century BC of Cave 10 to this composition.

Though not connected with the narration directly Monkey beneath a Palas Tree [ASI 009] is an interesting painting. The relaxed, dignified calm of the monkey, consisting basically of one masterly sweep of a long stroke of the brush starting beneath the chin and forming a curve outlining the head and spine and terminating beneath the knee-cap, is a fine example of the translation of closely observed scenes and moments of action or repose into simple yet profoundly explicit drawings. An equally interesting feature of the scene (not shown in the colour reproduction) is row of ants on one of the branches painted by the artist. The Ajanta artist has paid minute attention to represent all kinds of living beings, including ants!
