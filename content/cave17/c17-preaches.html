The Buddha preaching to the Congregation

The Miracle of Ascending the Heaven of Thirty-three Gods

Cave 17, 5th century

The theme of this painting is one of the four miracles performed by the Buddha. He ascended the Heaven of Thirty-three Gods and preached the Dharma to His Mother at Shaankashya, one of the eight holy places in the Buddhist faith. They are referred to as attha-mahaa-thaanaani.

Two more miracles have been depicted in Ajanta. His multiplying Himself a thousand-fold, called the Miracle of Shraavasti, is depicted in Cave 2 and is described in the scene Thousand Buddhas [Kbl 118]. The Miracle at Raaja-griha, the subjugation of a mad elephant at Raaja-griha is also painted in this cave. Shankaashya, where this miracle is believed to have taken place is known to day as Sankissa or Sankasia, in the district of Farukkhabad in the sate of Uttar Pradesh.

The Miracle: The Buddha went to the Heaven of Thirty-three Gods at the invitation of Indra (called Shakra in the Buddhist mythology). There he preached to the congregation of gods, which included His mother Mahaa-maaya. After this, He returned to the earth by the three-ladder made by Indra. On reaching the world He found a large assemblage of monks and noblemen waiting to hear His sermon. This theme is painted on the left wall of the ante-chamber.

The episode is composed in three rows. In the top row we see the Buddha preaching in heaven, in the middle we see the Buddha descending and in the bottom panel, the Master is giving a sermon before a congregation of people on earth. A major part is given in the sketch Preaching of the Buddha (Line ?).

The top panel is rather damaged, but some figures are identifiable. Close to the Buddha we see a group of women, including the mother of the Buddha Mahaa-maaya listens to the Sermon [Kbl 202]. Maaya-devi is ‘dressed in nakedness’, whereas the dresses of the other people look foreign. On the other side are the gods.

In the middle panel the Buddha can be seen descending to the earth. He is accompanied by His disciples and the gods. The Compassionate One is in His usual saffron robe.

The bottom row is better preserved. The characters comprising the audience are truly heterogeneous.

In this bottom panel of we see the Buddha preaches to the Congregation [Kbl 197]. He is sitting in dharma chakra mudra (teaching-attitude) exuding warmth and compassion. His throne is ornamented. His chair-bearers are Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani and Bodhi-sattva Avalokiteshvara. There are also two flying cherubs behind him. On his left are a few people squatting on the ground. All of them look like monks. The elderly monk in the centre of the front row is identified as Shari-putta, one of His important disciples. The one to his right looking at the Master is Ananda, His cousin and disciple. The one behind Shari-putta with a checked upper-cloth is another disciple, Moggallana. We notice at least three men in royal uniform riding horses.

Then we have a Woman Elephant-Rider [Kbl 203] with her entourage. She must be a queen as there is an umbrella held over head. Some identify her as Maaya-devi, the mother of the Buddha. She is sitting with a bolster of interesting design at her back. Careful readers might have noticed the endless variety of textile designs in Ajanta.

On the left part of the panel, there is another group of Audience [Kbl 204]. Among the people sitting on the floor, in the first row, starting from the left, Ajaata-shatru, his father King Bimbi-saara of Magadha and King Prasenjita of Koshala. Behind Bimbi-saara is a person of foreign origin. There are also a number of people with curved swords held high. Behind them are two men on horses. They look like foreigners again. Further above them are two caparisoned elephants with raised trunks. The riders may be foreign statesmen.

This grand mural, where over fifty people participate, is a subject of great admiration by art critics all over the world.
