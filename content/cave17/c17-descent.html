Descent of Indra

Adoration of the Buddha

Cave 17, 5th century, AD

The scene, Descent of Indra [Kbl 164] is painted on the back wall of the veranda to the left of the central door and by the side of the paintings depicting the alms-giving episode of Vishvaantara Jaataka. In the continuous panorama of murals on this wall, this group of six figures could be singled out showing the Descent of Indra [Kbl 164] with his troupe of celestial musicians from the heavens above. This may be taken as a solo-picture for this can neither be connected with any incident in the life of the Buddha nor associated with a jaataka.

A line sketch Indra with his Entourage [Line 05] and the colour reproduction Descent of Indra [Kbl 164] may be consulted while following the following description of the mural.

The central figure, that of Indra, is the biggest in size. He is of fair complexion, embellished with rich jewellery in the form of necklaces, armlets, bangles, earrings and a magnificent crown over his head. He has a dagger and a sword attached to his belt. The painting of Indra illustrates the extraordinary breadth and sureness of the draughtsmanship. The individual features, eyes and nose and mouth, are defined with free, flowing sweeps of the brush. The varying thickness of the lines provides a kind of shading, and the very freedom of the drawing imparts both freshness and boldness to the forms as well as the decorations.

The suggestion of movement is unmistakable. The strings of pearls round Indra's neck all swing in the opposite direction to the movement. The ends of ribbons tied round the hair are also flying. To emphasise movement towards the right-bottom corner, the artist has skilfully drawn a cluster of clouds, piled up in the opposite direction that is, receding towards the left top. The delineation of the cloud in the form of white patches with blue rims of varying thickness are so drawn as to appear as successive columns of cumulus cloud-heaps.

To the right of Indra, is shown a gandharva (celestial) with coarse features, a bulbous nose and sunken eyes, carrying a slender musical instrument. The up-turned loose ends of his upper garment with yellow stripes over green background, also suggest movement. The illusion of movement in space is skilfully created through these suggestions and the bent knees of the figures.

During this descent, Indra is accompanied by celestial musicians. To the right of Indra, there are two celestial nymphs. The one nearer the central figure, with a greenish or greyish hue, holds a pair of cymbals in her hands. Her eyes are downcast. She appears to be engrossed in music, oblivious of her surroundings. The dark girl next to her, also playing a pair of cymbals, is looking back at her companion while levitating forward. These two girls are loaded with sapphires, diamonds and pearls, although their garments are scanty, exposing the contours of their full breasts. The younger person near the left leg of Indra has a musical instrument, a sort of drum. With a twist of the neck he is looking back out of the corner of his eyes.

The most striking figure of the entire group is that of the flute-player who is a little ahead of the floating group. She is in her early teens, and since her head is turned away the artist has availed himself of this opportunity of displaying her coiffure by dividing the dark chignon into four quadrants by means of ribbons.

Musicians have been shown in the scenes of Dancing Girl with Musicians [Kbl 091] and Mahaa-janaka leaves the Palace [Kbl 096] in the narration of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1 and in the Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani panel [Line 03].

We have seen in the other compositions, like the scenes Queen captivates Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L] and Mahaa-janaka leaves the Palace [Kbl 096] of Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1, that the Ajanta artist has used a variety of sthaana-s (body positions) while portraying characters

The suggestion of weightless grace and elegance in these figures reflects the postures of Indian dance and reminds us of the effortless flight in the Gupta sculpture or the Pallava sculpture at Mahabalipuram. The entire composition is rich in tonal harmony creating that gay mood, that idyllic charm mellowed with vibrant movement and music, which is the essence of this mural.
