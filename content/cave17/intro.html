Cave Seventeen

This vihaara, perhaps excavated a quarter of a century after Cave 16, has a similar plan. The verandah, about 20 meters long and about 3 meters wide, has massive pillars in front. The main hall has three entrances, and the central door is adorned with carving. The twenty columns dividing the corridors on the four sides of the hall, which is about 20 meters by 6 metres has an ante-chamber, which is about 5.6 meters square. The shrine contains a massive figure of the Buddha in the dharma chakra pravartana mudra (turning-the-Wheel-of-religion-attitude). The central figure is flanked by the Bodhi-sattva Padma-paani on the right and the Bodhi-sattva Vajra-paani on the left. A View of the Main Hall of Cave 17 [Kbl 157] shows the cloth canopy-like ceiling painting in the main hall along with glimpses of decorations on the pillars and the walls. On the other hand a sample of the murals on the walls, in balance with the ceiling paintings as seen in the left aisle is given in A View of the Aisle of Cave 17 [Nou 030].

The paintings in the vihaara are in a comparatively good condition and this cave, like Cave 1, is a veritable paradise for art-lovers. Some of the important paintings are:

The long narration Vishvaantara Jaataka is done in two parts. The story starts on the left verandah to the left side of main entrance and the first episode, Vishvaantara distributes Alms [kbl 159] is painted here. The next two scenes, Vishvaantara consoles his Wife [Kbl 161] and Vishvaantara's Departure [Kbl 162] are depicted beyond the door. The second version of the story occupies a large part of the left wall of the hall. The principal scenes are: Vishvaantara informs his Wife [Kbl 186], the departure of the royal couple, of which the Citizens watch Vishvaantara’s Departure [Kbl 188R] is a poignant detail, the Restoration of Children [ASI 071] in King Sanjaya’s Court and, finally, the Coronation of Vishvaantara [Kbl 190]. An interesting detail of the palace scene is Maid braids the Hair [Kbl 187].

Descent of Indra [Kbl 164] and other celestials flying to adore the Buddha may be found on the rear wall of the verandah to the left and right of the door. Among these celestials are a Yaksha-Couple [Kbl 168] and the famous Black Apsaras [Kbl 169]

Maanushi Buddhas and Mithuna Figures [Nou 161/162] is painted above the doorway in two rows.

The Miracle at Raja-griha, is an incident in the life of the Buddha when he subjugated a mad elephant. The miracle is depicted above the two windows to the right of the main door in the rear wall of the verandah, of which Deva-datta hatches a Conspiracy [Kbl 171], mad Elephant Nalagiri [Kbl 172] and the ultimate Subjugation of the Elephant [Kbl 173] are the surviving important scenes.

Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 17 is painted on the outer wall of the front corridor, to left of the main entrance. The principal scenes of this narration are, Queen plans her Revenge, sporting Shad-danta [Kbl 179] with his herd, Sonuttara brings the Tusks [Kbl 180T] of Shad-danta to the royal court and the Queen faints [Kbl 180B] on seeing them.

Two anecdotes from Mahaa-kapi Jaataka are painted in this cave. The first one is depicted on the wall of the front corridor to the left of the main entrance Mahaa-kapi saves his Herd (Line ?). The other episode is painted on the left-hand wall of the back corridor and the Monkey helps a Man [Kbl 192] and Man throws Stone on the Monkey [Kbl 193] are two of the important scenes.

Sutasoma Jaataka is narrated on the wall of the back corridor and continued on the bend of the wall towards the left above the cell door. Deer Hunt [Mjt 167], Court of Sudaasa [Kbl 195], Coronation of Saudaasa [Kbl 196], royal Kitchen of Saudaasa [Kbl 197T], in which Saudaasa tastes Human Flesh [Kbl 197B], Army drives Saudaasa to the Forest [Kbl 199] and King fights against his Army [Kbl 198] are some of the surviving important scenes of this story

Buddha preaches to the Congregation [Nou 197], Mahaa-maaya listens to the Sermon [Kbl 202], A Woman Elephant-Rider [Kbl 203] and the Audience [Kbl 204] are the principal scenes of the episode describing the Enlightened One preaching the Law in heaven and its sequel, both of which are painted on the left wall of the ante-chamber.

The masterpiece of the classical period, Mother and Child before the Buddha [Kbl 200] may be seen on the rear wall of the ante-chamber to the left of the shrine door. This is a scene from the visit of the Buddha to Kapila-vastu, of which Mother and Child [Kbl 201] is a detail.

The story of an elephant’s love for his blind parents, the Maatri-poshaka Jaataka is painted on the rear wall of the back corridor between the first and second cell-doors to the right of the ante-chamber and some of its scenes are painted in the Capture, Freedom and Reunion [Nou 192], of which The Happy Reunion with the blind Parents [Kbl 207B] is a detail.

Mahisha Jaataka [Nou 207], the story of a benevolent buffalo, is narrated in the right-hand wall of the rear corridor, to the left of the cell door.

Simhala Avadaana is a grand composition and belongs to the classical period of Ajanta. It covers the entire wall of the right-hand corridor and continued on the upper part of the pilaster between this corridor and the one behind it. The principal scenes are Raakshasi-s as beautiful Maidens [Kbl 211B] with details like Raakshasi feigns Love [ASI 080] and Merry Couples under Tents [Kbl 212] and a detail Dallying Couple under a Tent [Kbl 213] the havoc caused by Cannibalistic Raakshasi-s [Kbl 214], Escape of Simhala [Kbl 216B] and Merchants show their Gratitude to Bodhi-sattva [Kbl 217], Infatuated King [Kbl 218/219], Massacre in the Palace [Kbl 221], Vultures on the Gate [Kbl 223], Simhala’s Army gets Ready [Kbl 224], Simhala rides a white Elephant [ASI 078], and final victory over the raakshasi-s.

A solo-picture, A Lady at her Toilet [Kbl 048] is painted on the upper part of the pilaster between the front and right corridors.

The episode from the Shibi Jaataka of Cave 17, of a king giving away his eyes, is painted on the pilaster between the front and the right corridors and continued on the on the wall of the right corridor. Of this narration A Monastery [Kbl 227R] set up by the king to distribute alms and the royal scene after the king has donated his eyes, King Shibi in Pain (Line ?) and its details Wailing Woman -1 [Kbl 227B/L] and Wailing Woman - 2 [Kbl 227T/L] are taken for discussion.

The Mriga Jaataka, or the story of the golden deer, of which the King arrives in the Forest [Kbl 229], a Hunter attempts to capture the Deer [Kbl 230], The Deer carried on a Chariot [Kbl 231] and the King returns on a Horse-back [Kbl 232] are some of the important scenes narrated on the wall of the front corridor.

And, finally, Mahaa-hamsa Jaataka, of which a Fowler brings the Geese to the Court [Kbl 183] and the Golden Goose gives Sermon [Kbl 184] are two important scenes painted on the left wall and the adjoining pilaster of the front corridor, to the right of the cell door.

The cave has a number of surviving painting on the ceilings. The following are some of them. Six Gandharva-s [nou 155], each pair of adjoining gandharva-s having one arm common to both is one. The shamiana-like painting shown in the Interior View of Cave 17 [kbl 157], covering the ceiling of the main hall is a grand composition. Its detail is given in Geometrical Design [asi 067]. A View of the Aisle of Cave 17 [Nou 030] shows the decoration on the ceiling over the aisles, a detail of it in the common motif, The Squares and Rectangles - 3 [ASI 083]. And Resting Bulls [kbl 174], an interesting study of animals in repose, painted in one such square.
