Maatri-poshaka Jaataka

Cave 17; 6th Century, AD

Maatri-poshaka Jaataka is an exquisite composition painted on the rear wall of the cave between the first and the second cell-doors right of the ante-chamber. The narration in this cave varies slightly from the jaataka story.

The Jaataka: The story as painted runs on the following lines. Bodhi-sattva was born as a magnificent white elephant in the Himalayas. His both the parents were blind and the white elephant took care of them with utmost devotion. Once he helped a man who lost his way in the forest. King Brahma-datta of Varanasi had lost his royal elephant and was prepared to pay a large amount of money to anyone who helped him to get a suitable elephant. The greedy person informed the king of the beautiful mighty elephant he saw in the Himalayas. A happy king sent his army to catch the animal. The elephant, in spite of his might, allowed himself to be bound and brought to Varanasi. But in the royal stable where he was housed he refused to eat the sumptuous food offered to him. The astonished kind wanted to know the reason. He let loose the elephant and followed him along with his solders. On reaching the forest the elephant met his old, blind parents and caressed them lovingly. The king was overjoyed with the devotion shown by the animal to his parents and freed the animal in the forest.

In the jaataka, the elephant was supporting only his blind mother, and hence the title. (maatri-poshaka means ‘caring-for-the-mother’). But in the Ajanta narration the elephant was nursing both his mother and father.

The story is narrated in two rows, as given in the line sketch Parents-Caring Elephant (Line ?). It starts in the left of the top row with the person who was rescued by the elephant giving out the whereabouts of the white elephant to the king. The scene of the captured elephant being led to the city is painted at the left bottom of the mural. The elephant refusing to eat is shown on the right of the top row. The released animal walking majestically towards the forest is pictured above the captured elephant and the happy reunion is found to the right of the bottom row. The entire bottom row is shown as Capture, Freedom and Reunion [Nou 192], of which the Happy Reunion with the blind Parents [Kbl 207B] is a detail.

In the first scene, the king is sitting on a low throne with his queen standing by his side. A person standing to his left is giving information regarding the white elephant that he saw in the forest. A person, perhaps one of the ministers, is listening to the conversation; so do the two women from a window.

The next scene is shown at the left bottom of the lower row of Capture, Freedom and Reunion [Nou 192], where the captured elephant is led to Varanasi. He is brought under control with strong ropes tied around his hind legs. The elephant is preceded by two horse-riders and followed by a prince with an umbrella over his head.

The refusal of the elephant to eat is shown in the right of the top row. In this scene the king, seen at the centre of the panel, is supervising the feeding the elephant. The king is identified by the customary royal umbrella. To his right half hidden by the wall, the queen is peeping, perhaps watching the strange spectacle. The person to the right of the king is obviously informing him that the elephant is refusing to eat. The maid further to the right is carrying some edibles on a tray. Before the brooding elephant is seen some food in a large vessel and also sugar-canes are seen lying around.

The king, after having failed in his attempt to feed the elephant, orders the release of the animal, fearing that it may die of hunger. He has also ordered his solders to find the reason for the strange behaviour of the magnificent animal. His being let loose and passing through a gateway can be seen in the middle of the bottom row, Capture, Freedom and Reunion [Nou 192]. The animal happy gait of the elephant is unmistakable. The animal is preceded by a few soldiers and followed by a horse rider and a few foot-soldiers.

Finally, the Happy Reunion with blind Parents [Kbl 207B] is painted on the right of the bottom row. We see the white elephant sprinkling water over his parents, and announcing his return. Being blind the parents happily caress their son with their trunks.

The importance of the composition lies in the artist displaying what one could call human feelings to animals. We have the white elephant painted four times, each in a different emotional situation. When he is caught and brought to Varanasi, he is moving against his wish. When he refuses food his determination is evident. Next on being let loose, he trots happily to meet his parents. When he meets his parents his joy knows no bounds.
