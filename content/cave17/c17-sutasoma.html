Sutasoma Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th century, AD

Several episodes from the Sutasoma Jaataka are painted on the wall of the back corridor with a great deal of imagination and skill. Next to Simhala Avadaana and Vishvaantara Jaataka this narration is the longest in Ajanta. Unfortunately, a major potion of the painting has perished.

The Jaataka: King Sudaasa of Varanasi went on a hunting expedition. Exhausted, he took rest on a stone. While he was thus sleeping, a lioness attracted by the king came to him and licked his heel. This made her pregnant. Her son Saudaasa became the king after his father's death. One day his cook by mistake served him human flesh. Thereafter the lioness's instinct was quickened and he became a cannibal. He would satisfy his cannibalistic appetite by having his subjects murdered. In course of time the citizens drove him out of the country. While living in the forest, Saudaasa preyed on travellers. One day King Sutasoma of Indra-prastha, a Bodhi-sattva incarnation, had gone to the forest to bathe, where he was attacked by Saudaasa, and was taken as a prisoner. Sutasoma requested permission for a day's freedom so that he could hear a sermon from an ascetic. When Sutasoma kept his promise his captor was impressed. Then Bodhi-sattva preached the Law of Dharma to Saudaasa, who gave up cannibalism and joined the Holy Order.

One of the surviving early scenes of this story is the scene of the Deer Hunt [Mjt 167] by King Sudaasa. It shows the king of Varanasi setting out on a hunt with his retinue, accompanied by two other princes and a pack of dogs. The King is seen pursuing the deer and, having become separated from his retinue, entering a forest abounding in wild animals.

The nimble movement of the deer and the wild stare in its eyes are shown in this Deer Hunt [Mjt 167]. In this unsurpassed masterpiece, the horror of being hunted and killed is marvellously conveyed by the animal's abrupt and swift leap and the almost hypnotised look in its bulging eyes as it glances back at the approaching horse.

The forest scene continues above this panel, and the depicted scenes involve the lioness. We see the Court of Sudaasa [Kbl 195] where the lioness tells the king that she is carrying the king's child.

The Coronation of Saudaasa [Kbl 196] is the next important panel, where we find Saudaasa ascending the throne on the death of his father. The ceremonial bath is realistically portrayed and the excitement is unmistakable.

Another realistic presentation is the royal Kitchen of Saudaasa [Kbl 197T] where his cook inadvertently cooked human meat. The composition provides us information regarding contemporary social habits.

The next scene in which Saudaasa tastes Human Flesh [Kbl 197B] is another important panel. The king is seen sitting on a low stool and eating from a plate kept on another low stool. Two cooks are looking through a window, most likely, of the kitchen. The maids who are serving him are seen looking at the king with concern.

The detail of the battle scene, realistically depicting the confusion of war, in which cavalry and foot soldiers are participating, is shown in King fights against his Army [Kbl 198] when the army rose in revolt against the cannibalistic king.

Army driving Saudaasa to the Forest [Kbl 199] is depicted in the next scene. We see the king on the galloping horse. The movement of the horse and the commotion of the moving foot-solders are the result of close observation and draughtsmanship.
