Lady at her Toilet

Cave 17, 6th century, AD

There are three categories of murals in Ajanta: the first, picture-narratives the subject-matter of which is a jaataka or episodes from the life of the Buddha; the second, decorative designs, mostly found on the ceilings; and, the last, solo-pictures, self-contained with mythological or secular themes. It is possible that we may call some of these pictures secular, simply because we are not able to connect them with any religious theme.

Lady at her Toilet [Kbl 048], a solo-picture has amazingly escaped the ravages of time. It is painted on the upper part of the pilaster between the front and right corridors. A line sketch Toilet Scene (Line ?) is included for better appreciation.

It presents an aristocratic lady at her toilet with maids-in-attendance on both sides and a female dwarf further down. The central lady has an apparel of some gossamer-like fabric, almost transparent. The existence of this almost invisible fabric is deduced from the long folds of the lower garment. The lady is adorned with elaborate jewellery and she holds a mirror in her left hand and a champaka flower in her right. The beads of the pearl necklace have been drawn with thick pigment, as in an oil-painting, so that the gems are raised in relief.

The figure of the chair-bearer to the right, though tarnished and dark now, shows spontaneity, elegance and grace.

The artist has painted the lady with the mirror with an unusually slender waist and with the right foot placed somewhat awkwardly. Indian art prefers to depict feminine beauty not as the artist sees her in real life, but as she reveals her charm in his mental vision, endowed with all the attributes showered on her by the classical poets.

The Shilpa Shaastra enjoins "The beauty of a female figure, be that human or divine, should be enhanced by elongating the eyes, enlarging the breasts and reducing the girth at the waist". Thus, a beautiful woman shall have legs coming down like the trunks of a banana tree, and the palms and feet shall resemble the leaves of trees. This simile pada-pallava (feet-like-leaves) was what the artist attempted, and not the feet made of bones and muscles. Sadrishya (resemblance), one of the six limbs of painting, is to be in the realm of bhaava (emotional appeal) and not in the superficiality of roopa (form). Examples of such similitude are charana-kamala (lotus-like feet) and simha-kati (lion-waisted). See also the line sketch pada-pallava (feet-like-leaves).

In drawing the chawri-bearer, who is to the right of the lady, the artist seems to be swayed by the canonical injunctions of the shaastra-s. "She should have her body weight on one leg only, the other being softly touching the ground; she should have graceful curves, alluring contours and should look askance." The other maid also stands in the abhanga stance to balance the composition. The dwarf is certainly not beautiful; but she harmoniously mingles with the schematic composition and accentuates the charm of the three damsels by contrast.

The most notable feature of the painting is, however, the lovely chiaroscuro, shown in the splashes of light on the rose-coloured ranges of the conventional hills and the dark green shade of the foliage of the mango-tree forming the background.
