Maanushi Buddhas and Mithuna Figures

Cave 17, 5th Century, AD

Above the main entrance leading to the main hall of this cave there is a row of eight figures, the seven Maanushi Buddhas [Nou 161/162] and Maitreya, the Future Buddha. Below this row, on the lintel, are eight panels of Mithuna Figures [Nou 161/162] in amorous attitudes. Sculptured figures of river-goddesses Ganga and Yamuna on a makara (crocodile) flank each side of the doorway.

Maanushi Buddhas: The eight Maanushi Buddhas (Mortal-Buddhas) are Vipashyin, Shikhi, Vishva-vasu, Kraku-chchhanda, Kanaka-muni, Kashyapa, Shaakya-simha (Gautama) and Maitreya. The first three show three different mudra-s, namely, dharma chakra mudra (turning-the-Wheel-of-Religion-attitude), dhyana mudra (meditation-attitude) and abhaya-mudra (assurance-attitude), respectively. The next two figures are deep in meditation. According to the cultic conventions, all the figures have some common features, such as curled locks of hair with an ushnisha (turban, actually, a characteristic mark of hair on the head of the Buddha), long ear-lobes and down-cast half-closed eyes. The sixth Buddha, Kashyapa, is painted with a pale reddish hue and the seventh Buddha, namely Gautama, has the conventional yellow robe on his person. The last in the list, Maitreya, the Future Buddha, is the only one wearing a magnificent crown and other jewellery. This is because, unlike others, he is yet to renounce all worldly possessions.

Mithuna Figures: Under each of the above Buddha figures is a small rectangular slab showing mithuna figures (couples). The varied tones of their skins, display a remarkable sense of colour and a controlled rhythm in the draughtsmanship.

Sportive and jolly, they are sipping wine, caressing or wooing the partner in love, although one cannot find any coarseness in their compositions. In one of the panels a girl offers a cup of wine to her beloved; in another, the male figure, recumbent on a bolster, is caressing his ladylove; in a third the two lovers are almost embracing each other.

One wonders the propriety of selecting eight amorous couples under the Buddha-figures in a vihaara, a chapel-cum-dormitory for celibate monks. Admittedly, the figures are realistic, and the composition, drawing and colouring are excellent; yet, with our contemporary sensitivity, we cannot but feel a little ill-at-ease at the awkward juxtaposition of the incompatible ideas conveyed by the two parallel rows. Perhaps, the possible explanation is that the artist-monks had no inhibition in portraying the pleasures of life.

Kinship between Sculpture and Painting: Two exquisite sculptured figures of riverine deities, Ganga and Yamuna, lend support and add welcome grace to the doorway. The kinship we find between sculpture and painting, as that between painting and dance, in India is something altogether unique in the history of world-art. Most impressive is the way the two art forms co-exist at Ajanta, complementing each other. Artistic ingenuity has reinforced the relationship.

This kinship is apparent in this complex. Each figure is in concord with the other, sculpture blends with the murals, colours with the colour scheme, flower-studded door-panels with the glowing wallscape and the decoratively painted ceiling. A yearning for mutual exchange and unity that we find here permeates the walls of Ajanta, resounding with the story of this unity bonded in faith.

It may be mentioned here that in Ajanta and elsewhere even the sculptures were fully painted, though most of the paint has disappeared. The blending achieved must have been astonishing when the stone carvings were bedecked in colour, falling in line with the hue and colour scheme of the murals. One can only imagine the riot of colours of the original scheme.
