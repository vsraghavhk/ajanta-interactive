Mahaa-Kapi Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th century AD

The Buddhist faith preaches compassion towards all creatures. The Bodhi-sattva incarnated as animals many times to propagate this virtue. Once He was born as a monkey and the story is known as Mahaa-kapi Jaataka. There are two episodes narrated in this cave. In one of them He helped His herd escape from the attack of the king by forming Himself into a bridge across the river. In the second, He helps an ungrateful Brahmin who attempted to kill Him.

The first episode is painted on the inside wall of the front verandah, on the left when you enter through main entrance.

The Jaataka: Once the Bodhi-sattva was as born as the king of monkeys, and lived in a forest far away from the city. In the forest there was a very huge mango tree, which produced extremely delicious and extra-ordinarily large fruits. One day one of the fruits fell into the river and was picked up by a fisherman. Never having seen such a fruit he took it to his king. The king after enjoying the fruit, ordered his army to trace the tree which produced such delicious fruit. When the tree was located the king found to his dismay that a large herd of monkeys were destroying the mangoes. He, then ordered his army to shoot the monkeys down. The king-monkey apprehending danger to His subjects caught hold of a branch of the tree to convert his body into a bridge and ordered the monkeys to escape. The last monkeys, who were envious of the king-monkey, intentionally kicked the most benevolent monkey while jumping off his back. The tired monkey-king fell into the river below wounded. The astonished king , touched by what he had seen, rescued Him. But in spite of his nursing, the monkey-king died, but without any hatred towards the monkey who kicked Him.

In this badly damaged mural, the artist has taken some liberty in the narration. Instead of a mango tree he has drawn a banyan tree. This is evident from the shape of the leaves and from the presence of aerial roots. He has also added a touch of humour which will be mentioned in the discussion that follows. The mural is almost obliterated and hence the discussion is supported by a line diagramme Mahaa-kapi saves his Herd (Line ?).

The narration of this jaataka is another illustration that the scenes are not painted in the chronological sequence. In most of the paintings we find that scenes are grouped together on the basis of location. To follow the sequence would mean abrupt changes in the settings, which, the artist might have thought, would disturb the composition.

The story starts at the top left with a monkey sitting comfortably on a branch and enjoying a mango fruit. For the next scene is half way down the mural where the king riding a horse is marching with his army towards the forest. The king may be identified by the royal umbrella over him. The commotion of the marching of the army is evident, with the solders in great animation, carrying swords and shields. In the next scene, which is shown a little higher up, three archers are shooting at the monkeys on the tree. On the top of the mural is painted the monkey-king stretching his body to act as a bridge and monkeys passing over his back. Next we see the fallen monkey-king being rescued by two solders on a blanket. The scene in which the Bodhi-sattva is preaching the Law to the king is not included in the sketch.

Let us see the secular part of the composition, for which the Ajanta artist has always had a weakness. A low wall separates the river from the forest. The river is shown abounding in fishes and water birds. In the lower portion there are a number of bathers, males and females. The Ajanta artist who never misses an opportunity to add a touch of humour, has portrayed a short man, probably a court-jester, eves-dropping on the bathing beauties!

Another episode from Mahaa-kapi Jaataka is painted on the left-hand wall of the back corridor and the Monkey helps a Man [Kbl 192] and Man throws Stone on the Monkey [Kbl 193] are two of the important scenes.

Let me narrate this episode in brief. Once a man, while collecting some fruits in a forest, fell into a pit from which he could not come out. The Bodhi-sattva, born as a monkey, saw his plight and with great effort carried him on his back to safety. When the tired monkey was taking rest, the ungrateful man picked up a big rock and threw it at the monkey so that he could eat it. The monkey was badly injured and admonished the man for his ungrateful act. Then the compassionate monkey led him out of the forest.

In the first scene Monkey helps a Man [Kbl 192], we see on the top right the fallen man shouting for help. On the left is the compassionate monkey responding to his call. The forest scene is composed excellently, the trees and two deer are very natural.

In the scene Man throws Stone on the Monkey [Kbl 193], at the centre the exhausted monkey is asleep and the ungrateful man is about to throw a huge stone. The effort of lifting a huge stone is unmistakable. On the left we see the monkey chiding him.
