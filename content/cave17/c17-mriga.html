Mriga Jaataka

Cave 17, 5th century, AD

An episode from Mriga Jaataka covers the front wall to the left of the window. (Mriga = deer)

The Jaataka: Once the Bodhi-sattva was born as a Golden Deer with a sweet voice. One day the deer found a man drowning. It swam across and saved him. When the man thanked it, the deer requested him to keep its existence secret. On reaching his place, Varanasi, he heard that the King of Varanasi had offered a reward for anyone who could tell him about the Golden Deer. The greedy person guided the king to the abode of the deer. Before the king could shoot it, the deer spoke, and the king was enchanted by its sweet voice. On hearing that the man whom it had saved has treacherously betrayed the Golden Deer, the king decided to punish the man with death. The deer intervened and asked for mercy. The king, then, took the Golden Deer to Varanasi and requested it to give a sermon to his wife.

The story starts on this panel after the greedy man returns to Varanasi. The complete mural is reproduced as a sketch in Story of a golden Deer (Line ?) and some of its scenes are reproduced in colour.

On the top, in the sketch, we can see the man giving out information about the Golden Deer to the king. The king is sitting cross-legged on an erected platform. The drummer on the right suggests that the proclamation of the reward has just been made. There is a group of people excitedly discussing the announcement.

For the next scene we need to go to the bottom of the panel. We see the King arrives in the Forest [Kbl 229] with his retinue, the abode of the Golden Deer. The normal commotion associated with such an expedition is portrayed very effectively. The artist has succeeded in capturing the majestic trot of the royal horse.

To the right we see a Hunter attempts to capture the Deer [Kbl 230]. The hunter is shown of reddish complexion, obviously to highlight him from the others in the crowd. He, while attempting to capture the deer, has his hands chopped off. The chopped hands can be seen just in front of the deer.

Here the Ajanta artist has added this dramatic event not found in the jaataka story. To signify the forest location, the artist has painted a lion and a lot of vegetation in the lower portion.

The king, seeing the miracle, comes to know the truth regarding the deer that it is none other than the Bodhi-sattva Himself. In the middle scene we see The King returns on a Horseback [Kbl 232] along with the Golden Deer carried on a Chariot [Kbl 231] with great honour. The humility in the visage is unmistakable. Even the horse has lowered its head in reverence. Since it is a ceremonial procession the umbrella is in attendance. In the previous scene the umbrella was missing, perhaps because while hunting the umbrella-bearer cannot keep pace with the king riding a horse. The deer in the chariot is majestic and composed, as one would be who is about to who would preach the Law of Dharma to the queen on reaching the palace.

Mention may be made here of a common practice in Ajanta composition. Very often episodes of a story are grouped according to the place of their occurrence and not according to their sequence of occurrence as can be seen in this narration. For example, on the top row are grouped together those scenes which took place in the palace, and the forest scenes depicted in the bottom row. We can see similar groupings even in the pre-Christian era paintings like the Shad-danta Jaataka of Cave 10.
