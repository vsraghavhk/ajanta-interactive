Miracle at Raja-griha

Cave 17, 5th Century, AD

On to the right of the main-doorway is painted this story of a miracle performed by the Buddha, called the Miracle at Raja-griha or the Subjugation of Nalagiri.

The Story: Deva-datta was a cousin of the Buddha. He was envious of the Great Being and attempted to murder him. He failed miserably every time. Subjugation of Nalagiri is one such episode. It may be recollected that King Bimbi-saara was a faithfully follower of the Buddha. But his son, Ajaata-shatru, who came to power after dethroning his father took steps to remove Buddhist influence in his country. Deva-datta conspired with King Ajaata-shatru of Raja-griha, and released an intoxicated elephant, called Nalagiri, on the streets of Raja-griha just when the Master was passing through. The people ran helter-skelter on seeing the charging elephant. But the Most Compassionate One was unperturbed. When Nalagiri came close he gently stroked him, to which the mad elephant responded by kneeling down in obeisance. Deva-datta's scheme was defeated by the love shown by the Buddha.

In the first act, we find the king and Deva-datta hatches a Conspiracy [Kbl 171]. The location is a two-storied palace building. On the right side of the top floor we have King Ajaata-shatru in royal dress and a crown. To his left is Deva-datta who is in the garb of a Brahmin priest. The left part of the king is damaged and so is the major portion of the right part of Deva-datta. The king is wearing jewellery studded with pearls, diamonds and other precious stones. From the gestures of these characters we may reconstruct the plot. Deva-datta has raised two fingers. He has argued his case.

Behind the king we find four women, three directly behind the king and one below the other maids. If we understand the syntax of Ajanta properly, the women are all palace-maids, and the queen is not among them. The maid in the middle is somewhat short and is wearing a long sleeved jacket. She is looking at the maid to her right, who, from her looks is shocked at the conspiracy taking shape in the balcony.

There are five maids seen on the lower floor. They are animatedly discussing the shocking rumour they have heard.

The noteworthy feature is the hairstyles of these maids. There is no repetition. Particularly interesting is the hair-do of the woman who is seen second from the right on the lower floor. This bears a close resemblance to the head-dress of present-day Mohini Aattam dancers of Kerala. The architectural details of this scene are worthy of study. Also interesting are the sthaana-s (body-postures) of the women and their mudra-s (hand-gestures).

Separated by a conventional arch, the next act begins. We see a bhikshu hurrying, probably taking the information to the Buddha. Unfortunately, this part of the mural is badly damaged. In the next Act, which is better preserved the charging of the rogue elephant and its subjugation, are depicted.

A line diagramme Subjugation of Nalagiri [Line 07] and a colour reproductions mad Elephant Nalagiri [Kbl 172] and Subjugation of the Elephant [Kbl 173] support the following description of the scene.

The incident takes place in the market place with rows of shops on the ground floor and residential apartments on the upper floor. The former has awnings of canvas or bamboo-mat in the form of lean-to verandahs to protect the shops against rain and sun. It may be pointed out that a similar market-scene can be found painted in Vishvaantara Jaataka in the same cave.

The awnings of the first shop from the left are found to have been dislodged by mad Elephant Nalagiri [Kbl 172], which dominates the composition. There are terror-stricken people here and there running away in fright. The shopkeepers pull down the door-shutters and the ladies in the balconies look horrified. Near the hind part of the elephant a mounted guard is seen with his right hand raised to warn the crowd. The gigantic monster has a sinister look and a man is seen dangling between the tusks.

In the next scene Subjugation of the Elephant [Kbl 173], to the right we find the elephant kneeling before the Buddha cringing in obeisance. The Master, standing on a lotus, with his disciple, Shari-putta, on his right, is seen caressing the subjugated giant, stroking him with affection. Shopkeepers and pedestrians are all impressed at the miracle. Some have folded their hands to show respect. The smartly dressed ladies on the upper storey, who were torn a few minutes ago in horror and anguish, are now relieved of the tension and are seen strewing flowers and garlands on the Master.

This particular scene has been a subject of criticism by Yazdani. He observed: "In drawing the figures of the ladies in the balconies, the artist has exhibited both imagination and artistic skill, but the composition of the entire subject would appeal more to a lover of the legend than to the art; for the two large representations of the elephant have covered almost the entire space, and the figures of the Buddha and his chief disciple show little spontaneity or freedom of expression qualities which are essential to art of a higher class."

A lover of art for art sake may not agree with the artist who is driven purely by religious fervour.
