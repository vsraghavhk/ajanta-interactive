King Shibi donates his Eyes

Shibi Jaataka

Cave 17, Late 5th century AD

The story of the Bodhi-sattva born as King Shibi who was hailed for his generosity is painted in this cave. We have seen in the Shibi Jaataka of Cave 1, the king cut off a part of his own flesh to a hawk to save a pigeon. In this episode, from the Shibi Jaataka of Cave 17, the king gives away his eyes to a blind Brahmin. The episode is painted on the pilaster between the front and the right corridors and continued on the on the wall of the right corridor.

The Jaataka: King Shibi, ruler of Arittha-pura, was famous all over the world for his observing the Buddhist virtues steadfastly. He distributed alms generously. He built six monasteries, one at each of the city’s gates, from where alms were distributed every day. Lord Indra wanted to test his generosity. He came to the king in the guise of a blind Brahmin and sought the king’s eyes as alms. Much to the protest of his subjects, the king had his eyes gouged out and presented them to the Brahmin gladly. The story of the generous King Shibi is popular in Hindu and Jaina lore.

The painting is greatly damaged. The earlier visitors have written about four or five scenes, but only a part is now extant. In one of them we can see the king after he has given away his eyes. This is shown as a line sketch King Shibi in Pain (Line ?) and two details of this scene Wailing Woman -1 [Kbl 227B/L] and Wailing Woman - 2 [Kbl 227T/L]. Also reproduced is a view of a Monastery [Kbl 227R].

Monastery [Kbl 227R] is a view of one of the six halls of alms built by the king to distribute alms to the needy. On the right a person is bringing some stock of grain for distribution. At the centre a royal attendant is pouring grain onto a piece of cloth held by a woman, who is happy to receive it. The one behind her is waiting for his turn. On the left there is a long queue of persons waiting and watching. In the background is a lady in monastic dress holding a tray in her left hand. The whole scene is fully animated. The scene depicts contemporary wooden architecture, and is shown realistically in normal perspective. We have seen that the Ajanta artist has chosen reverse perspective in the Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1 and in the Vidhura-pandita Jaataka in cave 2.

The scene King Shibi in Pain (Line ?), is subsequent to his giving away his eyes to Indra who came in the guise of a blind Brahmin. King Shibi may be seen sitting on a low throne. From his visage it is unmistakable that he is in great pain. He is supporting his body with his right-hand and is covering his left eye with the other hand. Tears and blood can be seen dripping from the exposed right eye. His sitting posture and his diadem proclaim his royalty. Detail of two women in sorrow are Wailing Woman -1 [Kbl 227B/L] and Wailing Woman - 2 [Kbl 227T/L]. The former is seen beating her chest and pulling her hair, and the pain in the other woman is depicted in her holding her head in her hand. The sorrowful mood of the maidens as well of the of the people around pervades the whole atmosphere. The grief of the women portrayed in this scene is similar to what we observed in the women shown in Shibi Jaataka of Cave 1.
