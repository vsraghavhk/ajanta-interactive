19. Portrayal of Women

Without exaggeration it can be said that Ajanta's creative genius lies in the portrayal of women. The lovely ladies of the courts with their handmaids, the dancers and the musicians, the devotees, the common women and even the beggar girls are all drawn with brilliant zest and extraordinary knowledge. In fact, one wonders at the propriety of painting such sensuous and voluptuous women in religious places inhabited mainly by celibate monks who have renounced all worldly pleasures.

Whether they are painted in repose, talking to their lovers, instructing their handmaids, admiring themselves in mirrors, carrying offerings or simply standing, sitting and gossiping, the Ajanta women are always painted with a sort of wonder akin to awe.

Undoubtedly the Ajanta artist had succeeded in reproducing the soft roundness of her breasts, the curves of her hips, the turn of her head, the contortions of her body, the gestures of her hands, or the slanting glance of her eyes. Every one of the Ajanta women is invariably refreshing. An interesting feature of Ajanta women is that they never lose their dignity and nowhere are they besmirched or belittled.

Removed in time, we are not able to understand the Ajanta artist fully. This is perhaps because we apply today's scale of morality to judge the past. We have wondered why very sensuous women were painted at all in these religious caves. To add to our bewilderment, we find that the normal practice in Ajanta depict the heroines in the scenes naked, or in near nudity, while all the maids in the same scenes are fully clothed. Queen captivats Mahaa-janaka [Kbl 090L], a scene from Mahaa-janaka Jaataka in Cave 1 and dying Princess [ASI 003], a scene from the episode of the Conversion of Nanda of cave 16 can be cited as two such examples. Further, we may have no explanation for the fact that most of the heroines of Ajanta are dark complexioned. Apart from the black Apsaras [Kbl 169] of Cave 17 and the dying Princess [ASI 003] of Cave 16, there are other dark-complexioned beauties in Ajanta.

Just as the Buddhas and Bodhi-sattva-s are the central themes in painting the sacred images, in the orbit of secular subjects the women are undoubtedly the focal point.

20. Painting on the Ceilings

For reasons unknown, the Ajanta artist never painted religious themes on the ceilings, where the colour scheme, delineation and repetitious themes were rendered with equal craftsmanship. Spontaneity of movement is evident in all these.

From the artistic point of view, the paintings on the ceilings show power of imagination, perhaps even excelling the wall-murals. In these paintings, the Ajanta artist might have attempted to offset the seriousness of the themes on the walls.

These drawings have taken the texture of a carpet, brilliantly woven, immediately captivating the eyes and filling the senses. They do not throw a challenge to the intellect but cover the void above the walls with idyllic coloured designs.

The ceilings are filled with geometric designs, flower decorations, animals and birds and humorous scenes.

21. Painting on the Ceilings - Geometric Designs

One of the themes is the huge concentric circle enclosed in a square, with a number of flowery bands within it; they are usually to be found in the centre of the ceiling, main hall, antechamber and inner shrines. The ceiling of Cave 2 is the best such example, of which Squares and Circles - 3 [ASI 048] is a good example.

Another equally cherished theme consists of a number of rectangular panels filled with decorative motifs framed by smaller squares or rectangles with representations of fruit and floral forms. Some of these motifs are repeated a number of times so that the effect is that of an enormous printed textile spread over the sanctuary. This effect may be seen in Cave 17 and a part of this is shown in Squares and Rectangles - 2 [ASI 083].

These decorative panels present as typical a collection of Indian patterns of ornamental design as one could find. The floral designs that fill these squares are perfect examples of the Indian ability to reduce natural forms to a moving decorative pattern.

These pliant vine patterns, filled with rhythmic adaptations of flower and bird and human forms, are the painted equivalents of those wonderful fantasies in relief sculpture that are to be seen both in the ornamentation of the Ajanta caves and in such a Gupta monument as the Dhamekh stoopa at Sarnath. Some of the paintings on the ceiling of Caves 1, 2 and 17 illustrate this.

22. Painting on the Ceilings - Animal Motifs

The artist has been able to draw animals spaced between flowers and plants with extraordinary dexterity. One of the most forceful and vigorous drawings of Ajanta depicts the fighting Bulls [Mjt 013] painted on a pillar of Cave 1. The complex of decoration on the ceiling of Cave 1 includes a wonderful panel of an Elephant [ASI 028] enclosed in an entanglement of lotus flowers. Although the elephant's shape has been distorted to fit the confines of the frame, the artist of this wonderfully alive and cursive drawing still suggests the massive anatomy and ponderous strength of the elephant.

23. Painting on the Ceilings - Scenes in a lighter Vein

As in other instances, a scene depicted on the ceiling of Cave 1 in which a Clown [ASI 008] is cradled amongst flowers, as if seated on a swing, shows the expertness of the artist in mingling a portrayal of what is seemingly naturalistic together with totally imaginary elements.

Panels as on the ceiling of Caves 1 and 17, with their grotesque and weird creatures, exhibit a remarkable charm.

Most intriguing and least expected in a religious shrine are the scenes depicting a royal harem like the panel Royal Harem [Kbl 108] and people enjoying each other's company with wine as depicted in Foreigners Drinking [ASI 043]!

Many of the motifs and ornaments that we find on the ceilings of Ajanta are still alive. They have been absorbed into the different branches of Indian artistic expression and are esteemed even today: in the alpana-s, jewellery, brocades and borders of conventional sari-s, dresses, etc.
