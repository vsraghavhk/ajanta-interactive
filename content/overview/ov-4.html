4. Political Situation and Patronage

The beginning of the Ajanta chapter is a tribute to the religious tolerance of Indian rulers.

The earliest caves in Ajanta (Caves 9 & 10) were excavated during the rule of the Saatavaahana-s, who had their capital at Pratishthana (called, Paithan, to day). During their rule there was brisk trade and commerce within the land and with the Mediterranean world, which brought in enormous riches.

The second phase was of greater artistic activity at Ajanta during the rule of the Vaakaataka and Chalukya dynasties from the 4th to the 7th centuries AD.

The rulers of the Vaakaataka and Chalukya dynasties, like the Saata-vaahana-s, were themselves Hindus but all these kings actively allowed Buddhism to flourish in their territory.

However it must be conceded that there was no direct royal patronage during almost the entire period of Ajanta's artistic activity. But the rich mercantile community, organising itself into guilds, had provided the requisite patronage.

Prof. Walter Spink of the University of Michigan at Ann Arbour, has devoted his whole life to unravelling the secrets of Ajanta. According to him Ajanta represents the apogee of the classical age and gives valuable insights into the material life, aesthetics and spiritual values of the period. Using contemporary detection techniques, he claims that he can date each painting and each sculpture on a year-to-year basis. According to his research, the second - that is the maharani - phase of Ajanta building activities began in 462 AD and was completed in just 18 years!
